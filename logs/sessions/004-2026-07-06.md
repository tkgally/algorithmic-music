## What was done

Verified and matured all six psychology pages → `reviewed`. Method: six parallel verification subagents (one per page), each instructed to fetch the cited sources, check specific numbers/quotes, and flag dead or fabricated links; I read every report, applied the fixes, and promoted each page. The pages were already well-researched (session 001) — the pass surfaced mostly **citation-attribution** problems (a claim cited to a source that doesn't actually contain it), plus a small number of numbers worth softening. No page needed a substantive rewrite of its central claims.

- **auditory-perception-basics** (the page with the most real problems): the Dowling (1978) "Scale and contour" citation URL (`arxiv.org/pdf/2604.13119`) was **fabricated** — it resolves to a real but unrelated 2026 paper on melodic-contour clustering by different authors, 48 years off. Replaced with the correct DOI (`10.1037/0033-295X.85.4.341`). Also fixed three more mismatched citations: Plomp & Levelt 1965 (roughness at ~25% critical bandwidth) was pointing at an unrelated arXiv hearing-physiology review that never mentions roughness or dissonance — replaced with the original 1965 paper (MPI-hosted PDF); the ~2 ms gap-detection figure was attributed to "Kelly et al." when the actual paper is Gold, Nodal, Peters, King & Bajo (2015) — Kelly et al. is a different study cited *within* it; echoic-memory duration was attributed to "Otsuka & Kashino" — the actual authors of PMC4149571 are Nishihara et al. (2014). Softened the loudness-integration frequency-dependence sub-claim (the cited cochlear-implant paper doesn't establish it) and relabeled the "2025 replication" of Huron's voice-denumerability study as a "follow-up study" (it extends the paradigm with a novel manipulation rather than strictly replicating it).
- **repetition-and-familiarity**: the Schellenberg focused-vs-incidental exposure finding (inverted-U vs. linear liking) was cited only to Green & Bavelier (2012), which doesn't contain it (that paper shows a monotonic mere-exposure effect, not the inverted-U/mode split). Added the real source, Schellenberg, Peretz & Vieillard (2008). Added a missing citation (Nettl 1983) for the "repetition is a near-universal" claim, and clarified that the ">90% of music is repeats" estimate is Huron's, cited approvingly by Margulis, not Margulis's own finding.
- **pleasure-and-reward**: the structural chill-trigger list was attributed in the Sources line to Mori & Iwanaga (2017), which is actually about the psychophysiology distinguishing chills from tears, not a list of structural triggers. Re-cited to Guhn, Hamm & Zentner (2007) and Grewe, Nagel, Kopiez & Altenmüller (2007), the papers that actually establish the acoustic/structural correlates. Softened "~5% get little musical reward" to the literature's actual range (3–10%, depending on BMRQ cutoff) — the 2014 Mas-Herrero paper itself doesn't state a single prevalence figure.
- **complexity-and-preference**: Berlyne's collative-variables list and the "two overlapping reward/aversion systems" model were both cited to a 2016 review (fnhum.2016.00536) that doesn't actually contain either — that paper only says the inverted-U comes from "interacting arousal systems," no more. Re-attributed both to Chmiel & Schubert (2017), which does quote Berlyne (1969) directly on the reward/aversion mechanism and cites his fuller collative-variables list. Added a caveat that the Güçlütürk et al. "artifact of averaging" study (the page's strongest anti-inverted-U evidence) used grayscale images, not music — informative but imported from visual aesthetics.
- **musical-expectation**: fixed an author-list error ("Rohrmeier & Cross et al." — it's a two-author paper). The Narmour A+A→A / A+B→C schema notation was cited only to Rohrmeier & Cross, which doesn't use that notation; added a direct citation to Narmour's 1990 monograph. Softened an overstated attribution — Hansen & Pearce (2014) directly demonstrates only the IC-vs-unexpectedness-rating correlation (r≈0.7); the RT/pupil/MMN correlations the page also claims are background citations *within* that paper to other studies, not its own findings. The predictive-coding precision-weighting citation (PMC6385564) was mislabeled as "Vuust/Koelsch tradition" — it's neither authored nor cited by either, and uses non-musical tone sequences; kept it as general supporting evidence for the mechanism and added the real music-specific source, Koelsch, Vuust & Friston (2019).
- **emotion-and-meaning**: no real errors — the best-sourced page of the six. Added the primary 2009 Fritz et al. citation (the page previously cited only a 2013 paper that discusses it secondhand) and a hedge noting the register/timbre cells for anger and fear in the emotion-cue table are less settled in the literature than the rest of the table.

All fixes verified against fetched primary sources or their abstracts/DOI records by the verification subagents before I applied them; nothing was applied on a subagent's unverified say-so beyond what its report demonstrated.

## Decisions made (and why)

- **Same delegation pattern as session 003**: one verification subagent per page, fetching cited sources and reporting CONFIRMED/WRONG/UNVERIFIABLE per claim; I owned every edit, the cross-links, and the index. This kept editing judgment central while parallelizing the (slow) web-fetching work — the six subagents together made ~200 tool calls and took up to ~5 minutes each.
- **Distinguish citation errors from claim errors.** Every fix this session was "the citation doesn't support what it's cited for," not "the claim itself is false" — the underlying facts (Dowling's contour/interval memory finding, the Schellenberg exposure-mode effect, Berlyne's two-system model, etc.) all held up once traced to a source that actually contains them. Recorded this distinction explicitly in `wiki/log.md` since it matters for how much confidence a future session should place in the surviving (unflagged) citations on other draft pages.
- **Didn't over-fix contested-but-not-wrong content.** The emotion-cue table's anger/fear cells and the loudness-integration frequency figures are genuinely unsettled in the literature, not errors; added hedges rather than forcing false precision.
