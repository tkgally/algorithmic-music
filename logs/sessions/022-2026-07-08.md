# Session 022 — 2026-07-08

**Model (as reported):** Claude (Opus 4.8)
**Scope:** Find and fix the *real* cause of the "static in the middle of sustained notes" (Engine 01, and by extension the shared synth), using an exact reproduction the listener provided. This is the follow-up to session 021, whose reverb-grain + latencyHint fixes helped but did not eliminate the static.

## The clue that cracked it

After session 021 shipped v0.3, Tom re-listened and reported the static was **still there**, then gave the decisive repro:
- **Location:** "the extended note that appears at the end of the first and second melodic phrases."
- **Cross-browser:** the same static, in the same place, in **Chrome, Safari, and Firefox**.
- **Exact URL:** `…/01-tonal-classical/index.html#v=0.3.0&seed=d8d8d8&bpm=110&mode=major&tonic=C4&rev=0.24&vol=0.6`.

Cross-browser + same-place = **deterministic and in the signal** — not a real-time glitch, not a Safari quirk. That meant my offline (Chromium) render *contained* it, and I just had to point the analysis at the right note.

## Diagnosis (offline, headless Chromium + OfflineAudioContext)

1. Rendered `seed=d8d8d8`; the extended phrase-final notes are **beat 14 (E5, ~0.99 s)** and **beat 20 (D5, ~1.07 s, the HC cadence)** — exactly the notes long enough (> 0.7 s) to matter.
2. **Melody-only** A/B (chord/bass muted, vibrato and reverb toggled independently): the reverb only adds a dark tail; the vibrato is spectrally inert (its spectrogram "beading" is the ordinary signature of *frequency* vibrato, not AM). Neither is the static.
3. **A high-resolution zoom of the *dry* keys note** (vibrato off, reverb off) revealed a **broadband "puff" centered ~0.55 s into the note** — a spindle filling the space between harmonics up to ~2 kHz. Time-domain: a **2.3× amplitude bump** there.
4. That time coincides exactly with the **envelope release onset** (`relStart` = end − release ≈ 0.57 s into a 1.07 s note). Root cause: `envGain` in the shared `synth.js` held the gain **perfectly FLAT** at the peak through the sustain, then switched **abruptly to a fast exponential release** — a discontinuity in the envelope's *slope*, which splatters a broadband energy burst. On a long note the release starts near the **middle**, so the burst lands mid-note. This is invisible to aggregate metrics, is not the reverb, and only appears on notes long enough to have a mid-note release — which is why sessions 019/021 (reverb hypotheses) circled it.

## Fix (`experiments/lib/synth.js` `envGain`)

Never hold flat. `envGain` now takes a `sustain` (fraction of peak) argument:
- **Struck** voices (FM `keys` melody, lo-fi `rhodes`; `sustain ≤ 0.35`): **one continuous exponential decay** from the attack peak to a low floor — no sustain shelf, no mid-note corner; the only slope change is the final ramp-to-zero at the note's quiet end (the natural shape of a real EP).
- **Sustained** voices (`pad`/`strings`/`drone`/bass; higher `sustain`): a **gentle glide** to the sustain level then a release that continues the decay — decay-into-decay, a small corner, and their long releases make any residual negligible.
- Implemented with **only exponential ramps** (each has a defined endpoint), never a ramp after `setTargetAtTime` — that transition's start value is implementation-defined and differs between Chrome/Firefox/Safari; the output must be identical in every browser (the listener uses Safari).

Verified: a spectrogram of the same D5 note shows the **puff gone** (clean decaying harmonics); the amplitude bump is eliminated. Per-voice `sustain`: keys 0.22, rhodes 0.24, bass 0.4, strings 0.86, pad 0.92, drone 0.92.

Versions bumped: **tonal-classical 0.3.1, groove-lofi 0.2.1, ambient-drift 0.1.1** (all share the fixed `synth.js`).

## Notes / trade-offs

- The melody is now a **struck EP that decays and rings into the reverb** rather than a flat-held tone. This is more natural for the FM-EP timbre and is the price of a corner-free envelope that is also click-safe at the note end (the decay must reach a low level before the ramp-to-zero, or that final ramp would itself click on an exposed note). If Tom finds it too "plucky," the struck floor is one number to raise, but that trades against end-of-note click-safety.
- Session 021's reverb-grain smoothing and the melodic-contour variety are unaffected and still valuable; this is the envelope cause they were adjacent to.
