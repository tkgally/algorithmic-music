## Findings and fixes

**1. Octave equivalence (psychology cluster).** `auditory-perception-basics.md` stated octave equivalence is "near-universal across cultures... underlies all scale systems," citing `musical-universals.md` as supporting evidence. But `musical-universals.md` has a section titled "Octave equivalence, contested," reporting Jacoby et al. (2019): US singers reproduced melodies transposed by octaves when pitched outside their vocal range, while Tsimane' singers showed no such bias — the whole point of that section is that octave equivalence is *not* settled as universal. One page was citing the other as backing a claim the other page's content actually undercuts. Fixed: softened the auditory-perception-basics.md claim to "common... but not settled," described the Jacoby finding accurately, and pointed to musical-universals.md's fuller treatment instead of citing it as blanket support.

**2. Swing-ratio-by-tempo, a three-way mismatch plus a self-contradiction.** `rhythm-and-meter.md`, `expressive-performance.md`, and `jazz-and-improvisation.md` each state a swing-ratio-vs-tempo curve, all citing Friberg & Sundström (2002). They didn't agree: at ~200 BPM, one page implied ~1.5:1, another (interpolating its own table) ~1.65–1.75:1, a third gave yet another curve. Worse, `jazz-and-improvisation.md` contradicted *itself* — its Findings section said "~3.5:1 at slow tempos... to ~1:1 at ~300 BPM" while its Implications bullet said "linear from ~3:1 at 60–100 BPM... to 1:1 at 300 BPM."

I tried to resolve this by finding the paper's actual tempo/ratio table rather than picking one of the three existing (mutually contradictory) tables arbitrarily. The Music Perception 2002 paper is paywalled; I fetched the two open sources the wiki already cited — an ASA 1999 press summary (`acoustics.org/pressroom`) and a KTH lab page (`speech.kth.se/music/performance/Texts/ensemble_swing.htm`) — neither reproduces a precise tempo/ratio table, only the qualitative shape (ratio as high as "almost 3.5" at the slowest tempos tested, falling to 1:1 at fast tempos, short note ~100 ms constant across medium-to-fast tempi) plus one new, independently confirmed detail from the KTH page: at medium tempi, drummers' ratios run consistently *above* 2:1 and soloists' consistently *below* it.

Given I couldn't verify a precise multi-point curve, inventing one (as each of the three pages had separately done) would just be adding a fourth guess. Instead: designated `jazz-and-improvisation.md` (closest to the primary source, and where the measurement itself is described) as the single canonical statement, fixed its internal contradiction, added the drummer/soloist nuance with its KTH citation, and had `rhythm-and-meter.md` and `expressive-performance.md` state only the two verified anchors (~3.5:1 slow, ~1:1 by ~300 BPM, ~100 ms constant short note) plus a cross-reference, instead of each restating its own diverging interpolation table.

**3. Click-avoidance minimum ramp (algorithms/implementation cluster).** `web-audio-fundamentals.md`'s click-avoidance house rule states "gain moves get ≥3–10 ms ramps." `synthesis-recipes.md` claims every recipe "obeys web-audio-fundamentals' rules," then states attacks "≥2 ms," and its kick recipe specifically uses "amp attack 1–3 ms" — below even its own stated 2 ms floor, and well below the fundamentals page's 3–10 ms floor. Rather than force percussion attacks slower (which would blunt transients unrealistically) or drop the general floor (which is reasonable for continuous fades/automation), I added an explicit exception to `web-audio-fundamentals.md`: a percussive attack's initial ramp from 0 is the one case where sub-3ms is accepted, since any nonzero ramp already removes the step-discontinuity that causes clicks — the ≥3–10 ms floor is specifically for continuous gain moves (fades, crossfades, live automation), not note onsets.

**4. Voice-count cap (theory & craft cluster, minor).** `counterpoint-and-voice-leading.md` gives "≤3 concurrent independent lines" (Huron's perceptual denumerability limit). `composition-craft.md` gives "≤3–4 real parts" (a Rimsky-Korsakov orchestration-checklist number). These aren't actually in conflict — one bounds *perceptually independent streams*, the other bounds *general textural part-count* including doubled/homorhythmic parts — but stated bare, a future engine reader could pick either number for the same design decision. Added a one-line cross-reference in composition-craft.md distinguishing the two questions rather than changing either number.

**Checked and left alone (no real conflict):** jazz's "altered dominants pull toward resolution" vs. Impressionism's "extended chords deliberately don't resolve" — genuinely different, correctly-stated genre conventions, flagged by the subagent as worth a shared-module design note someday but not an error. Several duplicated passages (Satie's furniture-music anecdote in two genre pages, Reich's inaudible-process critique in three pages, Gold et al. 2019's inverted-U figures in three psychology pages, CHORAL's rule count in three algorithms pages, Xenakis's stochastic works in two pages) — all consistent with each other, appropriately cross-linked per `conventions.md`'s "prefer linking over repeating" rule; not action items.
