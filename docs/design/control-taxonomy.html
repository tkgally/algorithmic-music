1 · Purpose & scope

The site will compose and perform instrumental music across many styles — familiar ones documented in the wiki and original ones invented on the fly — behind an interface with three levels of control (comprehensive-site vision). This document fixes what those controls are.

It decides: the Start-level genre shortlist; the concrete control at each tier (name, type, range, default, and a one-line mapping into the composition/synthesis pipeline); how the tiers, the seed, and the URL relate; and how the user changes settings live while a piece plays.

It defers (to later design docs, so this one stays reviewable): the style-pack / style-vector schema (the internal data every control writes into — next doc); the compact URL encoding (music-representation-and-notation.md); the original-style sampling & coherence rules (style-invention-and-style-space.md); and the build (Phase 3c) — though §8 flags one architectural commitment the build must honor.

Basis: control-surfaces-and-user-parameters.md (precedent + HCI evidence), style-invention-and-style-space.md (the axis space), hybridization-and-fusion.md (how the two-genre melds work), plus the five preliminary engines as proven-control precedent.

2 · Design principles

Seven rules, drawn from the HCI and generative-music evidence, that every control obeys. They are why the tiers can share one engine.

  1. Auto = the seed decides. Every control defaults to auto. An untouched control is not “off” — it means let the seed choose a value within the selected genre. Setting a control pins it (removing it from what the seed samples). This is the project's own precedent (the percussion engine's per-axis auto), and it makes Start a special case of Advanced — almost everything on auto — rather than a separate product.
  2. Every control is a deterministic input. (version, seed, all settings) → piece, exactly and repeatably. A control is a value fed to the seeded pipeline (genre → sample/invent the style → compose → perform → synthesize), never a post-hoc tweak of the finished audio. (Live changes, §8, obey this too — they feed the ongoing pipeline.)
  3. The tiers are three distinct modes with a selector at the top of the page (Tom's call). Start shows a few consequential choices; Intermediate the familiar musical parameters; Advanced the full board including invented parameters. Switching mode reveals or hides controls but never discards what you set — a value pinned in Advanced still applies in Start, and vice-versa.
  4. Label for perception; scale for perception. Start/Intermediate controls use high-consensus listener words (brightness, warmth, energy, density, space), not engineering names. Numeric controls step in perceptual units: dB for loudness, semitones/octaves for pitch, ratio steps for tempo (a JND is ~5%, so equal-BPM steps feel coarse when slow and trivial when fast). Technical/original names appear only at Advanced, glossed on first use.
  5. Defaults are the product. Because most controls stay on auto, what “auto” produces is what most listeners hear. Defaults must be tasteful, not merely safe: a minimal surface raises the bar on the sampler behind it. Every genre ships a good-sounding default region.
  6. Randomize is first-class and constrained. A big Play and a dice/“new piece” control sample only the un-pinned axes, within the chosen genre(s) — “surprise me here,” not “surprise me anywhere.” Because the seed and settings live in the URL and copy to the clipboard, a lucky result is always recoverable and shareable, so exploration is lossless.
  7. Guard the failure modes. The one-knob macros and the small Start set must still yield coherent, non-arbitrary music. Behind every simplified control sits the coherence machinery (a stable reference pitch, within-piece repetition, a novelty budget); simplicity at the surface, not in the generator.

4 · Start tier

The flow: the user picks one or two genres (up to two — two genres blend), optionally nudges a few headline dials, and presses Play. A random seed that encodes those choices is generated and the piece plays; the seed fills in everything else. The dice reseeds within the same genre(s); the seed is shown, editable, and copyable.

Folk (Tom's call). Its default region samples across that family (a fiddle-and-pipes reel one seed, a Nordic asymmetric-beat tune the next), anchored on the shared traits (modal melody, drone/heterophony, dance meters, acoustic timbres). The wiki's european-folk-and-dance.md supplies the material — already broad (Celtic, Nordic, Balkan, Klezmer, Iberian, Central-European), and now extended with a Baltic section (Lithuania, Latvia, Estonia: sutartinės canon-in-seconds, drone box-zithers, hocket pan-pipes) so the family is fully covered.

4.3 · How a two-genre meld works

Selecting two genres blends two presets — it is not on-the-fly style invention (that stays Intermediate/Advanced, per Tom's call that Start offers presets only). The blend follows the wiki's account of how real fusions actually work (hybridization-and-fusion.md): a coherent hybrid usually takes different axes from different parents (e.g. rhythm/groove from one, mode/harmony from the other, timbre split) rather than numerically averaging everything — a naive average of two styles often lands between both listenable regions. So a meld picks, per axis, which parent leads (seeded, within compatibility rules), giving results like “a house pulse under classical harmony” or “lo-fi drums with folk modal melody.”

  • Max two selections (Tom's call), so the result stays coherent — three-way melds overspend the novelty budget.
  • Balance defaults to even (50/50) for now (Tom's call — provisional, adjustments expected once melds can be heard); the blend ratio and per-axis parent choice are exposed as the Style blend control at Intermediate/Advanced (§6.3). Full meld rules: style-vector schema §5.
  • Incompatible pairings (e.g. free-tempo Ambient × grid-locked House) are reconciled by the coherence rules — one parent's incompatible axis yields to the other's — rather than forced.

6 · Advanced tier

The full board — everything above at finer resolution, the style-space axes exposed directly, per-layer control, and the parameters invented for this site. The choice-overload caveat runs in reverse here: the expert user wants all of it, so this tier is deliberately generous and is not capped for its own sake. 6.2 · Per-layer overrides

Pin any setting per voice/role rather than globally — e.g. this palette for the lead, that register for the bass, more swing on the drums only — generalizing the engines' per-axis pinning to per-layer.

7 · Auto, seed & the URL

  • What Play does: collect the chosen genre(s) + any pinned controls → generate a seed that encodes them → the seed samples every un-pinned axis within the genre's region → compose → perform → synthesize. Same (version, seed, settings) ⇒ same piece, always.
  • The URL: seed + settings ride as a compact, versioned suffix on the URL; the same full URL reproduces the piece exactly. A version field lets old links keep working as the site grows. Encoding details are deferred to music-representation-and-notation.md; it need not be human-decipherable.
  • The dice (“New piece”): reseeds only the un-pinned axes — so pinning three controls and hitting the dice explores just the rest, within the genre selection.
  • Copy: the seed copies to the clipboard; a lucky accident is never lost.
  • Crossing modes keeps your settings: revealing Advanced never resets Start; hiding it never discards a pin.

8 · Live setting changes New — Tom's request

The requirement (Tom): changing a setting should alter the music immediately, at the point it is playing, without restarting the piece — because in the five test engines every change restarts from the top, which makes the difference impossible to hear.

Feasibility: yes — confirmed, and it is the better architecture. The only reason the test engines restart is that each one composes the entire piece up front, then plays it; any change re-runs that whole composition, so it can only start over. The site avoids this by composing just ahead of the playhead instead of all at once — the standard model for interactive/generative music (game-audio middleware, live-coding systems, Eno's generative apps all work this way). A change then affects the music that hasn't been generated yet, and the piece morphs forward seamlessly.

8.1 · How it works — compose just-in-time

The engines already schedule notes with a short lookahead (they decide the next fraction of a second of audio a little before it's needed). The site extends that from scheduling to composing: it generates only the next bar or phrase slightly ahead of the playhead, reading the current control values each time. So the audible “now” is fixed, but everything past the lookahead horizon is still open and responds to whatever the knobs currently say. No restart — the change simply lands as the music arrives at it.

8.2 · Three response speeds

Not every control can (or should) change at the same instant — a reverb tweak can be instant, but re-voicing the harmony mid-note would sound broken. So each control declares how fast it takes effect:

SpeedApplies toWhen it lands
Instantmix & sound: reverb/space, brightness, stereo width, volume, expression amount, dynamic range — and continuous timbrethe next scheduled note, and sustained notes ramp in place (tens of ms) — like turning a mixing-desk knob
Next boundarylocal-structural: density, harmonic richness, mode/key, palette, swing, meter, lead prominence, variationat the next bar or phrase, so the change is musical, not a mid-note jump
Re-plan from hereglobal/form: length, form template, intensity arc, and a genre or blend changethe remaining timeline is recomputed from the current position; a genre/blend swap uses a two-bar crossfade (Tom's call, provisional) so the seam is graceful

This is exactly the game-audio “parameter bus” idea the wiki already documents (control-surfaces-and-user-parameters.md, film-and-game-music.md): one control value flows continuously into the running generator.

8.3 · What it means for the shareable link (Tom's concern)

Tom is right that the URL changes with every tweak. The clean resolution keeps the compact one-URL-one-piece guarantee:

  • The URL always mirrors the current settings snapshot + seed. As you turn knobs live, it updates to reflect where you've arrived. Re-opening it (or sharing it) plays a deterministic piece that begins with those settings — i.e., it reproduces the destination, played from the top, not the exact sequence of live twiddles you made to get there. That keeps the link short and the determinism rule intact.
  • The live morph itself is a real-time performance. Its exact path depends on when you moved each knob, which a single snapshot can't capture — and for Tom's stated goal (hearing a setting's effect immediately) that's fine: the snapshot reproduces the sound you settled on.
  • Decided (Tom): the link stores only the destination snapshot — the "capture the exact morph" option is dropped. Live tweaking stays a real-time performance; the snapshot reproduces the sound you settled on.
Decided. Live changes are a first-class architectural commitment — the site's engine is built “just-in-time” from the start, unlike the test engines. The genre/blend swap is a two-bar crossfade and the shared link is the destination snapshot only (both Tom's calls, 2026-07-09). This folds into the site-architecture design doc and the wiki's engine-architecture page. Full data-flow spec: style-vector schema §7.

9 · How controls map to the engine

One pipeline serves all three modes because they differ only in how many axes the user pins vs. lets the seed sample:

  • A Start genre is a preset region of the style space — a style-vector center plus per-axis spreads. Picking it sets the sampling region; the seed draws a point inside. Picking two selects a blended region (§4.3).
  • An Intermediate control is a friendly handle on one axis (or a small rollup of a few), shifting the region's center or narrowing its spread on that axis.
  • An Advanced control is direct access to a raw axis, a per-layer pin, or an invented parameter (arc, blend, novelty budget…).

The object all of them write into is the style vector — specified in the companion style-vector schema, which fixes each field's type, how presets/invented styles/melds instantiate it, and the coherence constraints that keep a sampled point (and a two-genre meld) listenable. This document only fixes what the user sees and can set.

10 · Decisions & remaining questions

Decided by Tom (2026-07-09):

  1. Start genres — the eight stand, with Folk broadened from Celtic-only to pan-European (§4.1).
  2. Start size — keep the five headline dials + Genre. Genres are multi-select buttons, max two; two selected blend into a melded style (§4.2–4.3).
  3. Energy — one knob is fine; its meaning adapts per genre and per genre-combination.
  4. Intermediate set — accepted as proposed, to adjust after hands-on experimenting.
  5. Invented parameters — accepted as proposed, to adjust after experimenting.
  6. Start = presets only — on-the-fly style invention stays Intermediate/Advanced; Start offers single presets and two-preset melds.
  7. Three distinct modes with a Start/Intermediate/Advanced selector at the top of the page; settings preserved across modes.
  8. Live setting changes (new) — the piece must change live without restarting; confirmed feasible via just-in-time composition (§8).
  9. Two-genre meld balance is even (50/50) for now — provisional; adjustments expected once melds can be heard.
  10. Live genre/blend swap = a two-bar crossfade — provisional (§8.2).
  11. The shareable link stores only the destination snapshot (§8.3).

Items 9–11 are the three follow-ups the first review raised; Tom answered them 2026-07-09 and they are worked out in full in the companion style-vector schema (the meld in §5, the live morph in §7, serialization in §8). No taxonomy questions are currently open.
