# European folk and dance musics

European folk and dance music is, collectively, the largest reservoir of tightly grammared **instrumental** dance forms available to this project: hundreds of named tune types, each defined by a specific meter, tempo band, strain form, and characteristic rhythmic figure, refined over generations of actual dancing rather than concert listening. These traditions were rarely about harmonic drama or long-range development — they are short, cyclic, formulaic, and built to be played for hours from memory, which is exactly the profile a generative engine can exploit: the "rules" are compact, substantially codified by practitioners and ethnomusicologists, and the forms are inherently loop-friendly. This page surveys six clusters — Irish/Scottish/Celtic session music, Nordic fiddle traditions, Balkan and Klezmer musics, Iberian flamenco and fado, the Baltic drone-and-hocket traditions, and Central/Western European couple dances — each reduced to a tune-type grammar an engine can encode. It defers general aksak (additive-meter) theory to [rhythm-and-meter.md](rhythm-and-meter.md), ornament technique to the sibling [ornamentation-and-embellishment.md](ornamentation-and-embellishment.md), and modal-harmony mechanics to the sibling [modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md](modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md), adding only what is specific to these repertoires.

## Irish, Scottish, and Celtic session music

The best-documented tune grammar in this survey — practitioner conventions are extensively cross-checked on session.org (thesession.org), an Irish-session reference site used here as practitioner documentation rather than scholarship.

### Tune types

Each type is a fixed meter-plus-feel package, not just a time signature:

| Type | Meter | Rhythmic feel | Indicative tempo |
|---|---|---|---|
| Reel | 4/4 | even eighth notes, driving | ~104–140 BPM in practice |
| (Double) jig | 6/8 | two dotted-quarter beats, "diddly-diddly" | ~110–130 BPM (dotted-quarter pulse) |
| Slip jig | 9/8 | three beats to the bar, lighter/faster | played briskly; three-step dance metre |
| Hornpipe | 4/4 | written even, performed with a swung/dotted lilt and a marked figure closing each strain | ~120–130 BPM, more deliberate than a reel |
| Polka | 2/4 | two-beat, bouncy | brisk, Sliabh Luachra style |
| Slide | 12/8 | fast compound duple/triple hybrid feel | ~140–150 BPM |
| Strathspey | 4/4 | dotted rhythms including the **Scotch snap** (a short accented note before a longer one) | moderate, deliberate |

Breandán Breathnach's classic reference tempos (double jig 127, single jig 137, slip jig 144, reel 224 counted at two beats/bar ≈ 112, hornpipe 180) and modern session-practice ranges do not fully agree on counting convention (dotted-crotchet vs. simple beat), which is itself informative: tempo is felt and negotiated in a session, not fixed by a metronome marking. Hornpipe's defining device — an even-looking 4/4 realized with a swing plus a stock closing turn at the end of each strain — is a written/performed split worth encoding explicitly (write straight eighths, perform with a swing ratio, per the tempo-dependent long–short model in [rhythm-and-meter.md](rhythm-and-meter.md)).

### The AABB strain form — common, not absolute

The default architecture is **two 8-bar strains (A, B), each repeated**, giving a 32-bar tune (AABB) — the type most session tunes and most session.org transcriptions follow. It is not universal: some tune types depart systematically (slides are commonly built from 4-bar parts rather than 8-bar ones), and irregular-bar-count or three-part tunes exist throughout the repertoire — named examples include "The Ace and Deuce of Pipering" (a 13-bar strain) and the three-part reel "The Boys of Malin." Treat AABB as a strong default template with per-type and per-tune exceptions, not a hard constraint — see [form-and-structure.md](form-and-structure.md) on binary/rounded-binary forms generally.

### Tune sets and medleys

Session musicians chain **2–3 (sometimes more) tunes into a set**, playing each through several times before sliding into the next, typically ending on a cue understood by the group. The load-bearing convention is **type consistency** (reels follow reels, jigs follow jigs) while **key and mode are varied for contrast** — mixing major and relative-minor-ish (Dorian/Mixolydian) tunes, and sometimes deliberately stepping up a key for the final tune of a set. Transitions are managed by ear: altering a tune's final bar to cadence cleanly into the next tune's opening pickup. Sets are frequently chosen spontaneously by whoever leads, which is itself a generative process worth imitating (pick-next-tune-by-compatible-type-and-key rather than a fixed playlist).

### Modal melody

Irish/Scottish tunes draw on a small mode set built on the same D-major/G-major fingering (whistle, flute, fiddle): **Ionian** (major, most common), **Mixolydian** (♭7 relative to Ionian — the classic "modal" reel/jig sound), **Dorian** (the usual "minor-key" tune mode — brighter than natural minor because of its natural 6th), and, less often, **Aeolian**. D and G are the most common tonal centers, with A common for tunes favoring open string drones on fiddle; see [modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md](modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md) for the general mechanics of establishing a mode without a dominant (drone, first/last-note emphasis, sounding the characteristic degree early).

### Heterophony and accompaniment

Session performance is **heterophonic**: every melody instrument plays the same tune roughly together with small individual ornamental variation, not a strict unison and not harmonized parts — see [auditory-perception-basics.md](auditory-perception-basics.md) on how listeners stream near-simultaneous variants of one line. Chordal accompaniment is a later addition and stays modal/drone-oriented rather than functional: **DADGAD** guitar tuning (an open-D-suspended-4th voicing with no third, hence neither major nor minor — introduced to folk guitar by Davey Graham in the early 1960s and brought into Irish accompaniment specifically by Dáithí Sproule and the Bothy Band's Mícheál Ó Domhnaill from the mid-1960s) and the **Irish bouzouki** (GDAD, the same open/ambiguous logic) favor partial chords and ringing drone strings that let the melody itself define major/minor/modal color, rather than a fixed chord chart — tunes conventionally have no single "correct" chord progression, and even nominally modal tunes often locally raise the leading tone at cadences (practitioner mnemonic: "Mixolydian is major with a hint of minor; Dorian is minor with a hint of major").

### Ornamentation (pointer)

Named ornament types include the **roll**, **cut**, **tap**, **cran**, **triplet**, and **slide** (grace-note category, not the 12/8 tune type above); see [ornamentation-and-embellishment.md](ornamentation-and-embellishment.md) for technique and taxonomy — not re-derived here.

## Nordic fiddle traditions

### The Swedish polska: genuinely asymmetric beats

The **polska** (a large family of Swedish triple-meter tunes, not the Polish "polka" — regional named sub-types include the semiquaver-type Bingsjö and Hälsinge/Delsbo polskas and the older, syncopated Boda and Orsa polskas of Dalarna, sometimes differing village to village) is the clearest documented case of a **non-isochronous beat-level meter**: unlike an ordinary 3/4 (three equal beats), the polska's three beats have systematically unequal durations. This is a real, decades-old empirical research lineage rather than folklore, running from **Ingmar Bengtsson's** early notational studies (1974) through **Bengtsson and Alf Gabrielsson's** technology-based timing analysis (Uppsala/KMH; "Methods for Analyzing Performance of Musical Rhythm," *Scandinavian Journal of Psychology* 21(1), 1980, pp. 257–268), which introduced **SYVAR** ("systematic variations") for consistent, non-random deviation from an isochronous grid, found a cyclic **long–short alternation of subpulse durations** characteristic of polska's rhythmic feel, and — as a useful contrast case — showed the **Viennese waltz**, despite also being triple meter, is *isochronous* at the beat level (an evenly spaced 3+3 compound-duple structure). **Sven Ahlbäck** (Royal College of Music, Stockholm) formalized polska's asymmetry with a beat-proportion notation, commonly cited as **2+4+3** (in sixteenth-note-equivalent units summing to a measure — roughly 22%/44%/33% of the bar), i.e., a short first beat, a long second beat (roughly twice the first), and a third beat of intermediate length — explicitly *not* a fixed 9/16 subdivision but a proportional model that also allows tunes to alternate symmetric (3+3+3) and asymmetric (2+4+3) measures within one piece ("About Asymmetrical Beat in the Polska," in *The Polish Dance in Scandinavia and Poland*, Stockholm: Svenskt Visarkiv, 2003). This exact-ratio framing is not unanimous: **Tellef Kvifte** has directly challenged fixed-ratio models, arguing performance timing is not well explained by "a particular set of non-flexible rhythmic building blocks," and proposes instead a stable higher-level **"common slow pulse"** (the measure/dance-gravity level) without a correspondingly stable **fast pulse** (sub-beat subdivision) — i.e., the asymmetry is real and audible but resists reduction to one portable numeric ratio. A 2021 continuation of the Ahlbäck lineage (Misgeld, Holzapfel, Kallioinen & Ahlbäck, "The Melodic Beat: Exploring Asymmetry in Polska Performance," *Journal of Mathematics and Music*) found beat asymmetry is consistent within a performer/tune and correlates with the note-density of each beat, supporting Bengtsson's own earlier, informally-reported observation that beats with more subdivisions tend to run measurably longer. Treat the *existence and rough shape* of the long–short–medium asymmetry as well-established, and whether a single fixed ratio (vs. Kvifte's looser "common slow pulse") is the right model as a genuinely open question (see below).

### Hardingfele, springar, and halling

The **hardingfele** (Hardanger fiddle), Norway's national instrument (from the late 17th century, associated with the Hardanger region), carries **4–5 sympathetic resonance strings** beneath the bowed strings, adding a ringing, halo-like resonance to every note — a real acoustic feature worth modeling (a bank of undamped strings tuned to the scale, driven by proximity/frequency match to the bowed note) rather than only reverb. It is the characteristic instrument for **springar** (an asymmetric triple-meter dance in the same non-isochronous family as the polska, distinguished from the more regularly-structured Norwegian **pols** — *telespringar*, the Telemark regional form, has its own body of motion-capture and timing research continuing the Bengtsson/Gabrielsson lineage) and **halling** (a solo, athletic/acrobatic male display dance in a *regular* duple meter, roughly 95–106 BPM in 6/8 or 2/4 — an explicit contrast case: same instrument and cultural context as springar, but not part of the asymmetric-beat family). Regional hardingfele styles differ audibly: Telemark playing favors emotional depth and extended phrasing at moderate tempo; Hardanger-region playing favors dense ornamentation and faster tempos.

### Nyckelharpa

Sweden's **nyckelharpa** ("keyed fiddle," home region Uppland) is bowed like a fiddle but stopped by ~37 wooden keys sliding under the strings, and carries its own bank of sympathetic strings (the modern chromatic instrument: 3 melody strings, 1 drone string, 12 sympathetic strings) — the mechanical key action plus the sympathetic resonance together give it a bright, glassy, bell-like, harmonically dense timbre distinct from an unkeyed fiddle. See [timbre-and-orchestration.md](timbre-and-orchestration.md) for realizing sympathetic-resonance timbre in synthesis.

## Balkan musics

### Aksak dance meters in practice

Balkan "limping" (aksak) meters are not one grouping per time signature — real dances show **named regional variants of the same meter, and musicians and dancers sometimes count the identical rhythm differently**, which is worth reporting honestly rather than flattening to one canonical grouping (aksak theory itself is covered in [rhythm-and-meter.md](rhythm-and-meter.md); this is the practice layer). The foundational theory is Bulgarian composer-theorist **Dobri Hristov's** 1913 treatise on "neravnodelni razmeri" (irregular meters), which models every asymmetric Bulgarian meter as built from a "short" beat (2 fast pulses) and a "long" beat (3 pulses):

| Dance | Meter | Common grouping(s) |
|---|---|---|
| Paidushko horo (Bulgaria) | 5/8 (5/16) | 2+3, occasionally reversed to 3+2 in southern Bulgaria/Macedonia/Greece |
| Rŭchenitsa / ročenica (Bulgaria/Macedonia) | 7/8 (also notated 7/16) | **2+2+3** is most cited, but Hristov's own theory documents **3+2+2** as an equally attested variant ordering — both are real, not an error in either direction; fast tempo is often felt as 4+3 |
| Daichovo horo (Bulgaria) | 9/8 | 2+2+2+3 ("quick-quick-quick-slow"); unusually, a circle dance *with* a named leader role |
| Kopanitsa (Bulgaria) | 11/16 | Musicians count **2+2+3+2+2** (long beat = 3 units, in the middle); dancers reportedly feel the same rhythm as **4+3+4** (long = 4) — a genuine, sourced inversion of what counts as "long" between the musical and choreographic framings of one pattern |
| Gankino horo (Bulgaria) | 11/16 (also 11/8) | Same 2+2+3+2+2 family as kopanitsa in the academic/Hristov lineage — but the name is not univocal: some dance-teaching traditions apply "Gankino horo" to a different, 9/8 dance (2+2+2+3) instead, so the label alone is not a safe key into one fixed meter |
| Čoček (pan-Balkan, Romani-associated) | varies — 2/4, 7/8, 9/8, 10/8 all attested in named tunes | when 9/8: 2+2+2+3 or 2+2+3+2 — genuinely style/tune-dependent; one strand of scholarship (Silverman) treats "aksak/irregular" as the more essential trait of the genre even though many individual čoček tunes are plain 2/4 |

This project's percussion engine already implements parametric aksak meters (5/8 = 2+3, 7/8 = 2+2+3) as unequal beat-groups over a fine grid — see [findings-percussion-engine.md](findings-percussion-engine.md) — which generalizes directly to the fuller table above.

### Kolo and horo: the line-dance culture

**Kolo** (Serbian/Croatian/Bosnian, "wheel") and **horo**/oro (Bulgarian/Macedonian/Montenegrin) — with **hora** as the Romanian cognate — name the same social format across the region: an open or closed circle, single chain, or parallel lines of dancers holding hands or belts, danced at weddings and communal gatherings from simple walking steps to intricate figures. Formation details are locally specific rather than uniform: Balkan-dance scholar Dick Crum documented that Serbian practice typically has an **open circle with a named leader (*kolovodja*) and an "end man" (*keć*)**, often developing into a line or serpentine, while circles in northern regions more often stay closed with no leader — leadership structure genuinely varies by dance and region rather than following one rule.

### Bulgarian close-second diaphony

Traditional Bulgarian village polyphony (documented especially in the Shope and Pirin regions, and among the Bistritsa Babi singers recognized by UNESCO) uses **close, dissonant intervals — especially major and minor seconds — sung deliberately over a sustained drone**, not as an error but as a stable, intentional harmonic color; two voices create dissonance that resolves through ornamental melodic movement rather than through chord change. This singing tradition grew out of women's communal agricultural and ritual work and is functionally tied to seasonal, wedding, and mourning contexts.

### Balkan brass: trubači, Romani virtuosity, and čoček

**Trubači** (brass bands, the name from *truba*, "trumpet") fuse Ottoman-derived military-band instrumentation with Balkan folk repertoire; the style is strongly associated with **Romani (Roma) musicians**, whose virtuosic, highly ornamented improvisation defines the sound (Serbia's Guča trumpet festival, founded 1961, is the best-known public showcase, though ethnomusicological accounts of Guča also document real, contested ethnic categorization of "Serbian" kolo bands versus "Gypsy" čoček bands on the ground — Carol Silverman, *Romani Routes: Cultural Politics and Balkan Music in Diaspora*, Oxford University Press, 2012). **Čoček** is the characteristic dance/rhythm genre played by these bands, danced solo/improvisationally rather than in a fixed circle format like kolo — its meter is genuinely variable across sources and named tunes (see table above) rather than fixed, and it is frequently syncopated regardless of the underlying meter.

### Romanian doina and hora

The **doina** is a free-rhythm (unmetered), heavily ornamented, melismatic lament — vocal or instrumental (violin, nai/pan flute) — built by rubato elaboration of a fixed, usually descending melodic skeleton, favoring Dorian/Aeolian/Mixolydian modal color and, when performed by more than one voice, loose heterophony. Its free-rhythm-prelude function is directly documented as the historical replacement for an earlier practice: klezmer scholar Walter Zev Feldman (1994) records that among Jewish musicians in the region "the doyne had been only recently introduced from Romania, replacing an older genre named taksim" (the Ottoman/Arabic improvisatory prelude — see [maqam](tuning-and-scales.md)) — i.e., the doina-as-taksim-successor lineage is sourced for its klezmer adoption specifically. A further comparison to Indian [alap](indian-classical-music.md) is this project's own cross-cultural framing, not a claim found in the literature searched for this page — flag it as informed comparison only. The **hora**, by contrast, is a metered round dance (predominantly duple — 2/4, 4/4, 6/8, 12/8 — though slower sub-variants near 5/16 or 3/8 exist) at moderate tempo, danced in a circle with a repeating three-forward/one-back step pattern — structurally a world apart from the doina despite the two sometimes appearing back-to-back in performance (a rubato doina prelude resolving into a metered dance, a pattern klezmer inherited directly as a three-part doina→hora→bulgar suite — see below).

## Klezmer

### Modes

Klezmer melody runs on a small set of characteristic modes, distinct from Western major/minor:

- **Freygish** (also Ahava Raba/Ahava Rabboh, structurally identical to the Phrygian dominant scale / fifth mode of harmonic minor): scale degrees **1–♭2–3–4–5–♭6–♭7** — a flatted second next to a *natural* third is the signature interval (an augmented second between ♭2 and 3), shared in essence with flamenco's Phrygian-major final sonority and with Arabic maqam Hijaz. A 2025 peer-reviewed corpus study of Beregovski's classic Ukrainian/Moldovan klezmer collection (Malin & Shanahan, *Music Theory Online*) found freygish accounts for roughly **27%** of tunes, against ~37% plain minor, ~22% "raised-fourth" (see next), and ~14% major.
- **Mi Sheberakh** (also called "Ukrainian Dorian" or the "doina scale"; Malin & Shanahan's "raised fourth"): a Dorian-like minor mode whose defining feature is a **raised 4th degree**; it is the mode most associated with the doina-style rubato prelude within klezmer repertoire.

### Core dance forms

The Hebrew University's Jewish Music Research Centre "Lexicon of Klezmer Terminology" (drawing on named klezmer-scholarship sources: Sokolow 1987/1991, Stutschewsky 1959, Alpert 1993/1996, Rubin 1997, Feldman 1994) gives precise, sourced accent patterns for the core forms:

- **Freylekhs** — duple (2/4), a circle/line dance; its underlying accent pattern is additively grouped rather than evenly quadruple: **"8/8 meter, composed of two groups of 3 and one group of 2: 1-2-3 1-2-3 1-2"** (Sokolow 1987) — an aksak-like 3+3+2 cell packed inside a nominally symmetrical bar, comparable in spirit (though not lineage) to the Cuban tresillo. The corpus study above found freylekhs appear across *all four* modes (minor, freygish, raised-fourth, major) rather than being tied to one, i.e., the dance form and the mode are largely independent choices.
- **Bulgar** — primarily 2/4, "built on phrases with alternating triplets and quavers" (Rubin 1997); descends from the non-Jewish Bessarabian line dance *bulgareasca* ("in the Bulgarian manner") and became, per Rubin, "the most fashionable dance among American-Jewish immigrants" — effectively the core American klezmer dance form.
- **Hora / zhok** — a slow **3/8** dance with the second beat of each pair omitted, an explicit "limping" mechanism: **"the pattern is 1-3 1-3, etc."** (Sokolow 1991), earning the Yiddish nickname ***krimme tantz*** ("cripple's dance"). "Hora" and "zhok" name the same dance under regional dialect labels (western Moldavia vs. Bessarabia) — this klezmer usage is a different rhythmic animal from the predominantly duple Romanian round-dance hora described above, and from the fast duple modern Israeli hora; klezmer scholars flag all three as distinct dances sharing one name.
- **Doina** — a rubato, unmetered improvisatory prelude, strongly associated with the Mi Sheberakh mode, conventionally the opening piece of **a three-part suite: doina, then hora/zhok, then bulgar/freylekhs** (Sokolow 1987) — i.e., rubato lament resolving through a slow limping dance into a fast circle dance, mirroring the tempo/energy arc many of this page's other traditions also use.

### Ornament and ensemble

**Krekhts** [Yiddish, "sob/moan"] names a vocal-style catch or break ornament, imitating cantorial/vocal expression, especially idiomatic on clarinet and violin — technique owned by [ornamentation-and-embellishment.md](ornamentation-and-embellishment.md), not elaborated here. The classic **kapelye** (klezmer band) centers on clarinet and/or violin as lead voice(s), with cimbalom/tsimbl (hammered dulcimer) or accordion and upright bass or tuba for harmony/bottom, and (from the 1970s American klezmer revival onward) drum kit; brass (trumpet, trombone) appears especially in the Eastern European/Balkan-adjacent lineage.

## Iberian: flamenco and fado

### Compás as an accent-pattern timeline

**Compás** is flamenco's rhythmic cycle, and — like the timelines of [rhythm-and-meter.md](rhythm-and-meter.md) and [west-african-rhythm.md](west-african-rhythm.md) — it is best modeled as an **accent pattern over a fixed pulse count**, not merely an onset count or a time signature. The best-documented case: **soleá and bulerías share a 12-count cycle with accents conventionally placed on counts 3, 6, 8, 10, and 12** (counting from 1). This exact numbering is remarkably consistent across the flamenco-pedagogy literature and is qualitatively consistent with Grove Music Online's description of soleá's meter as "a combination of triple and duple beat bars... with strong beats at the end of each bar" — but it should be reported as **strong, convergent practitioner/pedagogical consensus rather than a claim independently verified here in peer-reviewed text**: bulerías in particular is also documented with more than one valid accent-pattern variant (12-3-6-8-10 in some sources, 12-3-7-8-10 in others, and some performers count it as a 6-beat rather than 12-beat bar). Soleá and bulerías share the same underlying 12-count family but differ enormously in tempo and feel: soleá is grave and unhurried, bulerías fast and driving (practitioner figures cite roughly 70–120 for soleá versus roughly 195–240 for bulerías — a large, multiple-of relationship rather than directly comparable click-track BPM, since compás counting is not a simple quarter-note metronome). **Seguiriya** is consistently described as a related but distinctly-accented 12-pulse cycle, felt as more irregular/tension-laden than soleá's — but sources genuinely disagree on the exact accented beat numbers (one scholarly thesis gives accents on 1-3-5-8-11, a general reference gives 2-4-7-10-12, and a guitar-pedagogy source instead describes it as five unequal-length pulses rather than fixed beat numbers at all); treat "a rotated/differently-accented cousin of soleá's cycle, more tense and unresolved" as the safe, honestly-hedged summary rather than committing to one specific numbering.

### Palo families

**Palos** are flamenco's named song/dance forms, each with its own compás, mode, tempo, and emotional character. Beyond soleá and bulerías: **alegrías** (part of the Cádiz **cantiñas** family alongside romeras, caracoles, and mirabrás — sharing the 12-count skeleton but in major mode, tempo roughly 120–170, a festive character partly derived from the 19th-century Aragonese jota, and a strict internal section sequence: salida, paseo, a minor-mode contrasting *silencio*, castellana, then a zapateado/escobilla footwork section closing into a bulerías); **seguiriya** (the deepest, most tragic palo, associated with *cante jondo*, "deep song" — a framing that traces partly to Manuel de Falla and Federico García Lorca's 1922 Concurso de Cante Jondo in Granada, which cast seguiriya as flamenco's most "pure" form, a period characterization worth flagging rather than treating as neutral modern consensus). Soleá is widely called the "mother" of the flamenco palos, though this label is itself contested — some scholarship notes soleá is younger than the tonás and seguiriya it is said to have "mothered."

### The Phrygian/Andalusian cadence

Flamenco's signature harmonic gesture is the **cadencia andaluza**, a descending four-chord phrase. Peter Manuel's peer-reviewed analysis states it directly: "the most basic and structural chord progression is Am-G-F-E, which is best described as iv-III-II-I rather than as a Western i-VII-VI-V" — i.e., flamenco practice analyzes the phrase from the Phrygian tonic (E) rather than the relative natural-minor tonic (A) — and that "harmony in flamenco and much of Andalusian folk music generally employs the chord progression F-E rather than B7-E or E7-Am as the fundamental cadence," meaning the final approach is a modal ♭II–I, not a functional dominant-to-tonic (Manuel, "Evolution and Structure in Flamenco Harmony," *Current Musicology* 42, 1986, pp. 47, 50). The final "I" is typically raised to a major triad (E major, not modally "pure" E minor) — a Phrygian-major final sonority; for the general mechanism see [modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md](modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md)'s Phrygian entry and [harmony.md](harmony.md) for the functional-cadence contrast.

### Falseta and palmas

A **falseta** is a guitarist's own melodic instrumental passage, defined in the same Manuel (1986, p. 47) source as "instrumental interludes (falsetas) preceding and following the sung coplas [verses]" — composed or improvised, and the place where a flamenco guitarist's individual voice is showcased against the otherwise communal cante/compás. **Palmas** (hand-clapping) supply rhythmic texture through two contrasting timbres, though the exact naming pair varies by source: the consistent member is **sordas** (soft, muffled claps made by cupping the hands), opposed either to **fuertes** ("hard" claps) or to **claras/secas** (bright, sharp claps) — these bright-clap terms appear to be near-synonyms for one clap type rather than a fixed three-way taxonomy. This is the most thinly-sourced claim on this page (tablao/dance-school sites and a weakly-cited Wikipedia article, no peer-reviewed source found); the underlying concept — two or more palmeros interlocking soft and bright claps into a composite pattern that reinforces or plays against the compás accents — is nonetheless well and consistently attested.

### Fado (Portugal)

**Fado** is Lisbon's urban song tradition, built around **saudade** [Portuguese, an untranslatable ache of longing, nostalgia, and bittersweet melancholy], sung by a solo *fadista* over an underlying duple meter with substantial rubato — the music "pauses at the end of a phrase and the singer holds the note for dramatic effect" — accompanied characteristically by the 12-string, pear-shaped **guitarra portuguesa** (two regional models with different scale lengths and tunings, Lisboa and Coimbra, plucked with fingerpicks rather than bare nails) alongside a 6-string **viola** (Portuguese for a steel-string guitar, not the orchestral instrument) and sometimes bass. Rui Vieira Nery — author of the standard history, *Para uma História do Fado* (2004; English translation *A History of Portuguese Fado*, 2012), and chair of the scientific commission for fado's 2011 UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage inscription — is the field's standard scholarly authority; UNESCO's own description frames fado as a 19th-century multicultural synthesis of Afro-Brazilian sung-dance genres, rural song brought by internal migration, and cosmopolitan urban idioms, transmitted informally within families and performing communities.

## Baltic traditions

The eastern Baltic — **Lithuania**, **Latvia**, and **Estonia** — preserves a layer of practice that is archaic in a way most of this page's other repertoires are not: its defining textures are **drone**, **close-second polyphony**, and **interlocking (hocket)** rather than the tune-plus-accompaniment grammar of the Celtic or Central European material. Two language families meet here — the Baltic languages (Lithuanian, Latvian) and Baltic-Finnic Estonian — and the musical traits cut across that line, which is why they are surveyed together. The standout tradition, Lithuanian sutartinės, is one of the world's clearest cases of deliberate, sustained major-second harmony organized as strict canon; it is also this cluster's best-documented material, and the rest (zithers, panpipes, drone song) is more unevenly sourced, flagged below where it thins.

### Sutartinės: canon and hocket in seconds

**Sutartinės** (singular *sutartinė*, from *sutarti*, "to be in concordance/agreement") are multipart songs from northeastern Lithuania (the Aukštaitija region), inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of Intangible Cultural Heritage in **2010**. Their melodies are tiny — UNESCO's description gives **two to five pitches** over two parts (a meaningful main text plus a refrain of nonce syllables) — but the parts are stacked so that adjacent voices sound a **major second** apart for much of the piece, a texture treated not as passing dissonance but as the intended, stable color. The standard scholarly framing (Eirimas Velička, *Frontiers in Psychology*, 2024, a peer-reviewed synthesis) sorts them into **dvejinės** (two-part, two singers in parallel seconds), **trejinės** (three-part, sung as a strict three-voice **canon**, each voice entering staggered on both phrases), and **keturinės** (four-part) [Lithuanian, roughly "twosomes / threesomes / foursomes"]; archives document "from 19 to 38" distinct performance methods (UNESCO puts the count near forty). Rhythm is complementary and hocketed — "a long note in one voice is filled with shorter notes in the other" — giving frequent syncopation and the impression of a single rhythmically moving cluster.

Whether the second was actually *heard* as consonant is an interpretive claim, and worth flagging as one: Velička cites Paliulis's judgment that "our ancestors' ears were fond of the interval of a second; it was apparently a consonance for them" — an inference about historical perception, not a measured fact. The genre is one instance of what German-language scholarship calls *Schwebungsdiaphonie* [German, "beating diaphony," the deliberate use of narrow, acoustically rough intervals] (Velička, citing Messner and Brandl) — the same close-second aesthetic as the Bulgarian diaphony described above and Bosnian *ganga*. See [auditory-perception-basics.md](auditory-perception-basics.md) for how the ear streams two lines a second apart; the audible beating between them is exactly what makes the texture "shimmer."

Sutartinės also have a fully instrumental life, and this is where the hocket is most explicit. They are played on **skudučiai** — a set of five to eight *unbound*, single-note wooden panpipes distributed among several players, each blowing only one or two pitches, so the scale and the tune exist only in the ensemble's interlocked composite — and on **ragai** and **daudytės** [Lithuanian, "horns" and long wooden trumpets, roughly 1.4–2.3 m, each sounding four or five natural harmonics], likewise hocketed in pairs (Šimonytė-Žarskienė; Ambrazevičius & Žarskienė on skudučiai tuning). Velička explicitly names the "closest analogues" of these wind pieces as Solomon Islands panpipe ensembles and Central African Baka forest-people wind music (citing Simha Arom's interlocking-hocket studies, 1991) — a direct bridge to the interlock/inherent-pattern architecture of [west-african-rhythm.md](west-african-rhythm.md) and [gamelan.md](gamelan.md).

### Baltic box zithers: kanklės, kokle, kannel

The region's signature string instrument is a plucked box zither: **kanklės** (Lithuania), **kokle**/kokles (Latvia), and **kannel** (Estonia), regional members of the **Baltic psaltery** family (with the Finnish kantele and Russian gusli). Each is carved from a single hollowed piece of wood (linden, maple, black alder, or oak) with a thin spruce soundboard, trapezoidal, and — distinctively — the strings do **not** pass over a bridge, which yields a quiet, ringing, harmonically rich but weakly projecting tone (Wikipedia, "Baltic psaltery," "Kanklės," "Kokle"). Tuning is diatonic with a built-in **drone**: the kanklės sets its lowest string as a bass drone, an interval of a fifth between the shortest and longest strings and seconds between neighbors (Organology; Katherine Rhoda); the kokle traditionally tunes its lowest (drone) string a **fourth below the keynote**. The idiomatic technique is to strum or pluck with one hand while the other hand **damps** the strings that should stay silent — "sound the whole rake, mute what you don't want," which naturally produces drone-plus-melody rather than note-by-note lines. The proposed etymology is itself evocative: Finnish linguist Eino Nieminen derived the names from a proto-Baltic *kantlīs*, "the singing tree."

Sacred associations are best documented for the Latvian **kokle**: it carries celestial symbolism (called "Dievs's kokle," carved with sun ornaments) and appears in some 274 dainas with mythic players including **Saule** (the sun goddess) and Jānis. Broader claims sometimes attached to the kanklės specifically (played at funerals, forbidden to women) circulate in popular accounts but could not be verified against a reliable source in this pass — treat them as unconfirmed. For realizing the bridgeless, ringing pluck timbre, see [timbre-and-orchestration.md](timbre-and-orchestration.md).

### Regilaul and the drone-polyphony belt

Estonian song rests on **regilaul** [Estonian] / runo-song, the old Baltic-Finnic sung poetry in *regivärss*, the **Kalevala meter**: an alliterative trochaic tetrameter (an eight-syllable line), formulaic, parallelistic, sung to narrow, chant-like melodies of a few tones with a lead-and-chorus (call/echo) delivery (Sarv, folklore.ee; Wikipedia, "Runic song"). It was widespread until the 18th century, then displaced by end-rhymed, more Western "rhymed folksong" (*riimiline rahvalaul*). As an instrumental template it contributes meter and structure — a steady eight-pulse trochaic line built from short additive cells — more than pitch; see [rhythm-and-meter.md](rhythm-and-meter.md).

The close-second-over-drone aesthetic is not only Lithuanian. Two further Baltic traditions carry it, both UNESCO-recognized in 2009: Estonian **Seto leelo** [the Seto people of southeastern Estonia] — a lead singer's line answered by a choir that overlaps and repeats it, over a sustained lower **drone** (the *torrõ*) beneath a higher part (the *killõ*), producing a rough parallel polyphony — and the Latvian **Suiti** bourdon singing of Kurzeme (on UNESCO's Urgent Safeguarding list), in which a *saucēja* [Latvian, "caller"] leads, a *locītāja* ["bender"] repeats the line about a semitone lower, and the other women hold a bourdon drone on "ēēē" (Latvijas Kultūras kanons). Both are the Baltic wing of the same close-second diaphony family as the Bulgarian material above. (Datings of these oral traditions as "archaic," and the popular claim that Seto leelo is "~2,000 years old," should be read as claims, not established chronology.) The drone is also instrumental: the Estonian bagpipe **torupill** (bag historically of seal or other stomach; a single-reed chanter of 5–6 holes; one or two — rarely three — drones) supplied dance music whose tunes "resemble archaic runic songs," some of Swedish origin (Wikipedia, "Torupill"); Lithuania's **Labanoro dūda** is the local bagpipe equivalent (documentation thin in the sources searched). Continuous drone routes straight into the modal defaults of [modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md](modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md).

### Baltic dances and the modal-drone default

Two layers coexist. The older is round-, chain-, and figure-dance — Lithuanian *rateliai* (round dances) and *suktinis* [Lithuanian, "twirler"], plus the danced side of sutartinės — carrying the drone/modal, narrow-range, stepwise, heterophonic character (Dorian, Aeolian, and pentatonic colors are the typical palette). The newer layer is the 19th-century pan-European couple dances absorbed into village ensembles: **polka** (2/4), **waltz** (3/4 — including the Estonian *labajalavalss*, "flat-foot waltz," danced without rising onto the toes), and **schottische** — the same forms treated in this page's Central/Western European section, and not distinctively Baltic in meter. Reliable absolute tempos for the specifically Baltic dances were not found in the sources searched (as with the schottische below). The living public face of all of this is the **Baltic Song and Dance Celebrations** — mass amateur festivals begun in 1869 (Estonia), 1873 (Latvia), and 1924 (Lithuania), proclaimed a UNESCO Masterpiece in 2003 and inscribed on the Representative List in 2008.

## Central and Western European couple dances

A table of the common salon/folk couple dances, most of which entered the ballroom repertoire in the 19th century and still anchor folk-revival and social-dance practice; several have older, informal antecedents worth a passing note but not covered here (see [medieval-and-renaissance-music.md](medieval-and-renaissance-music.md) for the deeper history of couple- and processional-dance forms that precede this set):

| Dance | Meter | Accompaniment pattern | Tempo | Origin/notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Waltz (Viennese) | 3/4 | "oom-pah-pah" (bass, then two mid-register chords) | ~180 BPM / 58–60 measures per minute — isochronous at the beat level, unlike polska (Bengtsson & Gabrielsson 1980) | Vienna; competition "slow"/International Standard waltz is danced far slower, ~84–90 BPM — roughly half the Viennese rate in the same meter; likely a parallel sibling of, not a direct descendant of, the Austrian *Ländler* |
| Polka | 2/4 | "oom-pah" | ~104–124 BPM (the separate Irish session polka above runs faster, >130) | Bohemia, early 1830s; the specific "peasant girl Anna Slezáková" origin story is a widely repeated legend first published 14 years after the fact (1844) — treat as folklore, not history |
| Mazurka | 3/4 (sometimes 3/8) | strong accent unsystematically on beat 2 *or* 3 (Britannica narrows this to beat 2 specifically); rhythmic cell of two sixteenths plus two eighth notes | mazur ~120–140 BPM, oberek ~160–180 BPM, kujawiak slower/melancholic | Mazovia, Poland, 16th century; an umbrella covering the moderate mazur/mazurek, the slow kujawiak, and the fast oberek, historically danced as a linked suite — Chopin's ~57 piano mazurkas mix rhythms from all three (stylized concert works, not tempo evidence) |
| Schottische | duple (commonly notated 4/4, sometimes 2/4) | step-step-step-hop plus a turning figure; "a kind of slower polka" (Oxford Companion to Music) | moderate; no reliable absolute BPM found in the literature searched | Genuinely no Scottish origin despite the German-derived name — but the specific, widely repeated "Bohemian origin" claim is only thinly (tertiary-level) sourced; the more careful Oxford Companion to Music commits only to "Continental European" |
| Musette | 3/4 (waltz-family) or dance-hall duple | accordion with intentionally **detuned reed ranks** ("musette tuning") producing a shimmering beating chorus | moderate–brisk | Paris bal-musette (emerging late 19th/very early 20th century), named after and modeled on the *cabrette* (Auvergnat bagpipe, itself historically called "musette"), which the accordion displaced as Auvergnat and Italian migrant communities mixed in working-class Parisian dance halls |
| Tarantella | predominantly 6/8 today | driving compound duple/triple, often major/minor alternation | starts moderate, **documented accelerando** | Southern Italy (Naples, Puglia — the Salento form is *pizzica*); rooted in the *tarantism* ritual (dancing an accelerating tarantella as folk cure for a tarantula bite); classically studied by Ernesto de Martino, *La terra del rimorso* ("The Land of Remorse," 1961) |

Two honest caveats on the tarantella. First, the earliest surviving notated tarantella tunes — eight melodies collected from Apulia by Jesuit polymath Athanasius Kircher (*Magnes, sive de arte magnetica*, Rome, 1641) — are mostly in **common time, not 6/8**; the now-standard compound-meter description hardened only in later centuries. Second, the accelerando is best supported as a **ritual performance practice** rather than a fixed compositional feature: secondary accounts of De Martino's 1959 fieldwork describe musicians watching a possessed dancer and deliberately raising tempo to intensify and eventually resolve her trance, which fits Gilbert Rouget's broader cross-cultural finding (*Music and Trance*, University of Chicago Press, 1985) that tempo acceleration plus crescendo is a near-universal trance-induction technique — but this page could not verify the claim against De Martino's primary text directly, so treat it as well-supported secondary consensus, not a confirmed direct quotation.

## Implications for generative engines

- **Dance-form template library.** The tables above are directly consumable as a data structure: `{ name, region, meter, beatGroups (for aksak: array of 2s/3s), tempoRange, accentPattern, characteristicCell, strainForm }`. This is a style-pack instantiation in the sense of [style-and-genre-overview.md](style-and-genre-overview.md) — one JSON-like record per dance, loadable by a shared composer the way [shared-libraries.md](shared-libraries.md)'s `theory`/`rng` modules are loaded by every engine. Seed a piece by picking one row (or a small compatible cluster, e.g., a reel-only tune set) rather than sampling meter and rhythm independently.
- **AABB-with-varied-repeats strain generator.** Implement the Irish/Scottish default (two 8-bar strains, each repeated) as a generator that produces A, a *varied* repeat of A (ornament/passing-tone changes, not a new tune), B, and a varied repeat of B — directly realizing [form-and-structure.md](form-and-structure.md)'s repetition-with-return at the smallest possible timescale, and providing the "establish then vary" motivic discipline [melody.md](melody.md) asks for. Keep an explicit exception table (slides' 4-bar parts, klezmer forms, Balkan asymmetric phrase lengths) rather than hard-coding 8 bars everywhere.
- **Compás as a generalized timeline.** Flamenco's 12-count accent skeleton (3-6-8-10-12) and the Bulgarian/klezmer aksak groupings are the same underlying device as the West African bell pattern and this project's own percussion-engine timelines ([findings-percussion-engine.md](findings-percussion-engine.md), [west-african-rhythm.md](west-african-rhythm.md)) — a fixed, asymmetric **accent cycle**, not a grid, that every other voice phases against. The novel case compás adds: a timeline can be an accent-weight cycle over an *already-regular* pulse count (12 even eighth notes) rather than an onset/silence pattern — i.e., "timeline" generalizes to any repeating accent-weight vector, onsets included or not. This extends [rhythm-and-meter.md](rhythm-and-meter.md)'s timeline treatment directly.
- **Mode-plus-drone harmony defaults.** Irish Mixolydian/Dorian tunes, klezmer freygish/Mi Sheberakh, flamenco's Phrygian-major cadence, and Romanian/doina Dorian/Aeolian/Mixolydian color are all instances of [modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md](modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md)'s drone/pedal-anchored, characteristic-degree-driven harmony rather than functional T–S–D syntax — route all five region clusters through that page's engine patterns (palette rotation, drone-consonance filtering, the "color clock" that keeps the characteristic degree audible) rather than building bespoke harmony per tradition.
- **Polska-style asymmetric beats as structured, not random, microtiming.** The long–short–medium beat-proportion literature (Bengtsson, Gabrielsson, Ahlbäck) is a second, independent case — beyond the funk/groove literature in [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md) — of a real musical tradition using *fixed, learned, non-isochronous* timing as its identity rather than noise. Implement it exactly the way [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md)'s structured-not-random principle demands: a per-style beat-proportion table (e.g., 2:4:3 sixteenth-units for one polska type) applied consistently every measure, with the option to alternate symmetric and asymmetric measures per Ahlbäck, never uniform random jitter.
- **Tune-set logic as a small constraint solver.** Chaining tunes by matching type and varying key (Irish sets) or moving between a rubato prelude and a metered dance (klezmer doina→freylekhs, Romanian doina→hora) is a reusable "sequence compatible units, vary one axis" pattern applicable to whole-piece pacing; see [tempo-duration-and-pacing.md](tempo-duration-and-pacing.md) for tempo-relationship design across such transitions.
- **Honesty labeling.** Several claims here are genuinely contested or regionally variable rather than settled (aksak groupings, seguiriya's exact relationship to soleá's compás, exact polska beat ratios, klezmer hora's meter) — carry the hedges into any UI copy or self-report ("inspired by," not "authentic reproduction of"), consistent with [generative-music-failure-modes.md](generative-music-failure-modes.md)'s caution against confident exoticism.

- **Sutartinės as a canon-in-seconds / secundal-drone generator.** Treat the close major second not as a dissonance to resolve but as the *target* sonority: generate a 2–5-note melodic cell and run it as a strict 2–4-voice **canon** at a short temporal offset so neighboring voices sit a second apart, filling each voice's long notes with the others' short notes (complementary rhythm). This is a concrete [modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md](modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md) case where the "dissonance" *is* the color, and an interlock case that reuses this project's percussion-engine interlock ([findings-percussion-engine.md](findings-percussion-engine.md)) and the split-a-line-between-two-voices trick of [west-african-rhythm.md](west-african-rhythm.md) / [gamelan.md](gamelan.md); [auditory-perception-basics.md](auditory-perception-basics.md) explains both why two lines a second apart still stream and why they beat/shimmer. Carry the honesty label — deliberate seconds read as "wrong" to untrained ears, so this is "inspired by sutartinės," not a reproduction ([generative-music-failure-modes.md](generative-music-failure-modes.md)).
- **Hocket-distributed voices and bridgeless-zither / pan-pipe / drone timbres.** The skudučiai model — one or two pitches per "player," the scale emerging only from the ensemble composite — is the [west-african-rhythm.md](west-african-rhythm.md) interlock generator applied to *melody*, and the Solomon-Islands/Baka analogue Velička draws from Arom makes the cross-tradition kinship explicit. Pair it with the cluster's characteristic timbres for [timbre-and-orchestration.md](timbre-and-orchestration.md): the bridgeless plucked box zither (kanklės/kokle/kannel — short, ringing, harmonically rich, low-projection pluck, drone-plus-strum via selective damping), end-blown stopped single-note pipes, and the continuous bagpipe (torupill) drone that grounds the modal harmony.

## Open questions

- Exact, sub-genre-specific polska beat-proportion tables (which regional polska type maps to which ratio) — the literature establishes the phenomenon robustly, but a portable numeric table for implementation needs direct, non-paywalled access to Ahlbäck 2003 and the Kvifte/Johansson/Misgeld-et-al. lineage, which this pass could not obtain.
- Whether an engine-generated aksak pattern needs a named-dance label to read as intentional, or whether the accent structure alone (independent of cultural label) is what listeners respond to — relevant to how much named-tradition specificity the dance-form library needs, echoing the same fidelity-vs-generality question raised in [findings-percussion-engine.md](findings-percussion-engine.md).
- How much of flamenco's compás is teachable as accent-weight alone versus requiring the call-and-response between cante, guitar, and palmas that practitioner sources treat as inseparable from the rhythm itself.
- Whether klezmer's mode/dance-form independence (freylekhs appearing in all four modes per the corpus study) generalizes to other traditions in this page, or is klezmer-specific.
