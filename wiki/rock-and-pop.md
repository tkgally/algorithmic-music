# Rock and pop

Rock and pop are the most familiar music in the world and, remarkably, the least covered so far in this wiki: [harmony.md](harmony.md) already carries the corpus statistics and [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md) the backbeat, but no page yet treats rock/pop as a compositional *system* — the form templates, the riff as a generative unit, the band as a fixed set of texture roles, and the instrumental branches (surf, post-rock, math rock, instrumental metal, shoegaze) this instrumental-only project can compose directly, without the vocal-topline layer that defines mainstream pop. This page is that system-level treatment: it links out for the numbers and the psychology already covered elsewhere and stays close to what an engine needs to *generate* idiomatic rock/pop, riff-first or loop-first, verse-chorus or through-composed.

## Form: the verse–chorus paradigm and its variants

[form-and-structure.md](form-and-structure.md) covers verse-chorus and AABA as form archetypes generally, and [phrase-structure.md](phrase-structure.md) covers the pop-specific SRDC (statement-restatement-departure-conclusion) unit; this section adds the rock-specific typology and its history.

John Covach's widely used primer sorts most rock songs into three formal types: **simple verse** (a single repeating verse-refrain unit, no distinct chorus — early rock and roll and blues-form songs), **AABA** (a 32-bar-derived strophe A repeated, contrasted by a bridge B, then A returns — inherited from Tin Pan Alley), and **verse-chorus** (distinct verse and chorus modules alternate, the chorus usually the song's structural and emotional goal). Within verse-chorus, Covach further distinguishes **simple verse-chorus** (verse and chorus share one harmonic progression, distinguished only by melody, lyric, and texture) from **contrasting verse-chorus** (each has its own progression) — a useful binary for an engine, since simple verse-chorus is far cheaper to generate (one loop, two treatments). **Compound AABA** nests smaller verse/chorus units inside each "A," and is common in more harmonically ambitious rock; a 2021 corpus study finds AABA-derived structures persist as a style-distinguishing device even in heavy metal, long after mainstream pop moved to verse-chorus (Hudson 2021).

The **prechorus** (also "channel" or "lift") is a historically dateable addition. Jay Summach traces its emergence to the early-to-mid 1960s — an incipient version already audible in Del Shannon's "Runaway" (1961) — as verse-chorus songs absorbed the four-part srdc rhetoric: statement and restatement collapse into the verse, departure becomes the prechorus, conclusion becomes the chorus. Its incidence rose steadily: roughly a third of Billboard top-20 verse-chorus songs had a prechorus by 1969, about 60% by the late 1980s (Summach 2011). It is defined by function, not fixed content: harmonic rhythm accelerates, harmony moves off the tonic (often toward the subdominant) for the first time in the song, phrase units fragment into shorter, faster-repeating cells, and texture builds — all cues that this section's job is to *want* the chorus, not to state or answer anything itself. The **post-chorus** is the newer, mirror-image device: a short (4-8-bar) section immediately after the chorus that either closes it out with fragmented vocal material (a "codetta" type) or opens a second, EDM-influenced climax of its own with synth hooks or a drop (an "anthem" type); it entered music-theoretic discourse around 2011 and is common where 2010s pop crosses over with EDM production (see [electronic-and-dance.md](electronic-and-dance.md) for the drop vocabulary it borrows). The **middle eight** (British usage for the AABA-style bridge, often exactly eight bars) still functions as the one guaranteed contrast point in an otherwise loop-heavy song — new harmony, new key area, or at minimum new register — before the final chorus reprise(s).

The commonest full-song template built from these modules is intro–verse–chorus–verse–chorus–bridge–chorus(es)–outro (informally "VCVC-bridge-C"). Trevor de Clercq's corpus dissertation, built on the same rock corpus underlying [harmony.md](harmony.md)'s statistics, is the standard reference for exactly how often each variant occurs and where sections land in time, but this research pass could not extract its quantitative tables (flagged in Open questions below). One qualitative finding is worth recording as a caution against over-confident labeling: de Clercq reports that independent analysts' section labels, even reduced to broad categories (verse/chorus/bridge), agreed only about 67% of the time — rock form is often genuinely ambiguous, not merely hard to label (de Clercq 2012). Intro and outro conventions (riff-only intros, fade-outs, cold endings) belong to [beginnings-endings-and-transitions.md](beginnings-endings-and-transitions.md).

## Harmony in practice

[harmony.md](harmony.md) owns the corpus statistics (chord-frequency priors, the Axis/loop family, root-motion symmetry); this section covers how rock composers actually *use* harmony, a different question from what a corpus counts.

**Power chords** — root and fifth (and often an octave), no third — are rock's basic harmonic atom. Omitting the third is not a beginner's simplification; it is what makes the chord **mode-ambiguous**: with no third to commit it to major or minor, the same shape reads as whichever quality the surrounding riff or vocal implies, so a single power-chord riff can be reharmonized or recontextualized cheaply. It is also a distortion-engineering necessity — the third's overtones intermodulate unpleasantly under heavy gain, while a bare fifth stays clean and punchy even at high distortion (Wikipedia, "Power chord"). **Loop harmony** — a short (usually two-to-four chord) progression repeated for a whole section or song, with no cadence in the classical sense — is the default over which most riff-driven rock is written; the composer's job is not to resolve the loop but to make everything *above* it (melody, arrangement, dynamics) carry the direction the harmony withholds, the trade already discussed generally in [harmony.md](harmony.md)'s loop-harmony section. **Pedal-point riffs** hold a bass note fixed while the harmony above it changes or a riff figure repeats around it — the cheapest way to anchor a riff-heavy passage to a tonic without functional motion (general pedal-point mechanics also in [harmony.md](harmony.md)). The **blues-derived I-IV-V grammar** — three chords, mostly root position, cycling in the 12-bar frame or compressed derivatives of it — underlies much of rock's harmonic vocabulary even outside literal blues forms; the full idiom and its turnarounds belong to [blues-country-and-roots.md](blues-country-and-roots.md).

**Modal rock** is rock's other major harmonic dialect. Walter Everett's influential taxonomy places rock tonality on a spectrum of six systems from traditional major/minor through modal practice to fully chromatic, nonfunctional harmony; general modal theory is in [modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md](modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md), so what matters here is Everett's rock-specific findings. Genuinely modal rock (his "Type 2") keeps coherent voice-leading but reduced harmonic function — Simon & Garfunkel's "The Sounds of Silence" is Aeolian with a real melodic descent but no functional pull to tonic; the Beatles' "Tomorrow Never Knows" is Mixolydian reduced to I-bVII-I neighbor motion over a static tonic pedal. Everett explicitly **rejects** the folk claim that blues is "the basis" of all rock harmony: blues-derived rock, in his analysis, keeps a *major-mode* harmonic/bass structure (boogie bass, piano) while only the *melody* draws on the minor pentatonic — structure and surface pull from different scales at once. A separate, harmonically flatter dialect ("Type 5") treats each degree of a minor-pentatonic scale as the root of its own major triad in parallel, nonfunctional motion — I-bIII-IV-I and I-bVII-I cycles with no voice-leading logic beyond parallel doubling (Monkees, Nirvana, Living Colour examples) — functionally identical to loop harmony above but pentatonic-derived rather than diatonic.

## The riff as the central rock unit

A **riff** is a short, repeated, register-fixed melodic/harmonic figure with enough rhythmic and intervallic distinctiveness to be instantly recognizable — rock and jazz's name for what classical theory calls an ostinato (the terms describe the same device; usage tracks genre, not structure). Where a chord progression is a harmonic skeleton and a melody floats above it, a riff typically **fuses** harmony, rhythm, and melody into one inseparable object, often carried by a single voice (guitar or bass) low in the ensemble's register. Four documented riffs illustrate the design space:

- **"Smoke on the Water"** (Deep Purple, 1972) — a pure power-chord riff: parallel dyads on G, Bb, C, G, Bb, Db, C, with a syncopated long-long-short rhythm and a chromatic upper-neighbor touch (C to Db and back) in its second half. No thirds anywhere, so the riff itself never commits to major or minor.
- **"Iron Man"** (Black Sabbath, 1970) — a chromatically descending power-chord riff, its bass moving stepwise E-D-C#-C, slow and doom-paced under heavy distortion; root motion by half- and whole-step rather than by fifths is exactly the kind of "flat," non-functional succession [harmony.md](harmony.md) documents for rock generally. (The band's earlier, self-titled song "Black Sabbath" is the more famous case of the tritone itself as riff material — the G-Db "devil's interval" that became a genre-founding sonority for metal.)
- **"Sunshine of Your Love"** (Cream, 1967) — a pentatonic/blues-box riff built on the D minor pentatonic "box" fingering, syncopated and low-register, harmonized with power chords over an I-IV-I blues frame; Clapton's solo deliberately shifts to D *major* pentatonic (and briefly quotes "Blue Moon") for contrast, showing how one riff's box shape can generate a whole improvisatory vocabulary by shifting mode within the same fret-hand geometry.
- **"You Really Got Me"** (The Kinks, 1964) — historically the riff that established the vocabulary: two power chords a whole step apart, stop-start rhythm with silence doing as much work as sound, played through a deliberately damaged amplifier (Dave Davies slit the speaker cone with a razor blade) — the moment power chords and distortion became rock's default sonority rather than a novelty.

**Riff-first composition** — write the riff, then build the song around it — is standard practice from Iommi's Sabbath riffs through the Stones, Zeppelin, and Deep Purple to modern metal songwriters; a riff functions as the seed the rest of the arrangement elaborates, with verse/chorus material, dynamics, and even lyrics often fitted to an existing riff rather than the reverse (MusicRadar). A riff *is* a hook in the sense used in [melody.md](melody.md)'s hooks section — pitch/rhythm distinctiveness at a structurally salient, repeated position — but rock riffs are typically lower in register, more rhythmically foregrounded, and more harmonically self-sufficient than vocal hooks; the psychology of why short repeated figures lodge in memory belongs to [memory-earworms-and-catchiness.md](memory-earworms-and-catchiness.md).

## The band as a texture machine

The standard rock ensemble is less a set of instruments than a fixed set of **texture roles**, each with a register and a job: bass locks to the kick drum and outlines the harmony's root motion; drums supply the backbeat (snare on 2 and 4 against the kick — full mechanics in [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md)); rhythm guitar (or a second guitar/keys part) carries the harmonic/rhythmic ostinato, often the riff itself or a strummed/chugged variant of the chord loop; lead guitar occupies the highest, most foregrounded melodic role, entering fully only for hooks, fills, and solos; keys, where present, fill the textural gap between the rhythm instruments' attack and the vocal/lead line as a sustained pad or a syncopated comp figure. This role-and-register division is what keeps a five- or six-piece band legible without formal orchestration training — each part has its own frequency lane and its own job.

**Loud-quiet-loud** dynamics — a hushed, sparse verse exploding into a loud, distorted chorus, often on the same riff or chord loop — is the genre's most portable large-scale dynamic device. The Pixies popularized it on *Surfer Rosa* (1988) and *Doolittle* (1989); Kurt Cobain named it explicitly as the model for Nirvana's arrangements, including "Smells Like Teen Spirit," telling Rolling Stone in 1994 that "I was basically trying to rip off the Pixies. I have to admit it," and elsewhere that "we used their sense of dynamics, being soft and quiet, and then loud and hard." Structurally it is loop harmony's cheapest large-scale contrast tool: hold the chords fixed, vary only dynamics, distortion, and instrumentation density between sections.

**Palm-mute vs. open chug** is a one-bit texture switch available on every riff: palm-muting (resting the picking-hand edge on the strings near the bridge) damps sustain into a tight, percussive chug, while releasing it lets the same notes ring fully open — the same riff can toggle between rhythmic-percussive and harmonic-sustained roles with no pitch change at all, which is why it is such a cheap and reliable arrangement device (verse chugged, chorus open, for instance). **Guitar voicing constraints** worth encoding for plausibility: standard tuning (E2-A2-D3-G3-B3-E4) makes power chords a two-finger shape that must relocate for each new root; **drop-D tuning** (low string down a whole step, to D2) turns the same power-chord shape into a one-finger barre across the bottom three strings, which is why drop-D and its "drop" relatives dominate riff-heavy hard rock and metal; a full barre-chord shape lets one moveable hand-position play any major or minor triad up the neck at the cost of muting open strings. An engine does not need a fretboard model to benefit from this — it needs to know that rock voicings are cheap, moveable, register-constrained (mostly below roughly E4) shapes, not freely spaced chorale voicings.

## Instrumental rock — the directly-usable branch

This is the branch of rock/pop this instrumental-only project can compose without the vocal-topline problem the rest of the genre depends on.

**Surf** rock's identity is almost entirely textural: a tremolo-picked (rapid same-note reiteration) lead line drenched in spring reverb, over a driving, tom-heavy drum pattern. Dick Dale, drawing on Middle Eastern/Arabic scale color and a fast picking style modeled on darbuka (goblet-drum) patterns, paired his playing with Fender's newly developed outboard spring-reverb unit (1961) to define the genre's "wet," cavernous guitar tone. The characteristic "surf beat" keeps the backbeat on 2 and 4 (per [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md)) but adds a driving eighth-note pulse and tom-tom fills or grooves (as in the Surfaris' "Wipe Out") rather than the hi-hat-centric feel of most rock drumming.

**Post-rock** is the genre most worth building an engine mode around: it discards verse-chorus almost entirely in favor of Brad Osborn's **through-composed** archetypes — forms with no recapitulated section — and its most recognizable shape is what Osborn later named the **terminally climactic form**: a piece that withholds its main high point until a single, thematically distinct climax at or near the very end, rather than distributing memorable peaks across recurring choruses. Osborn's corpus analysis finds post-rock instrumentals cluster in the **One-Part Monothematic** type: a single idea (often just two alternating chords) is stretched over the piece's whole duration and transformed purely by texture, dynamics, and additive layering rather than by introducing new material — his example "Mean Time 'Till Failure" reduces "pounding drums... to [a] quiet and serene synthesizer-only ending" through one continuous process. Godspeed You! Black Emperor and Explosions in the Sky are the genre's reference points in practice: pieces commonly run 6-12 minutes and sometimes much longer (Godspeed tracks routinely exceed 12), built as a single slow crescendo, or a small number of them, from a hushed, sparse opening to a full-band, maximally loud climax — quiet-loud-quiet at the scale of an entire piece rather than a single section (Osborn 2011).

**Math rock** applies odd and mixed meters (5/4, 7/8, 11/8...), tapping, and angular, interlocking guitar loops to rock instrumentation; it grew out of King Crimson's and Yes's 1970s meter experiments and Slint's early-1990s reworking of hardcore, with the genre name itself coined in music-press usage around 1994. Structurally it is riff-based like the rest of rock but treats meter itself as the source of angularity that harmony provides elsewhere; [rhythm-and-meter.md](rhythm-and-meter.md) covers the additive-meter and polyrhythm machinery an engine needs to generate this.

**Instrumental metal**, briefly: riff cycling (a small set of riffs alternating with little large-scale form beyond that alternation) is the primary structural device; palm-muted chug functions as a rhythmic-percussive layer nearly independent of pitch (the "djent" school, after Meshuggah, foregrounds this to the point of treating guitar as a polymetric percussion instrument, riffing in one cycle length against a drum pattern in another); drop and extended-range tunings extend downward reach; double-kick patterns and, in extreme subgenres, blast beats (alternating kick/snare single-stroke rolls, conventionally sixteenth notes at 180 BPM or faster) supply rhythmic density; and harmonic-minor and Phrygian-dominant (the fifth mode of harmonic minor: 1-b2-3-4-5-b6-b7) coloring gives riffs a "neoclassical" or "exotic" flavor distinct from the natural-minor/pentatonic default.

**Shoegaze/dream-pop** treats the electric guitar itself as a pad: multiple heavily effected (reverb, delay, chorus, pitch-shifted/reverse reverb) guitar layers merge into a single indistinct "wall of sound," explicitly descended from Phil Spector's production aesthetic, functioning harmonically and texturally rather than as distinguishable riffs or leads — a genre where the mix *is* the arrangement.

**Prog rock's suite forms**, in one paragraph: bands including Yes, King Crimson, and Genesis built pieces from multiple contrasting named "movements" rather than pop's verse/chorus modules, borrowing large-scale strategies (sonata-like return, palindromic or rotational large form) from Western art music while retaining rock instrumentation and riff-based local material; Yes's "Close to the Edge" (1972), a single 19-minute piece in four connected movements returning to its opening material, is the genre's reference case (Covach 1997).

## Pop melody and production, briefly

Pop **topline** writing (the vocal melody, often composed separately over a finished instrumental) follows a consistent register strategy: verses sit low and narrow, telling the story in a comfortable, conversational range with short repeated rhythmic-melodic cells; the chorus **lifts** — higher register, wider range, the song's most memorable phrase — with the prechorus doing the registral and harmonic work of climbing from one to the other (BMI). This is the same apex and register-center logic [melody.md](melody.md) already documents, applied at the section level rather than the phrase level. Beyond the notes, mainstream pop and rock production is itself compositional: the specific arrangement, timbre, and mix choices are not a neutral rendering of a score but part of what the song *is* — Albin Zak's study of rock record-making frames this as a genuinely collaborative compositional act distributed across songwriter, musicians, and producer/engineer, not a hierarchy with "composition" at the top and "performance" merely realizing it. This is the same producer-as-composer reality [electronic-and-dance.md](electronic-and-dance.md) documents for dance music, generalized to rock/pop production as a whole.

## Implications for generative engines

- **Riff-first composition mode.** Generate a short (one-to-two-bar), register-fixed ostinato first — pitch-class contour plus a distinctive rhythmic profile, power-chord- or pentatonic-box-sourced — then derive a variation set (transposition to new loop-harmony roots, register/octave shift, rhythmic displacement or fragmentation, palm-mute-vs-open toggling) and let the arrangement arc mute/unmute and layer that set across sections, rather than composing melody and harmony independently and laying a riff on top as decoration.
- **Two reusable dynamics-form envelope shapes.** (1) *Loud-quiet-loud*: oscillate density/distortion/register between sections on an otherwise-fixed riff or loop, cheapest at section-boundary granularity. (2) *Terminal climax*: a single, near-monotonic build (texture/density/dynamics rising with no intervening drop) reserved for the piece's final portion, for post-rock/instrumental-arc pieces — do not resolve early. Both are dynamics-as-form devices usable without a single cadence, complementing [tension-and-release.md](tension-and-release.md)'s general tension budget.
- **Band-ensemble style packs.** Encode the role/register/voicing contract from "the band as a texture machine" as a style pack ([generative-music-design-patterns.md](generative-music-design-patterns.md) P4/P5, per [style-and-genre-overview.md](style-and-genre-overview.md)): bass register plus kick-lock, backbeat drums, rhythm-guitar riff/chug layer, lead register ceiling, optional keys pad — plus a small, cheap voicing grammar (power-chord dyads, drop-tuning one-finger barres, palm-mute-as-a-bit) rather than free chorale voice-leading. Subgenre packs (surf/post-rock/math-rock/instrumental-metal/shoegaze) differ mainly in which roles are present, their register lanes, and the mute/reverb texture bit.
- **Verse-chorus form template with explicit contrast rules.** Implement Covach's simple-verse-chorus (share the loop, vary texture/register/melody) as the default cheap case; encode the prechorus as a scripted departure — accelerate harmonic rhythm, move off tonic (toward IV), fragment the phrase, raise density — immediately before every chorus; realize the chorus contrast as a register lift plus a density/distortion lift over the verse, matching the pop topline norm above; add post-chorus and middle-eight as optional modules.
- **Loop harmony vs. the existing functional machinery.** [findings-tonal-phrase-composer.md](findings-tonal-phrase-composer.md)'s backward Piston-walk generates goal-directed functional harmony one cadence at a time — the right engine for a bridge or middle-eight or a classical-leaning section, wrong for a verse or chorus built on a repeating loop. Rock/pop mode should instead pick a short loop (from the Axis/doo-wop/blues families already in [harmony.md](harmony.md), or a pentatonic-derived parallel-triad loop per Everett's Type 5) once per section and hold it, spending the Piston-walk machinery only where a song deliberately goal-directs (a prog/suite passage, a classical-leaning bridge).
- **Synthesis hooks for the texture devices.** Palm-mute-vs-open, tremolo picking, distortion/fuzz staging, and spring-reverb-style decay are exactly the kind of original, sample-free synthesis problems [synthesis-recipes.md](synthesis-recipes.md), [original-sound-design.md](original-sound-design.md), and [effects-and-mixing.md](effects-and-mixing.md) already solve generally (saturation, envelope shaping, reverb) — implement the mute/open switch as an envelope-decay-and-filter-brightness pair rather than inventing a new synthesis model.
