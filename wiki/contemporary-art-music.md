# Contemporary art music

Post-1945 concert ("art") music is, for a project that will invent original styles from high-order principles, the richest documented laboratory of deliberate style construction: each of its schools began from an explicit, often published decision about what a new music should be *made of*, and each left a record of whether listeners could actually hear what the system built. That record includes famous perceptual failures — structure elaborate on paper and inaudible in the ear — which are exactly the cautionary data this project needs. This page reads the main post-war techniques as a toolbox rather than a history: integral serialism, aleatory and mobile form, sound-mass and micropolyphony, spectralism (given the most space, because it composes with a synthesizer's own logic), and compositional microtonality, plus a compact survey of what came after minimalism. It links out where a sibling already owns a topic — [texture-and-density](texture-and-density.md) for the sound-mass *parameter*, [style-invention-and-style-space](style-invention-and-style-space.md) for Lerdahl's grammar argument and for Partch/Young, [minimalism-and-process-music](minimalism-and-process-music.md) for Reich and Pärt, [stochastic-chaos-and-automata](stochastic-chaos-and-automata.md) for Xenakis's mathematics — and concentrates on the compositional technique and its audibility.

## Integral serialism: a derivation system, and the audibility problem

### The row and total serialization (the mechanics are simple)

Schoenberg's twelve-tone method orders the twelve pitch classes into a **row** (series), from which a piece draws all its pitch material via four canonical transforms: **prime** (P), **inversion** (I, intervals flipped in direction), **retrograde** (R, played backward), and **retrograde-inversion** (RI), each available at twelve transpositions — a 48-entry derivation table from one ordered set. The post-war generation extended ordering to the other parameters. Messiaen's *Mode de valeurs et d'intensités* (1949) fixed each pitch to a register, duration, dynamic, and attack — a *mode* of values, not yet serial — and Boulez's **Structures Ia** (1952) turned that idea into **integral (total) serialism**, borrowing Messiaen's pitch and duration sets and adding twelve dynamics and twelve attacks, each governed by its own series (Melodigging; Wikipedia "Structures"). Goeyvaerts's *Sonata for Two Pianos* (1951) and Stockhausen's *Kreuzspiel* (1951–52) are parallel landmarks; Babbitt reached fully-ordered pitch independently in the U.S. (*Three Compositions for Piano*, 1947) and later added a **time-point system** mapping durations onto a serial grid. Ligeti's analysis of *Structures Ia* famously showed how near-mechanical the result is — once the tables are set, the surface is largely a readout (Wikipedia "Structures"). As a *derivation system* this is elegant and cheap to code; the ordering guarantees that every pitch class recurs before any repeats, so the material is maximally non-redundant by construction.

### What listeners actually hear

The honest problem is reception, and here the empirical literature is unusually clear. Krumhansl, Sandell & Sergeant (1987) probed serial listening with material from Schoenberg's Fourth Quartet and Wind Quintet: listeners *did* infer graded tone hierarchies from twelve-tone contexts with some consistency, but those hierarchies tracked surface salience (register, repetition, duration) more than serial position, and a classification task found **the prime form was not reliably heard as similar to its inversion, retrograde, or retrograde-inversion**. Dowling's melodic-transformation work converges: trained listeners recognize a *literal* repetition of a row above 90% when notes are equal in length and register, but recognition falls roughly halfway to chance for I/R/RI and for rhythmically varied ("realistic") presentation, with difficulty ascending inversion → retrograde → retrograde-inversion, and no evidence that listeners separate exact-interval transforms from merely contour-preserving ones (von Hippel & Huron 2020, reviewing Dowling; Krumhansl et al. 1987). Retrograde is essentially inaudible as a relation. Fred Lerdahl's "Cognitive Constraints" (1988) supplies the theory — a maximally uniform, non-repeating serial surface offers the listening grammar no stable reference and no hierarchy to build on, opening a maximal gap between compositional and cognized structure; that argument is developed in [style-invention-and-style-space](style-invention-and-style-space.md) and not repeated here. This is a genuine partisan battleground: Babbitt largely *accepted* the gap, arguing listeners cannot track precise parameter values and would inevitably "falsify" the structure (his 1958 essay, editor-retitled "Who Cares if You Listen?"), whereas the cognitive-constraints camp treats the gap as a design fault. The lesson this project takes, stated neutrally: **a derivation system guarantees coherence on paper, not in the ear.** Serial operations remain useful as a pitch-alphabet generator (see the implications), but only where a transform has an *audible* correlate.

## Aleatory and indeterminacy: chance at composition vs. at performance

Two distinct ideas travel under "chance," and the distinction is exactly the one a seeded engine cares about. **Chance operations** inject randomness at *composition time*: Cage's *Music of Changes* (1951) derived a fully-notated, fixed score from I Ching coin tosses ([algorithmic-composition-history](algorithmic-composition-history.md)) — the randomness is upstream of a determinate artifact, and Cage's aim was to remove the composer's taste, not to improve output. **Indeterminacy** instead leaves decisions to the *performer* at play time, so the work is a different object each performance. For Cage these served an anti-intentional aesthetic; for this project their value is inverted and purely structural.

**Mobile (open) form** is the most directly transferable case: modular pieces whose *section order* varies per performance. Stockhausen's **Klavierstück XI** (1956) prints nineteen fragments on one large sheet; the pianist plays whichever the eye lands on and proceeds to any other, taking the next fragment's tempo/dynamic/attack from the end of the last, stopping once a fragment is reached a third time. Boulez's **Third Piano Sonata** (1955–57) lets the performer choose routes through its formants; Earle Brown's **Available Forms I–II** (1961–62) has a conductor fix the order of events in real time, and his earlier *Twenty-Five Pages* (1953) may be played in any page order, either side up; Feldman's *Intermission 6* (1953) is the direct precedent — fragments to be traversed "from any sound to any other" (Wikipedia "Aleatoric music"; "Klavierstücke"). The direct relevance: **a seeded engine that picks a module order (and repeat counts) is mobile form made deterministic** — variable across seeds, fixed per URL. Riley's *In C* is the consonant, quality-guaranteed cousin ([minimalism-and-process-music](minimalism-and-process-music.md)).

## Texture and sound-mass composition

A parallel post-war move made *texture, density, and timbre* the primary material, with individual pitch receding — the modern additions to the classical texture taxonomy. [texture-and-density](texture-and-density.md) treats these as an engine parameter (sound mass, micropolyphony, pointillism, stratification) and is the home page; here is only the compositional-construction angle.

- **Micropolyphony** (Ligeti) builds an evolving cloud from dense canon: many independent voices — sixteen in *Lux Aeterna* (1966), a 16-voice a cappella choir; scores of chromatic parts across several octaves in *Atmosphères* (1961) — each moving stepwise by small intervals at staggered entries and slightly different rhythms, packed so tightly that no single line is individually audible. Ligeti derived the technique from studying Ockeghem and Bach canon, and described the aural result as an "impenetrable texture … a densely woven cobweb": the harmony is a by-product; the *events* are changes in density, register, and internal motion (Wikipedia "Micropolyphony"). The construction — identical or near-identical slow lines, canonically offset, confined to a moving band — is what makes it cheap to synthesize.
- **Cluster and glissando fields** (Penderecki): *Threnody to the Victims of Hiroshima* (1960, 52 strings) fills bands with tone clusters, quarter-tone glissandi, and extended noise techniques, the band's edges and density carrying the form.
- **Massed glissandi and statistical texture** (Xenakis): *Metastasis*, *Pithoprakta* — string glissandi placed by probability distribution; the mathematics is in [stochastic-chaos-and-automata](stochastic-chaos-and-automata.md).
- **Limited aleatorism** (Lutosławski, from *Jeux vénitiens*, 1960–61): each part is notated exactly in pitch, but in *ad libitum* sections players read at their own rhythm and tempo, uncoordinated with each other, then re-sync at conducted boundaries — "they know what to play, not exactly when" (onpolishmusic; Lutosławski Society). It yields shimmering, non-lockstep ensemble texture with no counterpoint solver, and is another seeded-engine natural.

## Spectralism: synthesis-native composition

Spectralism is the post-war school most relevant to this project because it *is* composition with a synthesizer's logic. Grisey and Murail (with Tessier and Lévinas, later Dufourt) founded **Ensemble l'Itinéraire** in 1973; Dufourt coined *musique spectrale* in 1979 (Wikipedia "Spectral music"; "Gérard Grisey"). The premise, in Grisey's much-quoted line, is that "we are musicians and our model is sound" — not literature, not mathematics — and, from his late writing, that the spectral approach is "not a closed technique but an attitude" (via overgrownpath; Grisey, "Did you say spectral?" 2000). Murail insists it is "an aesthetic rather than a style."

### From an analyzed spectrum to orchestral harmony

The signature technique is **instrumental (additive) synthesis**: analyze a real sound's spectrum, then orchestrate its partials as a chord, one instrument per partial. The textbook case opens Grisey's **Partiels** (1975, for 18 instruments, part of the cycle *Les Espaces Acoustiques*, 1974–85): a sonogram of the attack of a low **E2** (~82 Hz) on the trombone — reinforced an octave below at **E1** (~41 Hz) — is decomposed, and its component frequencies are distributed across the ensemble, "the assignment of different instruments to each harmonic partial … to harmonically and gesturally model the dynamic temporal evolution of the attack." The orchestration even honors each partial's measured amplitude: because the fourth partial reads weak in the analysis, Grisey scores it as a soft string harmonic (Wikipedia "Partiels"; Rose 1996; Hasegawa 2009). The orchestra becomes an additive synthesizer, and the "chord" is a snapshot of a real timbre.

### Combination tones, ring modulation, and frequency modulation as harmony generators

Because the harmony is derived from acoustics, spectral composers generate chords with the arithmetic of the electronic studio, applied to instruments: **difference and summation (combination) tones** from a pair of fundamentals, **ring-modulation** spectra (all f1 ± f2), and **frequency-modulation** sidebands. Murail's **Gondwana** (1980) computes FM spectra using Chowning's synthesis mathematics and scores the results for orchestra, "then scored as harmony for the instruments" (perfectcircuit; Rose 1996; "Design in the Spectral Music of Tristan Murail"). IRCAM's sound-analysis tools underwrote the movement. The resulting harmony is neither functional nor freely atonal but *derived* — a third category alongside the tonal and modal-static options in [modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony](modal-and-nonfunctional-harmony.md) and [harmony](harmony.md).

### The harmonicity–inharmonicity axis and process over stretched time

Spectral harmony lives on a continuum from **harmonic** (partials near integer multiples of a fundamental — fused, consonant, low roughness) to **inharmonic** (stretched or bell-like partials — tense, "noisy"). Grisey treated this **harmonic ↔ inharmonic** opposition, alongside a **periodic ↔ aperiodic** rhythmic one, as an explicit tension dimension, moving a sonority slowly along it so the transformation itself is the form (Grisey, *Périodes*; Hasegawa 2009). His *Tempus ex Machina* (1987) theorizes musical time as a "skeleton, flesh, and skin" — chronometric, perceptual, and subjective — and spectral pieces characteristically stretch a single process across many minutes, the antithesis of serial note-atomism. The two schools were, in fact, a generational revolt: spectralism defined itself against serialism's discrete-parameter thinking, favoring continuous timbral transformation over combinatorial pitch logic.

### Why this matters most here

For an engine that *literally composes with a synthesizer*, spectral thinking collapses the composition/synthesis boundary. An acoustic composer must microphone-and-FFT a real sound to learn its spectrum; **this engine already knows the exact partial structure of every voice it builds**, so the "analysis" step is free — it can derive chords from the actual partials of its own oscillators, and set each derived voice's gain from the modeled partial's amplitude. That specific capability is developed as a concrete algorithm in the implications below, and it ties into the project's original-timbre work ([synthesis-recipes](synthesis-recipes.md), [timbre-and-orchestration](timbre-and-orchestration.md), [original-sound-design](original-sound-design.md)) and its roughness scorer ([tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md)).

## Microtonality in composition

Post-war composers used microtones not as an effect but as the pitch *system*; the tuning math is in [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md), so this covers the compositional use. **Harry Partch** built an entire practice around a 43-tone just-intonation gamut and a self-built instrumentarium, framed by his "Corporeal" philosophy — covered in [style-invention-and-style-space](style-invention-and-style-space.md). **Ben Johnston** extended Partch's just intonation to *conventionally-notated* instruments by inventing an accidental-based **extended just intonation** notation (new symbols for septimal, undecimal, and higher-prime ratios), realized in his string quartets and *Suite for Microtonal Piano* (Fonville 1991; Gann). The early-20th-century quarter-tone lineage runs through **Ivan Wyschnegradsky** (an "ultrachromaticism" of quarter, sixth, and twelfth tones, often on two pianos tuned a quarter-tone apart; a *Manual of Quarter-Tone Harmony*), **Julián Carrillo** ("Sonido 13," custom pianos in 24-, 48-, 72-, and 96-EDO), and **Alois Hába** (quarter- and sixth-tone works, including a quarter-tone opera). **Georg Friedrich Haas** is the contemporary spectral-microtonal hybrid: he approximates overtone spectra with quarter- and sixth-tones and fuses Wyschnegradsky's ultrachromaticism, Webern, the French spectralists, and American just-intonation experimentalists (Partch, Young, Tenney) — *in vain* (2000) is the emblematic work (Wikipedia; Columbia bio). The compositional lesson: a microtonal system is a pitch *grammar*, not just a finer grid, and every one of these composers had to supply an anchor — just-intonation consonance (Partch, Johnson), a symmetric division (Wyschnegradsky), or a spectral model (Haas) — to keep the unfamiliar pitches parseable.

## After minimalism (compact)

- **Post-minimalism and totalism** kept the pulse and tonal surface but broke the strict frame ([minimalism-and-process-music](minimalism-and-process-music.md) has the lineage). John Adams adds Romantic harmony and orchestral drama to a minimalist "harmonic chug" (*Harmonielehre*, *Short Ride in a Fast Machine*); Louis Andriessen's *De Staat* (1976) and *Hoketus* (1977) drive loud, amplified ensembles with medieval **hocket** (a single line split between interlocking voices) — a Dutch, hard-edged branch (Andriessen sources; [texture-and-density](texture-and-density.md) covers hocket as interlock).
- **Holy minimalism** — Pärt, Górecki, Tavener — pairs slow, consonant, chant-rooted harmony with spiritual restraint (Górecki's Third Symphony, 1976; Tavener's *The Protecting Veil*). Pärt's tintinnabuli *mechanics* are an explicit two-voice algorithm covered in [minimalism-and-process-music](minimalism-and-process-music.md) and [style-invention-and-style-space](style-invention-and-style-space.md); it is the highest quality-per-line-of-code style the wiki knows.
- **New Complexity** (Ferneyhough, Redgate) makes *notational saturation* — nested tuplets, extreme dynamic and articulation detail — an aesthetic; its defenders frame the unplayably-precise page as "a dialogue" that yields a specific fraught, unstable energy rather than literally-audible structure, which is precisely the listener-perception question serialism raises in a new key (Duncan 2010).
- **Drone / duration school** — La Monte Young and Éliane Radigue (slow-morphing ARP 2500 drones of sustained tones with fine beating) — sits at the opposite pole from complexity and connects directly to [ambient-and-generative-genre](ambient-and-generative-genre.md).
- **Polystylism** (Schnittke, term coined 1971) juxtaposes or diffuses incompatible styles — tonal quotation against atonal chaos (*Concerto Grosso No. 1*, *Symphony No. 1*); as deliberate stylistic collision it is the concert-music sibling of [hybridization-and-fusion](hybridization-and-fusion.md).
- **Electroacoustic gesture** — musique concrète's vocabulary of recorded/transformed sound objects (Schaeffer's *objet sonore*; Smalley's spectromorphology of onset–continuant–termination gestures) supplies a **timbral form** language — attack, growth, decay, and morphing as the events — that a synthesis engine can borrow even without concrete sources ([electronic-and-dance](electronic-and-dance.md)).

## Implications for generative engines

The through-line is a single test taken from serialism's reception: **every original-style rule should have an audible correlate.** Structure the listener cannot decode reads as arbitrary — the naive-generativity failure ([generative-music-failure-modes](generative-music-failure-modes.md)), and the same lesson Reich drew ("in serial music, the series itself is seldom audible") and Lerdahl formalized ([style-invention-and-style-space](style-invention-and-style-space.md)). Concretely, which techniques transfer:

- **Spectral chord derivation from synthesized partials — uniquely feasible here.** The engine authored its voices, so it knows their partials exactly; no analysis needed. A sketch: (1) pick a *model* voice with fundamental f0; enumerate its strongest partials — `k·f0` for a harmonic patch, or the actual partial list for an FM/bell patch. (2) Select a subset (say 4–8), quantize each to the current pitch grid (12-TET or a microtonal table), and *orchestrate*: assign one synth voice per chosen partial, setting that voice's gain from the partial's amplitude (Grisey's weak-fourth-partial move). (3) For **combination-tone / ring-mod harmony**, given two fundamentals f1, f2, add the strongest of the intermodulation set `a·f1 ± b·f2` (difference and sum tones first) as chord tones — the harmony is literally the interaction spectrum of the two voices. (4) Expose a **harmonicity→inharmonicity dial**: warp partials as `f0·k^stretch` (or `f0·(k+drift)`), stretch≈1 giving fused/consonant, larger giving bell-like/tense — one continuous, psychoacoustically-grounded vertical-tension control that needs no learned tonal syntax, scored against the roughness model in [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md). (5) Make **process the form**: interpolate one derived spectrum into another, or walk the harmonicity dial, slowly across minutes so the transformation is the event ([tension-and-release](tension-and-release.md), [form-and-structure](form-and-structure.md)). This is the one contemporary-music technique the project can execute *more* natively than an acoustic composer.
- **Micropolyphony as a cheap texture generator.** N canonic voices (≈8–20) playing one slow stepwise line at staggered entries, each slightly detuned and at a marginally different rate, confined to a narrow moving band, give a rich evolving cloud from almost no material — high richness per line of code. Parameterize as a mode of the texture vector ([texture-and-density](texture-and-density.md)); its open question is whether cheap synthesis reads as *cloud* rather than *mud*.
- **Mobile form as seeded module ordering.** Author M self-contained modules with compatible boundaries; the seed fixes an order and repeat counts. Deterministic per URL, variable across seeds — Klavierstück XI made reproducible, and a natural fit for the book-of-screens state-path model in [meta-composition-and-style-machines](meta-composition-and-style-machines.md).
- **Limited aleatorism as seeded per-voice freedom.** Within a coordinated harmonic/temporal frame, let each voice draw its own rhythm and entry from a per-voice seeded RNG (*ad libitum*), re-synchronizing at section boundaries — Lutosławski's coordinated freedom, producing organic non-lockstep ensembles without a counterpoint solver.
- **Serial and set-based operations as an original pitch alphabet.** Row/set transforms (transpose, invert) and Xenakis **sieves** ([meta-composition-and-style-machines](meta-composition-and-style-machines.md)) give internally-consistent, non-default pitch/onset vocabularies for invented styles ([style-invention-and-style-space](style-invention-and-style-space.md)) — but adopt only transforms with an audible correlate (inversion and transposition are hearable as contour/height; retrograde is not), and teach the alphabet within the piece by over-representing its characteristic intervals early.
- **Spend the novelty budget, hold the anchors.** Post-war history is a controlled experiment in how far each axis can move before listeners bounce off; the styles that cohered (spectralism's consonance anchor, micropolyphony's stepwise diatonic bands, the drone school's sustained fusion) moved a few axes and anchored the rest on near-universals ([musical-universals](musical-universals.md), [complexity-and-preference](complexity-and-preference.md)).

## Open questions

- Does the spectral chord-derivation sketch actually produce *audibly* richer or more coherent harmony than a hand-tuned voicing table, blind, in a listening test — or is its appeal conceptual? (Cheap A/B once the harness exists.)
- Can naive listeners hear synthesized micropolyphony as an evolving mass rather than mud, and how many voices / how narrow a band is the threshold? (Shared with [texture-and-density](texture-and-density.md).)
- How much of spectralism's effect is the derived *pitch* content versus the microtonal tuning and the slow process time — i.e., does 12-TET-quantized spectral harmony keep the character? (Bears on whether the engine needs microtonal output for this style.)
- Is there any post-war concert technique whose *inaudible* structure nonetheless improves listener ratings (a hidden-order benefit), or does the audibility test hold without exception?
