# Sub-Saharan African musics

This page covers the major sub-Saharan systems that [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md) does not: that page documents Ewe/Yoruba/Akan/Mande timeline-ensemble theory (the asymmetric bell pattern, 3:2 cross-rhythm, fixed-ostinati-plus-lead architecture) in depth and is the place to read first. Here: the interlocking lamellophone and harp/xylophone repertoires of Zimbabwe and the Mande world, Central African ensemble polyphony as analyzed by Simha Arom, the cyclic-harmony popular musics of southern Africa, and the guitar- and horn-band styles that carried West and Central African idioms into twentieth- and twenty-first-century popular music. Each tradition below is a distinct, named, owned repertoire, not a sample of one undifferentiated "African music" — the Agawu-style caution against exoticizing and homogenizing that closes west-african-rhythm.md applies with equal force to "Pygmy music," "township jive," and every other label used here; they are retained because they are the terms the cited scholarship uses, not because they are neutral.

## Shona mbira: interlocking pairs and a rotating dyad cycle (Zimbabwe)

The *mbira dzavadzimu* is a 22–28-key lamellophone played with both thumbs and one forefinger, central to the all-night *bira* ceremony that summons ancestral spirits (Berliner 1978). [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md) already covers its **kushaura** ("to lead") / **kutsinhira** ("to follow") interlocking pair and the inherent melodies that result; this section adds the harmonic architecture underneath, which that page names as an open gap.

Classical mbira repertoire runs on a 48-pulse cycle organized as four 12-pulse phrases — a 4×12 structure consistent with the 12-pulse ternary framing already established in west-african-rhythm.md. Andrew Tracey's analysis ("The System of the Mbira," 1982) finds that roughly three-quarters of the classical repertoire shares one harmonic template: each of the four phrases states three sonorities (one about every four pulses), and the phrases relate by a one-step rotation — in Tracey's own numbering of the instrument's keys, phrase 1 states positions 1-3-5; phrase 2 raises the third position by a step (5→6, giving 1-3-6); phrase 3 raises the middle position (3→4, giving 1-4-6); phrase 4 raises the first position (1→2, giving 2-4-6); the cycle then drops back to 1-3-5 to repeat, so every phrase has the same "rising" shape and the cycle has no fixed beginning. Mark Scherzinger's independent analysis of one such piece (*Nhemamusasa*) reaches a compatible conclusion from a different angle: mbira "harmonic pitch spaces... though grounded in fifths and thirds, are not defined by tonal criteria... but by symmetric and near-symmetric dyad cycles" — a real harmonic system, but not a functional-tonal one, and not one to force into a I–IV–V hearing.

A pair of **hosho** (gourd rattles filled with seeds) keeps the pulse, typically accenting an off-beat position within the ternary subdivision; despite reading as accompaniment to outsiders, mbira players themselves often treat the hosho as the ensemble's real timekeeping authority. Around this fixed cyclic frame, players build extensive named variation and fingering vocabularies — Berliner's transcriptions catalogue dozens, e.g. *musaku* for a varied restatement of a part — concentrated especially in the high, melodically exposed line. A full taxonomy of these high-line variation techniques could not be independently verified from secondary sources in this research pass and is flagged below rather than asserted.

**Chimurenga** ("struggle") music, pioneered by Thomas Mapfumo with guitarist Jonah Sithole from the late 1970s, transposes mbira interlocking patterns directly onto electric guitar (and the hosho pulse onto hi-hat and drum kit), turning a spirit-possession repertoire into an explicitly political popular-music idiom during and after Zimbabwe's liberation war.

## Mande jali tradition: kumbengo versus birimintingo (Mali, Guinea, Gambia, Senegal)

The *jali* (griot) caste of Mande-speaking West Africa (Mandinka, Maninka, and related peoples) are hereditary musician-historians; Eric Charry's *Mande Music* (2000) is the field's anchor synthesis, covering *jaliya* (griot musicianship) alongside hunters' music, jembe drumming, and modern guitar bands.

Their two signature instruments are the **kora** (a 21-string bridge-harp built from a halved calabash and cowhide, played with two thumbs and two forefingers — eleven strings to the left hand, ten to the right — and associated with jali families across Mali, Guinea, Guinea-Bissau, the Gambia, and Senegal) and the **balafon** (a 17–21-key gourd-resonated xylophone, tuned pentatonic, tetratonic, or heptatonic by region, its gourds fitted with a buzzing membrane — traditionally spider-egg-sac silk — in the same "designed buzz" family as the mbira's bottle caps; see [timbre-and-orchestration](timbre-and-orchestration.md)).

Performance runs on a **two-mode texture** directly usable by a generative engine: **kumbengo** is a cyclic ostinato — a fused rhythmic/harmonic/melodic accompaniment pattern, repeated with light variation and accent shifts — while **birimintingo** is the contrasting mode, virtuosic improvised runs and ornaments that depart from that same ostinato. A player alternates between the two, typically dropping to a spare kumbengo under sung passages and expanding into birimintingo when the voice pauses. Vocal material has an analogous two-part structure: the **donkilo** is the song's fixed melodic skeleton, usually two or three phrases; **sataro** is loosely rhythmicized, speech-like declamation — praise, genealogy, commentary — delivered over a simplified kumbengo. Both pairs are the same accompaniment-versus-foreground polarity in different media.

## Central African polyphony: ostinato-with-variations and extreme hocket (Aka and Banda-Linda, Central African Republic)

Simha Arom's fieldwork from the 1960s–1990s with the Aka (a forest people of the Central African Republic and neighboring Congo, historically lumped with other communities under the now-contested umbrella term "Pygmy") and with the Banda-Linda and Dakpa peoples produced *African Polyphony and Polyrhythm: Musical Structure and Methodology* (1991; French original 1985), this section's anchor source. Gerhard Kubik's *Theory of African Music* vol. II, which documents Central African Republic harp music, is the region's other major theoretical treatment. Arom's method — a "re-recording" or playback technique developed from 1976 — isolates individual parts on multitrack tape and plays reconstructions back to the musicians for correction, letting him derive an implicit structural model that no single performer states outright, since real performances are always elaborated.

That model rests on analytical concepts that double as generative specifications. Central African ensemble music is organized around a strict **period** — a cycle length shared exactly by every part at once (isoperiodicity), even though the parts differ in what they do within it — built from a **minimal realization** (the reducible core pattern a part elaborates) subjected to continuous, license-bounded **variation**. Arom summarizes the resulting texture as **"ostinato with variations"**: cyclical the way a Baroque passacaglia is cyclical, but with variation happening in every voice at once rather than confined to a soloist. (Arom's full formal definitions of period, minimal realization, and model sit behind a paywall in the Cambridge edition; the account here is reconstructed from abstracts and secondary summaries and should be checked against the primary chapters before an engine spec leans on the exact formalism.)

Aka vocal polyphony realizes this with up to four interlocking voice parts and no fixed harmonic bass, combining **hocket** with **yodel** (Aka *dìyèí*/*dìyèngè*) — rapid alternation between a lower "big voice" and a falsetto "little voice" register — into a dense, constantly shifting contrapuntal texture that UNESCO has recognized as intangible cultural heritage; Arom judged its polyphonic complexity comparable to fourteenth-century Europe's. Arom defines **hocket** itself as "the interweaving, overlapping, and interlocking of several rhythmic figures located on different pitch levels in a specific scalar system," and the Banda-Linda and Dakpa horn ensembles push that definition to its logical extreme: five to eighteen players, each blowing a single-pitch horn (traditionally a length of wood hollowed by termites) or single-note flute, so that no individual plays anything but one repeated pitch and the melody exists only in the composite. It is the most literal hocket generator in this survey — one voice, one pitch, assembled entirely in performance.

## Southern Africa: cyclic harmony under bass-forward dance grooves

South African urban music built its own cyclic-harmony vocabulary independently, out of shebeen keyboard playing rather than drum ensembles. **Marabi** crystallized in Johannesburg's slumyards and shebeens from the 1920s: unschooled keyboardists (initially on cheap pedal organs) vamped a repeating four-chord cycle — commonly described as **I–IV–I6/4–V**, one chord per measure — under improvised right-hand melody, borrowing the harmonic materials of American ragtime, blues, and early jazz but the cyclic, endlessly repeatable *logic* of indigenous music; the point, per David Coplan's *In Township Tonight!* (1985) and Christopher Ballantine's *Marabi Nights* (1993/2012), was a groove dancers could enter without learning a song. **Mbaqanga** and **township jive** (1950s onward) continue the same cyclic-vamp logic — most simply described as **I–IV–V** with dominant sevenths — realized by an electric dance-band lineup (lead/rhythm guitar, bass, drum kit, saxophone) over indigenous dance rhythms such as Zulu *indlamu*; Mahlathini and the Mahotella Queens, backed by session groups such as the Makgona Tsohle Band, are the style's best-known exponents. The result across both styles is bass-forward and repetition-tolerant in the way [groove-and-embodiment](groove-and-embodiment.md)'s research on backbeat and low-frequency weight predicts.

Two related traditions are noted only briefly. **Isicathamiya** — soft, close-harmony Zulu a cappella descended from the louder *mbube* style, popularized internationally by Ladysmith Black Mambazo — is vocal and thus outside this project's instrumental scope; it is recorded here only for completeness. **Maskanda**, Zulu guitar music codified by John Bhengu ("Phuzushukela") from the 1960s, pairs a free-tempo introductory flourish (*izihlabo*) with a strict-tempo song built on independent-finger picking — the thumb running a fast, syncopated bass ostinato while the index finger picks a counter-melody above it — under declaimed praise-poetry (*izibongo*); that thumb/finger independence is itself a compact interlock pattern within a single instrument.

## The great popular styles as engine-ready grooves

### Highlife (Ghana, Nigeria)

Highlife grew from coastal Ghana's mix of local Akan/Ewe/Ga musical practice, palm-wine guitar style, and colonial-era brass bands from the 1920s onward, splitting by the 1940s into horn-driven **dance-band highlife** (E.T. Mensah) and smaller **guitar-band highlife** (E.K. Nyame and the Akan Trio, later the African Brothers Band), which by the 1960s–70s had largely replaced horns with **crisscrossing interlocking guitars**. The bell/key pattern documented in west-african-rhythm.md as the "standard pattern" is, per multiple sources, indigenous to Ghana and audible throughout highlife guitar parts — the same timeline machinery, realized in plucked strings instead of an iron bell.

### Afrobeat (Nigeria)

Fela Kuti fused highlife, Yoruba music, jazz, and James Brown-era funk into Afrobeat from the late 1960s with his bands Africa '70 and Egypt 80, based for years at Lagos's Afrika Shrine. The style runs on **one- or two-chord modal vamps** (closer to modal jazz and JB-style funk than to functional harmony) carried by up to three interlocking guitars — rhythm, lead, and a "tenor" guitar Fela added, a single-note staccato middle-register voice playing contrapuntally between bass and rhythm guitar — plus a horn section that riffs or answers Kuti's vocals in call-and-response, propelled by Tony Allen's loose, highly syncopated drumming. Songs commonly run 10–30 minutes, opening with an extended instrumental section built entirely from these interlocking vamps before the voice enters — a long-form arc closer to this project's [form-and-structure](form-and-structure.md) process/arch forms than to a pop verse-chorus; see [funk-soul-and-rnb](funk-soul-and-rnb.md) for the American funk half of the fusion.

### Congolese rumba and soukous (DRC/Congo)

Congolese rumba grew out of the Bakongo dance *maringa* and a 1940s–50s wave of Cuban son records (Sexteto Habanero, Trio Matamoros) that Léopoldville radio and record shops mislabeled "rumba"; Congolese musicians transposed Cuban guitar and horn textures onto local Lingala melody and rhythm — bandleader Franco Luambo's own summary was "the melody follows the tonality of Lingala, the guitar parts are African" — a fusion story detailed further in [hybridization-and-fusion](hybridization-and-fusion.md). By the 1970s the style's climactic instrumental section, the **sebene**, had crystallized into a three-guitar lattice: **solo** (lead melody, highest register), **mi-solo** ("half-solo," a bridging arpeggiated middle voice — the same structural slot as Afrobeat's tenor guitar), and **rhythm** (lowest, holding the cyclical vamp, commonly described as I–IV–V–IV), interlocking in call-and-response over a driving bass and the drummer's syncopated *cavacha* pattern. **Soukous** (1960s–70s) is the faster, guitar-forward descendant of this same architecture.

### Ethio-jazz (Ethiopia)

Mulatu Astatke, trained in the UK and at Berklee, fused Ethiopia's **qenet** (or *kiñit*) system — a small set of named pentatonic modes (*tizita*, *bati*, *ambassel*, *anchihoye*, and others), classified by ethnomusicologists Ashenafi Kebede and Cynthia Tse Kimberlin from the 1970s — with jazz harmony, instrumentation (vibraphone, electric piano, horns), and improvisational practice from the 1960s onward, producing a modal-melody-over-jazz-harmony hybrid; see [hybridization-and-fusion](hybridization-and-fusion.md) for more on Astatke's specific fusion technique.

### Taarab (one line)

**Taarab**, the Swahili-coast (Zanzibar-centered) genre popularized by Siti binti Saad from the 1920s, sets Swahili and Arabic text to an Arabic-, Indian-, and Great-Lakes-influenced orchestral sound; its melodic material belongs with the Arabic maqam material in [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md) rather than with the rhythm-forward traditions above.

### Contemporary: Afrobeats and amapiano

Two 2010s-into-2020s popular styles carry these lineages forward and are worth naming as engine targets, with an explicit recency caveat: both are still documented mainly by production-tutorial and music-journalism sources rather than peer-reviewed scholarship, so treat specifics as current practice, not settled history. **Afrobeats** (Nigeria/Ghana/UK diaspora — an umbrella term distinct from "Afrobeat" above) layers a 4/4 kick-and-snare "bounce" informally described against a 3-2/2-3 clave feel; one production-guide description places kicks around beats 1, 2.5, and 4 and snare/rimshot around 1.75 and 3.5, under syncopated vocal delivery. **Amapiano** (South African, Gauteng townships, mid-2010s) fuses kwaito, deep house, and jazz at roughly 108–114 BPM around its defining **log drum**: a pitched, portamento-capable low percussive tone, distinct from a fixed kick, that production sources describe as dancing around the beat rather than simply anchoring it.

## Implications for generative engines

1. **A general interlock-pattern family.** Generalize kushaura/kutsinhira, kora kumbengo, and the sebene's three-guitar lattice into one primitive: N voices with mutually exclusive (or near-exclusive) onset grids whose union realizes a target composite line or register span. This extends the two-voice "interlock generator" already named in west-african-rhythm.md to N voices and to pitched (not just percussive) material — the sebene's solo/mi-solo/rhythm split and Afrobeat's rhythm/tenor/lead guitars are both 3-voice instances worth implementing as named presets.
2. **Cyclic, non-functional harmony as an alternative ground.** Implement the mbira's rotating dyad/triad cycle (4 phrases × 3 sonorities, one-step-per-phrase rotation) as a harmony generator with no cadence — a spiral that returns rather than resolves — for engines that want cyclic groove-harmony instead of the functional progressions in [harmony](harmony.md). Marabi/mbaqanga's I–IV–I6/4–V (or I–IV–V) is the same idea in triadic, Western-tinged dress; presenting both side by side lets an engine expose a single "vamp cycle" abstraction with a tonal-functional-versus-symmetric-cyclic dial.
3. **Ostinato-versus-run as a lead-voice state machine.** Kumbengo/birimintingo is a clean two-state toggle — hold the accompaniment pattern (low information) or launch a virtuosic run (high information) — naturally gated by whether another voice (a singer, a call) is active. This lead-behavior primitive is reusable across genres, and pairs with the ostinato/lead-drum architecture already in west-african-rhythm.md and [percussion-music](percussion-music.md).
4. **Hocket as a texture generator, including its limit case.** Implement hocket as: assign each of N voices a subset of a target melody's pitches, down to exactly one pitch class per voice (the Banda-Linda limit case), silence each voice elsewhere, and treat ensemble size N as a tunable density/texture parameter — from a simple 2-voice interlock up to a maximal N-voice hocket cloud.
5. **Isoperiodicity as a cheap coherence constraint.** Arom's finding that every part in a Central African texture shares one exact cycle length even while varying freely inside it is directly implementable: fix one period per phrase-group (tying into this project's own [phrase-structure](phrase-structure.md) / [form-and-structure](form-and-structure.md) cycle architecture), then let each voice run an independent minimal-model-plus-variation generator inside that shared frame — coherence without a conductor or shared score.
6. **Idiom transfer as a generative operation.** Chimurenga's mbira-to-guitar transposition and Ethio-jazz's qenet-to-jazz-harmony fusion are both instances of one operation: take a pattern idiomatic to one instrument/tradition and re-render it under a different instrument's constraints (register, decay, polyphony limit). Worth implementing as an explicit "reidiomize" pass when an engine switches instrument packs, rather than only ever generating fresh material per pack.
7. **Genre presets as parameter packs, not separate codepaths.** Highlife, Afrobeat, soukous, Afrobeats, and amapiano each reduce to a tight bundle of defaults (tempo, vamp length and chord count, guitar/voice count, hocket density, log-drum-style bass behavior) layered on the primitives above — consistent with [style-and-genre-overview](style-and-genre-overview.md)'s "style as constraint pack" framing, and cheaper to maintain than bespoke generators per style.

## Open questions

- Berliner's full Shona vocabulary for mbira variation technique: this pass found *musaku* and related terms via secondary sources but could not independently verify a specific term for high-line variation encountered in preliminary notes. Needs a pass with direct access to *The Soul of Mbira* itself.
- Arom's technical definitions of "period," "minimal realization," and "model" are given here from abstracts and secondary summaries because the primary chapters of *African Polyphony and Polyrhythm* are paywalled on Cambridge Core — worth a follow-up pass with full-text access before an engine spec leans hard on the exact formalism.
- Marabi sources describe the vamp as I–IV–I6/4–V while mbaqanga/township-jive sources shorten it to I–IV–V; whether this is a real simplification over time or citation looseness across secondary sources is unresolved.
- Afrobeats and amapiano are contemporary, fast-moving, and thinly covered by scholarship as of this writing (2026); the production-technique claims here should be revisited as academic treatment catches up, and checked against direct audio analysis once this project starts building engines around them.
