# Funk, soul, and R&B

Funk is arguably the most fully engineered groove system in popular music: an ensemble discipline in which harmony is deliberately starved of movement so that rhythm — a lattice of short, complementary, per-instrument cells — can carry all the interest. Soul and R&B are its harmonic counterweight and shared root: they carry the vocabulary of the Black Protestant church (extended chords, plagal motion, call-and-response, the Hammond organ) into secular popular song, and funk itself grew directly out of that same soul/R&B lineage in James Brown's mid-1960s bands. Motown, Stax, and Philadelphia International each built a distinct production system on the soul side; funk itself split into recognizably different families (James Brown/the Meters, Parliament-Funkadelic, Sly Stone's proto-disco turn); and the whole complex resurfaces, restructured, in neo-soul and contemporary R&B. This page reads all of it for engine parts: onset grids, a chord palette, and named microtiming presets, building on the syncopation/backbeat/microtiming evidence in [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md) and the engine this project has already shipped, [findings-groove-lofi-engine.md](findings-groove-lofi-engine.md).

## "The One" and the interlocking cell: funk's rhythmic engine

James Brown's own formulation of funk's discipline, widely quoted from a 1988 interview: "Funk is coming down on the one. If it's on the one, then it's funky." He drilled this by cueing his band verbally mid-song ("On the one!"), shifting the ensemble's structural anchor onto beat 1 — not instead of the backbeat (snare still hits 2 and 4, per [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md)'s backbeat treatment) but underneath and around it, as the point every part's syncopation is measured against and the point section changes land. Historians commonly date this approach's emergence to 1964's "Out of Sight" and its consolidation on 1965's "Papa's Got a Brand New Bag," and Alexander Stewart's scholarly account adds the missing rhythmic-history link: American popular rhythm's underlying feel shifted in this period from a shuffled, triplet (12/8-derived) subdivision toward *even* eighth and sixteenth notes, a transition New Orleans R&B pioneered on record and Brown's bands then exploited — even eighths subdivide cleanly into the sixteenth-note grid that everything below depends on.

That grid lets a funk band do something structurally unusual: treat **every instrument as a short, cyclical, incomplete rhythmic cell** — a drum part, in effect — that only becomes music in combination with the others. Rickey Vincent's history of the genre frames funk arrangements as literally bottom-up: rhythm section built "first drums, then bass, then the rest." Anne Danielsen's *Presence and Pleasure: The Funk Grooves of James Brown and Parliament* (2006) — the reference musicological analysis of this repertoire — reads Brown's and Clinton's grooves exactly this way: a stack of short, looped figures in tight rhythmic counterpoint, none of which states a "tune"; the identity lives in the composite relationship between parts, and (per the microrhythm research her group has since built on this base, already cited in [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md)) in the *sound* of each part's timing, not only its onset grid. David Brackett's close reading of "Superbad" (1970) is the sharpest single demonstration of the payoff: the record's meaning and force ride on "the driving rhythm section and subtle emphases in the beat" more than on harmony, words, or melody in any conventional sense. The deep lineage of this ensemble logic — interlocking ostinato cells, call-and-response, prized timbral "dirt" over purity — is documented as continuous with West/Sub-Saharan African ensemble practice by Portia Maultsby and others (see [west-african-rhythm.md](west-african-rhythm.md) and, when written, [sub-saharan-african-musics](sub-saharan-african-musics.md)); funk is that logic run through electric instruments and a drum kit.

### Ghost-note guitar: chank and chicken-scratch

James Brown's guitarist Jimmy Nolen originated funk's rhythm-guitar language: tightly muted strings, picked close to the bridge in continuous sixteenth notes, alternating fully muted percussive scratches — guitar-pedagogy sources label the isolated hit a "chika" — with brief fretted-chord "chank" accents (the blend of muted and ringing notes); Wikipedia's "Chanking" entry traces the technique to Nolen's 1964 "Out of Sight" (a "double-chank" landing on the bar's first backbeat) and its full flowering on 1965's "Papa's Got a Brand New Bag." The muted scratches are ghost notes in [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md)'s sense — felt more than heard, functioning as a percussion voice — while the chank accents state the harmony exactly on the backbeat the drums and bass also mark. A representative cell (idiomatic pattern generalized from multiple guitar-pedagogy sources, not one specific transcription; lower-case = muted scratch, upper-case = chank accent):

```
16th:    1  e  &  a | 2  e  &  a | 3  e  &  a | 4  e  &  a
guitar:  x  x  x  x | X  x  x  x | x  x  x  x | X  x  x  x
```

### Bass: fingerstyle syncopation and slap

Two documented funk/soul bass traditions, both prioritizing rhythmic cells over sustained lines: **fingerstyle syncopation**, epitomized by Motown's James Jamerson (detailed under Motown below) — chromatic passing tones, ghost notes, and a melodic line that plays *against* a simple drum part rather than doubling it; and **slap bass**, originated by Larry Graham in Sly and the Family Stone (reportedly developed to compensate for a missing drummer at a gig) as alternating thumb-slaps on the low string with finger-"pops" on upper strings, and elaborated by Bootsy Collins (with James Brown's J.B.'s, then Parliament-Funkadelic) into a faster, effects-laden "staccato funk" — thumb striking the side of the string for a thicker fundamental, popped upper notes for treble snap, often through an envelope filter. An illustrative slap cell (a common teaching pattern, not a specific recording's transcription; T = thumb slap, P = finger pop):

```
16th:    1  e  &  a | 2  e  &  a | 3  e  &  a | 4  e  &  a
bass:    T  .  .  P | .  .  T  . | T  .  .  P | .  .  T  .
```

Both traditions keep the bass in a low, weighty register (roughly E1–G2 on a 4-string in standard tuning) consistent with [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md)'s low-frequency-timing findings — the slap "pop," notably, pushes a secondary rhythmic voice up into the mid register while the thumb holds the low anchor, letting one instrument cover two of the interlock's rhythmic roles at once.

### Drums: displaced snare accents and the Funky Drummer

Funk drumming keeps the kick as the tightest, simplest voice — often just beat 1 plus one syncopated hit — while pushing complexity into the snare (accented backbeat plus quiet ghost notes on weak sixteenths) and a continuous sixteenth-note hi-hat. Clyde Stubblefield's break on James Brown's "Funky Drummer" (1970) is the most sampled and most analyzed drum pattern in popular music (Alexander Stewart's *Popular Music* article and multiple drum-pedagogy transcriptions concur on its skeleton); the commonly taught version:

```
16th:    1  e  &  a | 2  e  &  a | 3  e  &  a | 4  e  &  a
hi-hat:  x  x  x  x | x  o  x  x | x  x  x  x | x  o  x  x
kick:    X  .  .  . | .  .  .  . | .  .  .  . | .  X  .  .
snare:   .  .  .  . | X  .  .  . | .  .  .  g | X  .  .  .
```
(X = accent, x = normal hi-hat sixteenth, o = open hi-hat accent on the "e" of beats 2 and 4, g = ghost note — the documented hardest one, on the "a" of beat 3, landing immediately before the beat-4 accent). Ethan Hein's transcription-in-progress notes the real recording deviates from any such grid — Stubblefield lays material behind the beat and swings lightly — and warns that quantizing it "won't be totally unmusical, but it won't have nearly the same power," an independent, funk-specific confirmation of [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md)'s structured-microtiming synthesis: the grid is necessary but the (small, consistent, per-instrument) deviation from it is where the feel lives.

### Horn stabs as punctuation

Brown's horn section (the J.B.'s: at various points Maceo Parker and Pee Wee Ellis on saxophones, Fred Wesley on trombone) functioned as a percussion voice in its own right — short, tightly unisoned "stabs" placed in the gaps the rhythm section left open, punctuating rather than carrying melody, with solo lines (Parker's especially) layering jazz phrasing on top of the riff-based ensemble writing. The same device recurs, more polished, in Motown's and Philly's larger horn/string sections (below): brief, syncopated hits that state a hook without competing with the vocal or the groove's density budget.

### Clavinet: the sixteenth-note lattice

Hohner Clavinet — a struck-string keyboard picked up electromagnetically, bright and percussive — became funk's other rhythm-guitar analog, most famously on Stevie Wonder's "Superstition" (1972): a two-bar riff in E♭ minor pentatonic, deliberately syncopated against the meter rather than aligned with it. Secondary analyses (Ethan Hein; a "dissected" note-by-note breakdown) agree on its two sharpest syncopation points: the line's flat-seventh lands a sixteenth note *early*, on the "a" of beat 3 rather than on beat 4 itself, and the riff resolves not on the following downbeat but on the very last sixteenth of the two-bar cell — the weakest possible metric position. That is funk clavinet logic in miniature: state the riff's identity at exactly the positions a straight, on-the-beat reading would leave silent. (The record layers two clavinet parts panned hard left/right for a thicker composite — an early example of the interlock principle applied within a single instrumental voice.)

## Funk harmony: stasis is the point

Funk vamps famously sit on one chord — sometimes two, in a slow harmonic shuttle — for a few minutes or an entire track, the inverse of functional harmony's mandate to keep moving toward a cadence (see the T→S→D grammar in [harmony.md](harmony.md)). This is the same trade this wiki has already flagged for modal jazz's "static mode, slow harmonic clock, groove and phrasing carry the interest" ([jazz-and-improvisation.md](jazz-and-improvisation.md)) and for drone-based and minimalist genres generally: when harmonic motion is suppressed, rhythmic and textural detail must — and, per [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md)'s whole toolkit of syncopation, velocity hierarchy, and ghost notes, *can* — carry the piece's interest. Funk supplies the sharpest evidence for the trade: Danielsen's and Vincent's accounts above describe bands spending their entire compositional effort on the interlock of five or six rhythmic cells sitting under a single chord.

The chord that vamp most often sits on is a heavily colored dominant: dominant ninths, dominant seventh-sharp-ninths (♯9), minor elevenths, minor sixths, and six-nine chords all recur as funk's harmonic palette. The ♯9 in particular — sometimes called the "Hendrix chord" for its rock-context popularization on "Purple Haze," but a funk and disco tonic sonority in its own right (Heatwave's "Boogie Nights" is a commonly cited disco-era example) — earns its "funky" reputation, per music-theory writer Stuart Isacoff, from the deliberate tension between the chord's major third and its enharmonic minor third (the ♯9); James Brown used plain dominant ninths just as constantly (e.g., "Papa's Got a Brand New Bag"). When a vamp does move, the two idiomatic options are a **chromatic planing** of the same voicing up or down a semitone or whole tone (documented in Parliament-Funkadelic's practice) rather than functional root motion, or a brief lean to **IV** — the gospel-descended plagal motion soul shares with funk's church-rooted harmonic ear, covered next.

## Soul: gospel's harmonic language, secularized

Soul music is what happens when gospel's harmonic and vocal vocabulary — extended chords, plagal (IV–I, "Amen") motion, a leader-and-chorus call-and-response texture, the Hammond B3 as the church's signature instrument — is set to secular lyrics and a backbeat. Braxton Shelley's *Analyzing Gospel* (2019), the leading recent music-theoretic treatment, centers the **vamp**: a repeating, escalating musical cycle he calls a "ritual technology" through which a congregation "experience[s] with their bodies what they believe in their hearts" — the same stasis-plus-intensification structure funk borrowed for secular ends, arriving by a different, church-first route. The Hammond organ moved from Black sanctified and holiness churches into R&B and soul directly through musicians like Bill Doggett, and by the mid-1950s into "soul jazz" organ trios, carrying its churchy full-drawbar registration ("gospel full" in the drawbar preset table of [synthesis-recipes.md](synthesis-recipes.md)) with it. Plagal motion surfaces constantly: Al Green's "Take Me to the River" (1974) stacks *quadruple* plagal cadences in its prechorus, and I–IV chord alternation is a stock soul-verse pattern (commonly charted, e.g., for Sam & Dave's "Soul Man" — treat that specific attribution as pedagogical-source-level, not peer-reviewed). Slow soul ballads frequently carry this into a 12/8 (compound quadruple) meter — four beats each subdivided in three — the same meter doo-wop and blues ballads use for their triplet "sway"; treat the specific 6/8-feel claim as informed characterization from production/pedagogy sources rather than a measured finding.

### Motown's system

Motown's house band, the **Funk Brothers** (Benny Benjamin and later Richard "Pistol" Allen on drums, James Jamerson on bass, Earl Van Dyke on keys, a rotating guitar/horn/percussion pool including tambourinist Jack Ashford), played on nearly every Motown single from 1959–1972. Its defining device is **James Jamerson's bass**: chromatic passing tones, ghost notes, inversions, and open strings woven into genuinely melodic, syncopated lines that move *against* a simple drum part rather than doubling it — the documented paradigm case is "I Was Made to Love Her," where Benny Benjamin holds constant quarter notes on the snare while Jamerson's bass supplies all the rhythmic and melodic motion underneath (the transcriptions in Allan Slutsky's *Standing in the Shadows of Motown*, 1989, are the standard reference). Above that, the "Motown backbeat" doubles the snare on 2 and 4 with Ashford's tambourine and a chopped rhythm-guitar chord, all three locked together — confirmed for "You Can't Hurry Love" (1966) — while the kick varies between a lighter, syncopated pattern and, on some sides, a fuller four-on-the-floor (kick on all four quarters):

```
16th:        1  e  &  a | 2  e  &  a | 3  e  &  a | 4  e  &  a
kick:        x  .  .  . | .  .  .  . | x  .  .  . | .  .  .  .
snare:       .  .  .  . | X  .  .  . | .  .  .  . | X  .  .  .
tambourine:  .  .  .  . | X  .  .  . | .  .  .  . | X  .  .  .
```
Strings and horns sit on top as a "sweetening" arrangement layer (Motown's Detroit arrangers, later Paul Riser) — orchestral color added to a rhythm-section groove that is already complete without it, a layering principle covered generally in [timbre-and-orchestration.md](timbre-and-orchestration.md).

### Stax and southern soul

Stax's house band, Booker T. & the M.G.'s, built the label's grittier "Memphis sound" around drummer Al Jackson Jr., whose backbeat is consistently described by collaborators as sitting slightly *behind* the beat — guitarist Steve Cropper: "It became our way of life." **Treat this as practitioner testimony, not a measured finding** — it is exactly the kind of claim [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md) flags as contested at the psychoacoustic level (Frühauf et al. found *quantized* timing rated highest and *early* deviations penalized more than late ones), but it is a good illustration of that page's synthesis for a different reason: Stax musicians describe the drag as a small, fixed, consistently-applied offset — a genre signature, not random jitter — which is precisely the *structured* microtiming the research supports, whatever its exact magnitude turns out to be. Stax's other signature device is dense **horn-section ensemble writing**: the Memphis Horns (trumpeter Wayne Jackson, saxophonist Andrew Love) built a "fat," blended two-horn-doing-the-work-of-many sound, gospel-inflected via Love's church background, on hundreds of sides including Otis Redding's and Sam & Dave's.

### Philly soul

Gamble and Huff's Philadelphia International, with house band **MFSB** (Ronnie Baker/Norman Harris/Earl Young at the core) at Sigma Sound, layered lush orchestral strings and horns over funk-descended rhythm tracks — 1974's "TSOP (The Sound of Philadelphia)" is the genre's signature side. Drummer Earl Young's driving, syncopated open/closed hi-hat pattern over a steady kick (on "The Love I Lost," 1973) is widely credited as the direct precursor of the four-on-the-floor-plus-open-hi-hat pulse that would become disco's chassis, making Philly soul the hinge between funk/soul's backbeat-centered rhythm and the family described next.

## The funk families: differences in groove parameters

### James Brown and the Meters: tight, syncopated, spacious

James Brown's own bands sit in a fairly narrow, danceable tempo band — algorithmically detected tempos (hedge: automated BPM tools sometimes mis-detect by a factor of two) cluster roughly 90–120 BPM, e.g. "Cold Sweat" ≈ 112–120, "Sex Machine" ≈ 106–108, "Funky Drummer" ≈ 96 — with the dense sixteenth-note interlock described above and a hard, unwavering One. The Meters, New Orleans' great funk band, share the sixteenth-note grid but spend it differently: drummer Zigaboo Modeliste plays with "second-line" syncopation always implied (a debt to New Orleans parade-beat phrasing), turns beats around, and divides fast composite lines across all four limbs rather than concentrating them in the hands, producing a "quasi-swung" feel — sixteenths that are neither straight nor triplet-swung but somewhere between — over spacious, often two-bar rather than one-bar rhythmic cells, with more silence per cell than Brown's tighter grid.

### Parliament-Funkadelic: slower and heavier

George Clinton's Parliament-Funkadelic collective (Bootsy Collins and later bassists, keyboardist Bernie Worrell, guitarist Eddie Hazel) generally reads as *slower and heavier* than James Brown funk, though this is more a matter of arrangement density than a dramatically different metronome mark: fewer onsets per bar, more low-end sub-bass and synth weight, guitar parts pulling from psychedelic rock rather than chicken-scratch, and vamps stretched over many more bars per chord or arrangement idea before anything changes — reviewers describe Parliament grooves as feeling like they "stretch out forever." The interlock principle survives, but with more space between cells and a correspondingly larger per-note weight.

### Sly and the Family Stone and the disco transition: four-on-the-floor plus sixteenth hats

Sly and the Family Stone (with Larry Graham's newly invented slap bass) pushed funk toward a denser, more mechanically even sixteenth-note surface than Brown's syncopated One-centered feel. Combined with Philly soul's Earl-Young-style kick-on-every-beat pattern, this produced disco's rhythmic chassis: kick on all four quarters, closed hi-hat sixteenths with an open accent on the off-beats, snare or clap doubling the backbeat — a much more metrically *even* groove than funk proper, trading syncopated density for continuous, undifferentiated forward motion (disco tempo commonly cited at 110–130 BPM, faster and steadier than JB-style funk):

```
16th:    1  e  &  a | 2  e  &  a | 3  e  &  a | 4  e  &  a
kick:    X  .  .  . | X  .  .  . | X  .  .  . | X  .  .  .
hi-hat:  x  x  x  o | x  x  x  o | x  x  x  o | x  x  x  o
snare:   .  .  .  . | X  .  .  . | .  .  .  . | X  .  .  .
```

## Contemporary R&B and neo-soul

### The Dilla/drunk feel

J Dilla (James Yancey), producing for Slum Village, D'Angelo, Erykah Badu, and others in the late 1990s–2000s, built a drum-programming style long described as "drunk" or unquantized. Dan Charnas's *Dilla Time* (2022) and independent analysis (Ethan Hein) both insist this reading is wrong: the deviations are "intended and meticulously executed," not sloppy — commonly implemented as a moderate swing (roughly 53–56% on the eighth-note grid, short of a full triplet swing) plus specific elements nudged individually off-grid: hats and snares displaced *late* by small, fixed amounts, repeated **identically every bar**, which is exactly what marks it as a fixed feel-preset rather than noise (per-cycle-varying jazz microtiming, by contrast, differs bar to bar). This is a direct, genre-specific confirmation of [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md)'s central correction — structured, not random, deviation — and maps onto this project's existing swing-as-phase-warp-plus-fixed-offset mechanism in [findings-groove-lofi-engine.md](findings-groove-lofi-engine.md) almost exactly; Dilla feel is a second named preset for the same mechanism, not a different one.

### Neo-soul harmony

Neo-soul and its jazz-adjacent wing (Robert Glasper's trio and collaborators are the commonly cited reference) push soul's already-extended gospel harmony further: tightly clustered "rootless" voicings stacking a 9th, 3rd, and 11th close together rather than spread across a wide register; quartal (fourths-based) voicings borrowed from modal jazz ([jazz-and-improvisation.md](jazz-and-improvisation.md), [harmony.md](harmony.md)) for a floating, less triadic color; minor-ninth chords used as harmonic resting points; and a gospel-reharmonization habit of inserting altered, "dirty" ii–V motion inside otherwise static or slow-moving progressions. Chord *planing* (moving a fixed voicing shape over a stable or slow-moving bass, common over the interlock-cell rhythm section below it) is the connective link back to funk's chromatic-planing vamp practice above. Common-tone voice leading between these dense voicings — [counterpoint-and-voice-leading.md](counterpoint-and-voice-leading.md)'s "shortest distance" principle — keeps the stacks from reading as a run of disconnected clusters.

### Trap-influenced modern R&B drums

Since roughly the 2010s, mainstream R&B production has absorbed trap's drum vocabulary — 808 sub-bass kicks (often pitched and glided rather than fixed), rapid hi-hat rolls/subdivision bursts, and half-time snare placement — layered under R&B's slower harmonic and vocal sensibility ("trap soul"); this is trap-family drum-machine practice, not a funk/soul-descended acoustic-kit idiom, so full treatment belongs with [hip-hop-and-beat-making](hip-hop-and-beat-making.md).

## Implications for generative engines

- **An interlock-cell groove generator.** Generalize [findings-groove-lofi-engine.md](findings-groove-lofi-engine.md)'s kick/snare/hat interlock into N complementary per-instrument 16th-note cells (kick, snare+ghosts, hi-hat, chank guitar, bass, clavinet/horn stabs) under a shared **density budget**: each cell owns a small onset count, and a **mutual-exclusion/answer constraint** keeps cells from stacking on the same strong positions — a new onset should preferentially fill a position the other active cells leave silent (directly implementable as a per-position "claimed" bitmask checked at generation time), reproducing the "every instrument is a drum, together they complete each other" structure Danielsen and Vincent describe.
- **Vamp-form style packs.** A funk/soul/gospel pack is 1–2 chords (or a slow chromatic-planing shuttle) held for the section's full length, with interest supplied entirely by the interlock generator above plus an arrangement **arc**: instrument layers enter/exit (horns in, guitar drops to just chank, everything but the drums cuts for a **break**) rather than the chords changing — reuses the layer-activation-timeline idea already documented for EDM in [electronic-and-dance.md](electronic-and-dance.md) and the style-pack architecture in [generative-music-design-patterns.md](generative-music-design-patterns.md) and [style-and-genre-overview.md](style-and-genre-overview.md).
- **A 7♯9-and-extensions chord palette.** Add dominant 9, dominant 7♯9, minor 11, minor 6, and 6/9 to the shared chord-construction vocabulary in [harmony.md](harmony.md)/[shared-libraries.md](shared-libraries.md), tagged as vamp-tonic-eligible (unlike jazz's ii–V-driven extensions, these need to sound stable held indefinitely, not resolving).
- **A velocity-hierarchy ghost-note grammar as a reusable layer.** The chank guitar, ghost-note snare, and slap-pop patterns above are all instances of one grammar already established in [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md): loud accent on strong/backbeat positions, very quiet (≈10–30 velocity) filler on weak sixteenths. Implement it once, parameterized by instrument (which positions are accent-eligible, how dense the filler is), and reuse it for guitar, snare, and bass rather than writing three bespoke pattern generators.
- **Feel presets as performer microtiming tables.** Extend the existing swing-phase-warp-plus-fixed-per-instrument-offset performer (already built for lo-fi laid-back feel) with named presets drawn directly from this page: "JB tight" (quantized-to-near-quantized, minimal swing, hard One), "Dilla drunk" (~53–56% swing, hats/snare nudged late by a small fixed amount, identical every cycle — not per-cycle-random), and a hedged "Stax behind" (small fixed snare drag, flagged low-confidence per the practitioner-testimony caveat above). This is a data problem, not an architecture problem: the mechanism in [findings-groove-lofi-engine.md](findings-groove-lofi-engine.md) already supports arbitrary named presets.
- **New voices needed.** [synthesis-recipes.md](synthesis-recipes.md) already covers the Hammond drawbar organ (with a "gospel full" registration) and an FM electric piano; it has no **clavinet** recipe. A bright, fast-decay, slightly inharmonic FM or filtered-pulse voice (high modulator ratio, fast index decay, a touch of pickup-style resonance) would be needed before a funk/soul engine could realize the Superstition-style riff layer described above.

## Open questions

- The JB-vs-P-Funk tempo/density comparison above is an informed characterization (arrangement density, not a clean BPM gap); no source in this pass gives a rigorous quantitative comparison — worth a small corpus check (tap tempo + onset density per bar across a sample of each) before hard-coding parameter presets.
- Stax's "behind the beat" magnitude has never been measured in this pass (only practitioner testimony); it is exactly the kind of per-style microtiming template [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md) already flags as missing from the literature.
- How large should the interlock generator's per-cell density budget be, and how strict the mutual-exclusion constraint, before the composite stops sounding like a funk band and starts sounding either too sparse (inert) or too dense (mush)? Untested; a candidate first experiment for this idiom.
- Neo-soul's cluster/quartal voicings and funk's chromatic planing both "float" harmonically — is there a shared, implementable voice-leading rule that covers both, or are they different enough to need separate generators?
