# Blues, country, and roots musics

Blues is the harmonic-melodic bedrock under jazz, rock, R&B, and country alike — a small, closed set of forms and pitch practices, worked out by African American musicians in the American South from roughly the 1890s–1920s, that the rest of American popular music never stopped quoting. This page collects blues form, pitch language, and rhythm; the country-music practices (song form, harmony, grooves, instrumental idioms) that sit downstream of blues and of Anglo-Celtic fiddle music; bluegrass as a fast, virtuosic, self-consciously "modern" 1940s reworking of the same string-band material; and old-time/Appalachian fiddling and gospel as bluegrass's and country's own roots. It is written as a Start-level style family and as a donor system: nearly every device here (the AAB fill, the shuffle, the pedal-steel bend, the solo-rotation form) is directly implementable and reusable well outside these genres. [jazz-and-improvisation.md](jazz-and-improvisation.md) already covers swing-ratio numbers and jazz-blues substitutions; this page links there rather than repeating them.

## Blues form

### The 12-bar scheme and its variants

The basic 12-bar blues is I–I–I–I / IV–IV–I–I / V–IV–I–I, three four-bar phrases built from only the tonic, subdominant, and dominant. The **quick-change** variant moves to IV already in bar 2 (I–IV–I–I / IV–IV–I–I / V–IV–I–I), a small change that keeps the harmonic rhythm from feeling static this early in the form. Bar 9 is normatively V, bar 10 either IV or V depending on style. Jazz's further reharmonization of this skeleton — chromatic ii–V motion into bars 5 and 9, tritone substitutions, "Bird blues" bebop changes — belongs to [jazz-and-improvisation.md](jazz-and-improvisation.md#form); it is a different, jazz-specific layer on the same 12-bar chassis and is not repeated here.

### 8-bar and 16-bar forms

The 8-bar blues is the form's second most common length, distinguished from the 12-bar not just by being shorter but by a different harmonic rhythm: the I chord moves to V already in bar 2 (rather than to IV in bar 5), e.g. I–V–IV–IV–I–V–I–I ("Key to the Highway," "How Long Blues," "Trouble in Mind," several early Bessie Smith and Big Bill Broonzy sides). A 16-bar form exists too, essentially an 8-bar form with the opening I chord stretched to fill 8 bars before the rest proceeds as usual; "Key to the Highway" is recorded in both 8- and 16-bar versions depending on performer, which is a useful reminder that these forms are a family artists reshape, not a fixed catalog.

### AAB, statement–restatement–answer, and the fill

The blues stanza is three lines, **AAB**: a line is sung, repeated (often with small melodic variation), then answered by a new, rhyming third line — mapped one-to-one onto the 12-bar form's three four-bar phrases. Titon's *Early Downhome Blues* (1977; rev. 1994), a grammar built from 48 melodic transcriptions, works out this AAB melodic grammar in detail; David Evans's *Big Road Blues* (1982), from fieldwork in Mississippi and Louisiana, documents the same structure as a living compositional practice among folk-blues singers rather than a fixed textbook form.

The load-bearing consequence for texture is that each vocal (or lead) line only fills part of its four-bar phrase — the remainder is answered by a second voice. This is **call-and-response between line and fill**: a guitar, harmonica, or piano lick occupies the gap left after the voice stops, in conversation with it rather than accompanying it continuously. Muddy Waters put the rule bluntly: if your guitar doesn't answer your voice, you're not playing the blues. B.B. King's own singing/playing alternation (he essentially never sang and played at the same moment) is the classic instrumental realization. Fills of this kind trace to the same call-and-response practice found in field hollers, work songs, and gospel; the ornamentation vocabulary they're built from (bends, slides, grace-note slurs) is covered in [ornamentation-and-embellishment.md](ornamentation-and-embellishment.md). For an engine, this is the rule to enforce structurally, not optionally: a lead voice that leaves rest space every alternate phrase, and a second voice/register that is only active in that space.

### Turnaround formulas

Bars 11–12 (or the last two bars of an 8-bar chorus) are the **turnaround**: a short cadential formula that, because nearly every blues performance repeats the chorus, is expected to land on or imply V rather than close on I — the listener needs to feel pulled back to the top.

| Turnaround | Roman numerals | Voice motion / notes |
|---|---|---|
| Basic | I – V | simplest: restate tonic, then land on V to launch the next chorus |
| "Blues" turnaround | V–IV–I–V | ends on V, not I, because another chorus follows; e.g. in A: E7–D7–A7–E7 |
| Ragtime | I–VI7–ii–V | VI7's major 3rd is a chromatic passing tone into ii's root (C→C♯→D over C–A7–Dm7–G7) |
| Diatonic ("jazzy") | I–vi–ii–V | same shape, diatonic vi substituted for the dominant-quality VI7 |
| Minor-blues | i–bVI–V(alt) | the minor-key equivalent; the closing V often carries an altered 9th |
| Chromatic walk-down | I, then a descending chromatic bass/inner line through bVII7/VI7 toward V | the "walk-down" family of closing licks, independent of any single chord label |
| Stock cliché | the "How Dry I Am" cadence | cited by blues pedagogy (Baker) as the single most common closing formula — a fixed melodic-harmonic tag rather than a generic progression |

## Blues pitch language

### Blue notes: what the measurement literature shows

Popular shorthand describes blue notes as the b3, #4/b5, and b7 "flattened a quarter tone" from equal temperament — a tidy claim that the measurement literature does not actually support. Court Cutting's cluster analysis of pitch-tracked recordings from 15 performances by early blues masters (*Empirical Musicology Review*, 2018) found not fixed points but three **variable pitch clusters**, centered near 319, 583, and 1038 cents above the tonic — zones of inflection around the minor third, the tritone/#4–b5 region, and the minor seventh, each with real spread across performers and phrases rather than one universal quarter-tone offset. A companion commentary in the same venue (Hähnel) is a reminder that pooling cent measurements across different performers, instruments, and eras into a single "scale" is itself a live methodological question, not settled fact.

Two complementary accounts predate and frame the measurement: Peter van der Merwe's *Origins of the Popular Style* (1989) proposes a "ladder of thirds" — a stack of thirds above and below the tonic — in which blues performance routinely produces a **neutral third**, neither the notated major nor minor third; van der Merwe puts it plainly: "most of the thirds will be neutral in actual performance," a claim about performance practice, not a fixed pitch. Hans Weisethaunet's "Is There Such a Thing as the 'Blue Note'?" (*Popular Music*, 2001) is the field's own skeptic: he separates "blue note" as an alleged discrete pitch phenomenon from "blue feeling" as a broader expressive performance practice, and warns against essentializing microtonal inflection as a single, quantifiable "African" fact opposed to "Western" harmony. Treat the quarter-tone folklore as a rough, honest simplification of something that is actually a **flexible target zone**, reached by continuous pitch motion, not a fourth scale-degree option sitting statically between two equal-tempered pitches.

### Bends, slides, and vocalized tone

Because the blue-note zone is a target reached in motion, its natural home is any continuously-variable-pitch source: string bends and slide/bottleneck guitar, harmonica draw-bends, and the singing voice itself, all gliding into or through the zone rather than fretting/keying it as a fixed pitch. Fixed-pitch instruments (piano) fake it by "crushing" — sounding the b3 and 3 together or in immediate succession — or by a grace-note slur a semitone apart, van der Merwe's own description of the mechanism. This is the delivery-mechanism side of the pitch-language question; the general ornament vocabulary (bends, slides, scoops, grace notes) is covered in [ornamentation-and-embellishment.md](ornamentation-and-embellishment.md). The same gliding-tone aesthetic is why blues instrumentalists are consistently described as playing "vocally" — a guitar or harmonica line is judged by how much it sounds like it is talking or crying, not by how cleanly it hits notated pitches.

### Mixing major and minor pentatonic

The textbook "blues scale" (minor pentatonic plus a chromatic b5 passing tone) understates actual practice, which freely mixes major-pentatonic and minor-pentatonic material over the same chord. The two scales already share the root and 5th; blues phrasing toggles the 3rd (and often the 6th/7th) between major and minor versions within a single phrase or even a single lick — sliding up into the major 3rd from the b3, or answering a minor-pentatonic phrase with a major-pentatonic one over the same I chord. This toggling, not a single special scale, is the actual mechanism producing pentatonic "blue" ambiguity, and it is the same neutral-third practice van der Merwe describes, spread out into a full melodic vocabulary rather than compressed into one pitch.

## Blues rhythm and groove

### Shuffle and slow-blues 12/8

The blues **shuffle** subdivides each beat into a triplet and omits the middle partial, producing the long-short "swung eighths" feel; this is the same long-short triplet family as jazz swing, and [jazz-and-improvisation.md](jazz-and-improvisation.md#swing-feel-the-measured-evidence) already carries the measured tempo-dependent swing-ratio canon (Friberg and Sundström) that this page defers to rather than re-measuring — dedicated acoustic measurement specific to blues shuffle (as opposed to jazz ride-cymbal swing) is thinner in the literature than the jazz corpus, an honest gap. **Slow blues** is often notated outright in 12/8 — four dotted-quarter beats, each explicitly divided into three eighth notes — which is the shuffle's implied triplet subdivision written as the meter itself rather than as a performance inflection over 4/4; it is the meter of songs like "When a Man Loves a Woman" and sits adjacent to the gospel-ballad 6/8 feel discussed below.

### Boogie-woogie bass patterns

Boogie-woogie piano is defined by a relentless, ostinato-like left hand — "eight to the bar" — transposed to follow the I–IV–V changes underneath a freely syncopated right hand. Named patterns include:

| Pattern | Notes (in C7) | Character |
|---|---|---|
| Rolling arpeggio | C–E–G–A \| Bb–A–G–E (root-3-5-6, b7-6-5-3) | the most iconic figure; the 6th is a mixolydian passing tone connecting the 5th to the b7th |
| Alternating shuffle bass | G–C–A–C (5-1-6-1, repeating) | simpler, historically earlier ("the Marshall"-type pattern) |
| Descending-tag variant | as above, dropping in pitch at the end of each two-bar cycle | a New Orleans-influenced elaboration ("the Jefferson"-type pattern) |

### Stop-time

Stop-time interrupts the normal flow of accompaniment: the band hits sharp, unison accents (typically beat 1 of every bar, or every other bar) and then rests or holds, while a soloist or vocalist continues alone through the silence, creating the illusion of suspended time though the underlying pulse never actually changes. It is a dramatic, occasional device — a stop-time break before a final turnaround, or under a vocal punchline — not a default texture; see [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md) and [rhythm-and-meter.md](rhythm-and-meter.md) on rest as a first-class rhythmic device.

## Country

### Song forms and key choices

Modern country defaults to verse-chorus form; classic and honky-tonk-era country (Hank Williams and predecessors) leans more on **AABA**, frequently with a bridge built on the same IV-IV-I-I/I-I-V-V "passamezzo"-descended harmonic schema found across many mid-century American popular songs. Key choice is a practical craft constraint as much as an aesthetic one: guitar- and fiddle-friendly open-position keys (G, C, D, A, E) dominate, with a capo used to reconcile a singer's comfortable key against a guitarist's preferred fingering — a constraint an engine can encode directly as a preferred-key list rather than treating key choice as arbitrary.

### The Nashville Number System

The Nashville Number System, devised around 1957 by Neal Matthews Jr. of the Jordanaires (who adapted the idea from gospel shape-note notation) and later refined and spread by session musician Charlie McCoy, notates chords as scale-degree numbers (1–7) in the stated key rather than letter names, so any chart is instantly playable in any key — a session leader calls a new key and the numbers do not change. Conventions: a bare number is a major chord; a dash or lowercase "m" (4−, 4m) marks minor; accidentals use b/#; a diamond drawn around a number means hold the chord for the full measure ("playing diamonds"); a caret/hat means strike and cut off immediately; underlines and dashes mark multiple chords or subdivisions within a measure; slash notation (1/3) marks a specified bass note or inversion. This is a genuinely transposable, scale-degree-relative harmony notation, and its general treatment belongs with [music-representation-and-notation.md](music-representation-and-notation.md); for this page it is the concrete existence proof that Nashville session musicians already solved "represent harmony so it transposes for free," which is exactly the representation problem a multi-key style pack needs solved.

### Harmonic vocabulary

Country's harmonic core is the same diatonic I-IV-V skeleton and secondary-dominant coloring (V/V, V/vi) documented in general in [harmony.md](harmony.md#color-and-departure-mixture-secondary-dominants-modulation); nothing here overrides that page's transition tables. The one country-specific device worth naming on its own is the **"truck driver's gear change"** (or "gear shift") — an abrupt modulation up a semitone or, more often, a whole step for a song's final chorus, typically with no new lyric content, just a repeat in the higher key, named (unflatteringly) for its blunt, mechanical effect. [harmony.md](harmony.md#color-and-departure-mixture-secondary-dominants-modulation) already notes pop's use of this "direct" shift; country and its parody wing both lean on it hard enough that "Weird Al" Yankovic's country pastiche "Truck Driving Song" plays the trope for a joke. For an engine it is a cheap, well-documented, one-line rule: transpose the final section up a step with no pivot chord.

### Signature grooves

- **Train beat / boom-chick (2-beat):** alternating bass-note-then-strummed-chord pattern — bass note (root) on beat 1, full chord on the "and," bass note (often the 5th) on beat 3, chord again on its "and" — simulating a chugging train; shared verbatim with bluegrass rhythm guitar.
- **Country waltz:** 3/4, a strong beat 1 followed by two lighter beats, danced with a smooth turning step; musically close to a ballroom waltz, distinguished mainly by tempo and repertoire context rather than by meter.
- **Two-step:** felt in duple time (commonly written 4/4), danced quick-quick-slow-slow, spanning a wide tempo range (roughly 130–200+ BPM danced).

### Instrumental idioms

**Pedal steel** is this family's most striking synthesized-voice concept. Foot pedals and knee levers mechanically raise or lower specific strings by a half or whole step while the other strings and the sliding steel bar hold still, producing an **oblique bend directly into a new chord voicing** — one voice glides continuously while the others sustain, a glissando between chord positions with no re-attack. On the standard country (E9) tuning, for example, the "A pedal" raises two strings a whole step to produce the instrument's signature turnaround bend. This is directly transferable: it is the same mechanism as the shared synth library's `pitchExpr` continuous-`detune`-automation layer built for expressive single notes in Engine 04 ([findings-cantabile-engine.md](findings-cantabile-engine.md)), generalized from one gliding voice to one gliding voice inside an otherwise static chord.

**Chicken pickin'** (Telecaster country lead) is hybrid picking: fretted notes sounded conventionally while the picking-hand fingers pluck and snap other strings sharply off the frets, producing muted, percussive "cluck" attacks; it depends on a bright, fast-transient tone (a Telecaster bridge pickup, low action) to read clearly. **Fiddle double-stops with shuffle bowing** pair a drone or harmony string under the melody string with a repeating bow pattern — a slurred downbow spanning beats 1 (and 3) followed by short alternating strokes on the offbeats — which is what puts backbeat emphasis into a single unaccompanied fiddle line (the "Nashville shuffle" bowing). **Honky-tonk piano** keeps a ragtime-descended "oom-pah" left hand — a single bass note on beats 1/3 alternating with a mid-register chord on 2/4 — under a syncopated right-hand fill vocabulary, historically played on cheap, sometimes thumbtack-hammered ("tack piano") uprights whose bright, clangy timbre was what let the piano cut through a noisy barroom.

## Bluegrass

### String-band roles and exact figuration

Bill Monroe fixed bluegrass's five-piece core — mandolin, banjo, fiddle, guitar, upright bass — around 1945, when Earl Scruggs joined the Blue Grass Boys. Each role has a specific, well-documented figuration:

- **Banjo** (Scruggs-style three-finger picking): each measure is an 8-note right-hand roll spanning thumb (T), index (I), and middle (M) across the 5 strings. The **forward roll** cascades low to high (a representative version: T-I-M-T-I-M-T-I across strings 3-2-1, the thumb dropping to the droning open 5th string mid-pattern), the **backward roll** mirrors it high to low (M-I-T ordering), and the **alternating thumb roll** keeps the thumb on every other pluck, bouncing it between the 5th-string drone and a bass string while index/middle carry the melody strings — the steady, droning pulse heard in "Cripple Creek." Exact fingerings vary a little by teaching source; the load-bearing structural fact for an engine is the fixed shape (8 plucks per measure, thumb/index/middle spanning 5 strings) rather than any one canonical sequence.
- **Mandolin chop:** a fully muted chord struck on the backbeat (beats 2 and 4) — the fretting hand releases pressure immediately after the pick stroke so the chord dies instantly ("chucking"), functioning as the snare-drum surrogate in a drummerless string band. Monroe's own innovation; it is what gives bluegrass rhythm a harder drive than old-time.
- **Flatpicked guitar:** melodic solo breaks built from scalar runs, hammer-ons/pull-offs/slides, and crosspicking (arpeggiated string-skipping); Doc Watson brought fiddle-tune melodies to the flatpicked guitar in the early 1960s, and Tony Rice later extended the vocabulary with jazz-inflected phrasing. The "G run," a stock descending-then-ascending bass-string lick associated with Lester Flatt, is the idiom's single most recognizable closing formula.
- **Bass:** root-fifth alternation on beats 1 and 3 (plucked upright), the harmonic anchor beneath chop and rolls alike.

### Breakneck tempo and solo rotation

Bluegrass tempos run from a medium ~110–130 BPM up to breakneck breakdowns of 140–180+ BPM; Monroe's own "Bluegrass Breakdown" was cut around 175 BPM. The form built on top of that tempo is **"taking a break"**: each lead instrument (banjo, fiddle, mandolin, guitar, dobro) plays one full pass of the tune's chorus or verse in turn while the others drop to backup (chop, rolls, bass), cycling through the whole band; instrumental fiddle-tune breakdowns cycle the AABB tune itself repeatedly with a different lead instrument each time through. This is, as a form, engine-perfect: fixed melodic/harmonic material, a deterministic instrument-rotation order, and a clean, total lead/backup role swap on every cycle.

### The high lonesome modal edge

Many bluegrass fiddle tunes sit in **Mixolydian** (the major scale with a lowered 7th — "Old Joe Clark," "Red Haired Boy," "June Apple") or **Dorian** ("mountain modal" tunes, whose b3/b7 against a major-feeling drone gives the bluesy dissonance practitioners call "the flat-7 sound"). "High lonesome" also names bluegrass's close, tense vocal harmony — often a dissonant or modally inflected tenor part above the lead melody — that Bill Monroe pioneered; working musicians use "modal" and "the flat-7 sound" loosely rather than drawing a hard Mixolydian/Dorian distinction, and an engine can reasonably do the same by treating both as one "modal-edge" flag over an otherwise major-key tune.

## Old-time, fiddle tunes, and gospel

### Old-time versus bluegrass

Old-time string-band music is **ensemble unison/heterophony**: fiddle, banjo, and whatever else is present play the same tune together throughout, "inside the beat" with a relaxed, non-virtuosic pulse, and structurally has no solo breaks at all — a piece simply is the tune, played together, repeated. Bluegrass, which Monroe built from this same repertoire and instrumentation in the mid-1940s, adds the individualist solo-rotation layer described above plus a harder, more syncopated drive (the mandolin chop). For an engine this is a clean two-mode fork over identical melodic/harmonic material: unison-ensemble mode versus lead-rotation-with-backup mode.

### The fiddle tune: AABB form and modal tunings

The fiddle tune's binary **AABB** form — an 8-bar A strain played twice, then an 8-bar B strain played twice, the whole cycled repeatedly (AABB AABB AABB…) as long as the dance or jam needs — is close to universal across the Anglo-Celtic-American dance-tune repertory (reels, jigs, breakdowns, hornpipes); the form and much of the actual tune stock descend directly from Irish and Scottish dance music, elaborated in Appalachia. [european-folk-and-dance.md](european-folk-and-dance.md) is the natural home for the Celtic-tradition side of this ancestry; this page notes only the American-fiddle-tune consequence.

Old-time fiddlers frequently use **cross-tuning** (scordatura, e.g. re-tuning the standard G3–D4–A4–E5 fiddle to A3-E4-A4-E5 or G3-D4-G4-D5) to open up sympathetic ringing and easy drone double-stops under a modal melody. The banjo's own droning, re-entrant 5th string — typically tuned to the tonic or fifth, struck by the thumb but never fretted — is the same open-string-drone logic on a different instrument. **Clawhammer** (frailing) banjo, old-time's counterpart to bluegrass three-finger picking, plays a repeating two-part cell called "bum-ditty": a full downward brush across the strings on the beat ("bum," struck with the back of a fingernail) followed by a quick thumb pluck of the 5th-string drone on the offbeat ("-ditty"), the same cell repeated through an entire tune.

### Gospel's harmonic gifts

Gospel contributes a harmonic signature that both country and — via its own dedicated lineage in [funk-soul-and-rnb.md](funk-soul-and-rnb.md) — soul and R&B draw on directly: a heavy reliance on **plagal (IV–I, "Amen") motion** rather than authentic (V–I) cadencing, often with the major IV briefly darkened to a borrowed minor iv on the way home (IV–iv–I) for a bittersweet color on the return; and slow, swaying ballads set in **6/8** or a 6/8-feel triplet subdivision — the same triplet grid as a blues shuffle or 12/8 slow blues, just felt in two beats per bar rather than four, and at hymn tempo rather than blues tempo.

## Implications for generative engines

- **A 12-bar style-pack family.** Implement a form-grid parameter (12-bar basic/quick-change, 8-bar, 16-bar) plus the turnaround library above (keyed by roman numerals so it transposes freely) plus a **mandatory fill/call-response rule** — the lead voice rests every alternate two-bar unit, and a second "fill" voice or register is only active in that rest — plus a shuffle-feel flag (straight vs. shuffle subdivision, scaled by tempo per [jazz-and-improvisation.md](jazz-and-improvisation.md#swing-feel-the-measured-evidence)'s swing curve). The fill/call-response rule is the single most load-bearing item in this list: without it, a 12-bar generator produces a chord chart with a tune over it, not a blues.
- **Banjo-roll and boom-chick accompaniment generators.** Encode the roll patterns and the country boom-chick/train-beat pattern as literal fixed pluck-sequence templates keyed to the current chord's chord tones — figuration templates, not note-by-note generation — the same approach the shared library already uses for comping patterns ([findings-cantabile-engine.md](findings-cantabile-engine.md)'s v0.2 comping-template pass).
- **A bend-capable lead-voice spec.** Represent each blue note as a target pitch-offset curve — a bend or scoop into a cents-wide zone (roughly 280–350 cents around the third, 550–620 around the tritone/b5, 950–1050 around the seventh, per the measurement literature above) — riding the shared synth's `detune`-based `pitchExpr` automation already built for Engine 04's `note.expr` layer ([findings-cantabile-engine.md](findings-cantabile-engine.md)). The same mechanism covers blues bends, pedal-steel glides into a chord, and fiddle slides; only the target zone and glide shape need to change per style. This belongs in [shared-libraries.md](shared-libraries.md) as a reusable synth-layer capability, not a one-engine hack.
- **A solo-rotation form template.** A reusable "cycle a fixed AABB or 12-bar chart N times, one lead instrument per cycle, others drop to a backup/rest pattern" form generator, parameterized only by the lead-instrument rotation order — directly reusable anywhere an engine wants a "trading" texture, well beyond bluegrass.
- **Nashville-number-style internal harmony representation.** Store every style pack's chord chart as scale-degree numbers with quality/inversion/hold markup, exactly as Nashville session musicians do, rather than absolute letter names, so a single chart transposes to any key at generation time — see [music-representation-and-notation.md](music-representation-and-notation.md). This is the natural internal format for every style pack this page catalogs.

## Open questions

- How wide (in cents) should an engine's blue-note target zone actually be, and does the answer vary by tempo, register, or instrument the way jazz swing ratio does? Cutting's clusters are pooled across performers and eras; per-performer or per-instrument spread was not separable from the sources accessible in this pass.
- Does the solo-rotation form generalize cleanly to non-bluegrass engines (a jazz combo trading fours is a near neighbor — see [jazz-and-improvisation.md](jazz-and-improvisation.md)), or does it depend on bluegrass's specific fixed-tempo, fixed-form breakdown context?
- Is there a measurable acoustic difference between a blues shuffle and jazz swing at matched tempo, or are they the same phenomenon under different names? The literature measured jazz ride-cymbal swing in detail (Friberg and Sundström); a dedicated blues-shuffle acoustic study was not located in this research pass.
