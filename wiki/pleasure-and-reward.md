# Pleasure and reward

Why does an abstract pattern of sound — carrying no food, no mate, no money — engage the same reward circuitry as things that keep us alive? The answer matters enormously to this project: a generative engine can be theoretically correct about melody and harmony yet still produce music no one *wants* to hear again, and the reward literature says the missing ingredient is usually **anticipation** — the setup and payoff of expectation, not the sounds themselves. This page covers the peak-pleasure phenomenon of chills, the dopamine findings that localize musical reward, the evidence that music reward is a real and dissociable thing, and the broader aesthetic emotions (being moved, awe, nostalgia) that most listening actually runs on.

## Chills / frisson

The clearest handle on musical pleasure is the "chill" (frisson): a shiver, gooseflesh, a wave down the spine, often with a lump in the throat and measurable autonomic changes (skin-conductance rise, heart-rate change, piloerection). Chills are common but not universal — a large fraction of listeners report them, with prevalence and frequency varying by personality (notably Openness to Experience) and by how much a listener engages attentively. Chills are reliably rated as *intensely pleasurable*, which makes them a convenient, physiologically-anchored marker researchers use to study peak musical reward. A related but distinct peak response is *tears/being-moved*, with a different physiological signature (slower breathing, different arousal profile) — both are pleasant, but they are not the same event.

## Structural triggers of chills

Crucially for an engine, chills are not random — they cluster at identifiable structural moments. Sloboda's foundational questionnaire study (1991) and later work by Grewe, Panksepp, and others converge on a list of triggers:

- **Sudden dynamic or textural change** — an unexpected swell, a new instrument or voice entering, a drop, a sudden thickening or thinning of texture.
- **New or unprepared harmony** — a harmonic move the listener did not see coming; enharmonic changes; expressive dissonance and its resolution.
- **Melodic appoggiaturas and suspensions** — a dissonant non-chord tone leaning into a resolution (the "sigh" figure).
- **Melodic or harmonic sequence** — a pattern repeated at rising pitch, building anticipation.
- **Register leaps / a melodic peak** — the melody vaulting to a high point, especially a solo voice soaring above the texture.
- **Events arriving earlier or later than prepared for** — a structural expectation violated in time.

The common thread is **expectation**: nearly every trigger is a violation, delay, or dramatic confirmation of something the listener was led to expect. Chills mark the moments where the expectation machinery ([musical-expectation.md](musical-expectation.md)) pays off hardest.

## Blood & Zatorre 2001: reward circuitry

Blood and Zatorre's PET study (2001) was the landmark. Playing listeners self-chosen music that reliably gave them chills, and scaling brain activity to chill intensity, they found that as chills intensified, activity changed in a network of regions implicated in **reward, motivation, emotion, and arousal** — ventral striatum, midbrain, amygdala, orbitofrontal cortex, ventromedial prefrontal cortex — the same structures engaged by food, sex, and drugs of abuse. This was the first hard evidence that music taps the brain's core reward system rather than some separate "aesthetic" faculty.

## Salimpoor: dopamine, anticipation vs. peak

Two studies by Salimpoor, Zatorre, and colleagues sharpened the picture:

- **2011 (Nature Neuroscience)**: using PET with a dopamine-binding tracer plus fMRI, they showed music-induced chills cause actual **dopamine release**, and — the key result — release occurs in **two anatomically and temporally distinct** parts of the striatum. The **caudate** (dorsal striatum) is active during the *anticipation* phase, in the seconds *before* the peak; the **nucleus accumbens** (ventral striatum) is active at the *peak* pleasure moment. Pleasure has a build-up circuit and a payoff circuit, and they are different places. The anticipatory dopamine is arguably the more important for design — it is the *wanting* that pulls the listener forward.
- **2013 (Science)**: listeners heard 60 previously-unheard excerpts and placed real monetary bids on how much they would pay for each. **Nucleus accumbens activity, and especially its functional connectivity with the auditory cortices** (and orbitofrontal cortex), predicted how much money a listener would spend on a novel track. Reward value arises from the *coupling* of the sound-analyzing cortex with the reward system — the reward tracks how the incoming music matches the templates and expectations stored in auditory cortex.

## Specific musical anhedonia: reward is dissociable

Does music reward really stand on its own, or is it a side-effect of general reward or of arousal? Mas-Herrero and colleagues answered with **specific musical anhedonia**: a minority of people (on the order of ~5%) get little or no pleasure from music despite (a) intact music *perception* (they can tell sad from happy, detect wrong notes) and (b) intact reward responses to *other* things (money, food). Their subdued response is specific to music — reduced physiological reactivity and reduced nucleus-accumbens engagement to music, but normal responses to a monetary task. Individual differences are measured with the **Barcelona Music Reward Questionnaire (BMRQ)**, which finds a wide, roughly five-factor spread of musical hedonia across the population. The upshot: musical reward is a genuine, dissociable trait, not reducible to general reward or arousal — and it varies enormously between people, which any evaluation of "good music" must account for.

## Anticipation is the engine

Tying the reward findings to expectation theory yields the project's most actionable principle. Musical pleasure is driven substantially by **prediction and its resolution**, not by momentary acoustic beauty:

- The anticipatory (caudate) dopamine of Salimpoor 2011 is a *wanting* signal that ramps during the setup, before any "beautiful" sound arrives.
- **Reward prediction error** accounts (Gold, Cheung, Zatorre) recast musical pleasure in reinforcement-learning terms: pleasure arises when outcomes beat the current prediction, and — because the brain is rewarded for *learning* — from successfully reducing uncertainty. Gold et al. (2019) found liking peaks at intermediate predictability (a reward for learnable-but-not-trivial structure); Cheung et al. (2019) found pleasure depends on the *interaction* of context uncertainty and event surprise, with nucleus accumbens tracking uncertainty (see [musical-expectation.md](musical-expectation.md) for both).
- This is why sound design and correct harmony are necessary but not sufficient: without a stream of well-formed expectations to set up and resolve, there is nothing for the reward system to anticipate. It is the most likely mechanism behind "intriguing but not satisfying" — intriguing surfaces with no anticipatory arc.

## Aesthetic emotions beyond chills

Chills are the *studied* peak, but most rewarding listening is subtler. Music elicits a family of **aesthetic emotions** — being *moved* (tenderness/poignancy, the main driver of sad-music enjoyment; see [emotion-and-meaning.md](emotion-and-meaning.md)), **awe** and **wonder** (at scale, beauty, or the sublime), **nostalgia** (often the dominant felt response to slow, reflective music), and simple absorption/flow. These do not all show the frisson signature and some are quiet, sustained pleasures rather than peaks. For a project targeting *attentive* as well as *background* listening, these low-arousal rewards are as important as chills — a piece that induces calm absorption or gentle nostalgia is succeeding even if no one shivers.

## Dose-response: familiarity and pleasure

Pleasure is not fixed per piece; it moves with exposure. As covered in [repetition-and-familiarity.md](repetition-and-familiarity.md), liking (and the affective/reward response) typically **rises over the first several exposures** and, under attentive listening, eventually **declines with overexposure** (an inverted-U); background listening satiates far more slowly. Reward-system engagement to familiar music can be robust even for music people say they dislike. So the pleasure a track delivers is partly a function of *where the listener is on its exposure curve* — first-listen reward and tenth-listen reward are different quantities, and an engine that generates ever-fresh material keeps listeners perpetually on the low, early part of the curve, before familiarity-driven reward has accrued.

## Implications for generative engines

- **Design the anticipatory arc, not just the sounds.** The dominant reward signal (caudate dopamine) fires during *build-up*. Engineer setups — rising sequences, harmonic tension, textural crescendos, delayed resolutions — so the listener is actively wanting the next moment. Beautiful timbre with no anticipatory structure leaves the reward system idle.
- **Place chill-triggers deliberately at structural peaks.** The trigger list is a concrete recipe: at a section's climax, combine several of {sudden dynamic/textural swell, a new voice entering, an unprepared harmony, an appoggiatura resolution, a rising sequence, a melodic leap to a registral peak, an early/late arrival}. Do not scatter them; concentrate them where an arc has been built.
- **Target intermediate, learnable surprise.** Reward-for-learning and the inverted-U (Gold 2019) say pleasure peaks when structure is discoverable but not trivial. Give the listener patterns to predict and then reward/violate those predictions — see the surprise-band guidance in [musical-expectation.md](musical-expectation.md).
- **Build in familiarity to accrue reward over time.** Because reward rises with exposure, a piece that establishes and returns to memorable material lets familiarity-driven reward accumulate within a single listen; perpetual novelty forfeits it.
- **Expect huge inter-listener variance.** Specific musical anhedonia and the BMRQ spread mean ~5% get little musical reward and the rest vary widely. Do not treat any single listener (or the developer's own ear) as ground truth; evaluate across many people and report distributions, not averages (see [listening-tests-and-feedback.md](listening-tests-and-feedback.md)).
- **Do not equate reward with high arousal.** Awe, being-moved, nostalgia, and absorption are real rewards without chills. Slow, quiet, harmonically simple music can be deeply rewarding — essential for the background/ambient side of the project.

## Open questions

- Which chill-triggers survive translation to *synthesized* timbres and browser playback, where a "new instrument entering" or a "soaring voice" may lack the recorded-performance affordances that make the trigger land?
- Can an engine engineer anticipatory arcs that reliably produce measurable pleasure in listening tests, or does peak reward require personal/veridical familiarity it cannot supply on first listen?
- What is the realistic ceiling of first-listen reward for generated music, given that much human musical pleasure is familiarity- and memory-dependent?
