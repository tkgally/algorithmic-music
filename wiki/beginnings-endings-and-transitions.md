# Beginnings, endings, and transitions

Every piece has to start, has to move between sections, and has to stop, and each of these three moments is as stylistically marked as harmony or rhythm — yet this project's wiki so far covers them unevenly: phrase-level cadences are documented in [phrase-structure](phrase-structure.md), whole-piece form in [form-and-structure](form-and-structure.md), and one style's ending has been solved in engineering detail in [findings-tonal-classical-engine](findings-tonal-classical-engine.md), but nothing collects how pieces begin, how they hand off, and how each genre signals "we are done" — the gap this project's research agenda flags as [R6](project-open-questions.md). This page is that collection: a cross-genre catalog of beginning, transition, and ending idioms with enough mechanism, and where evidence exists enough numbers, that an engine's style pack can implement each as data.

## Beginnings: setting the terms of listening

An opening's first job is to give the listener a sample to predict from: [musical-expectation](musical-expectation.md) explains that schematic expectation is learned from a style's statistics, so the first seconds are where a piece hands over the statistics it wants judged against. [style-and-genre-overview](style-and-genre-overview.md) names what gets declared first — instrumentation, tempo/feel, harmonic vocabulary, and production aesthetic are the "style tokens" that identify a genre within seconds, and an opening is where they all arrive at once: tempo and meter, key or mode (or, for drone musics, simply the tonic), and texture/palette, the minimum a listener needs before the piece can be heard as going somewhere.

### A catalog of intro types

- **Count-in / pickup** — a shouted or clicked count, or a melodic anacrusis ([phrase-structure](phrase-structure.md)), that launches the downbeat while establishing little beyond tempo.
- **Vamp-till-theme** — the rhythm section grooves on static harmony until a cue brings in the theme; borrowed from the theatrical direction "vamp till ready" / "vamp till cue," an instruction for a pit band to loop a passage indefinitely until an onstage event tells them to stop. The same device opens big-band, gospel, and jam-band sets.
- **Fade-in** — the mirror of the fade-out (below): the mix rises from silence, implying the music was already happening. Rarer than fade-out but recognizable in psychedelia and DJ-mixed sets.
- **Sound-emergence** — ambient's version of fade-in: voices individually waking at staggered times rather than one volume ramp. This project's own ambient engine implements it as a measured "global swell" from near-silence ([findings-ambient-drift-engine](findings-ambient-drift-engine.md)); see [ambient-and-generative-genre](ambient-and-generative-genre.md) for the Eno lineage.
- **Thematic head-start (in medias res)** — borrowing the literary term for starting mid-action, an opening that skips preamble and opens on the hook, riff, or chorus itself; the streaming-era data below is evidence this is becoming pop's default rather than its exception.
- **Drum intro** — an unaccompanied rhythmic pickup before pitched instruments enter, common in rock and especially funk; a stripped drum break used this way is historically also the raw material breakbeat-based genres later sampled and looped (see [hip-hop-and-beat-making](hip-hop-and-beat-making.md)).
- **Alap-style unmetered exploration** — Hindustani and Dhrupad performance opens with a slow, pulseless statement of the raga's tones and no drum, gradually admitting pulse and then cycle; see [indian-classical-music](indian-classical-music.md). The longest intro type surveyed here — tens of minutes in the old Dhrupad style.
- **Taqsim** — the Arabic/Turkish counterpart: an improvisation, usually unmetered and often over a drone, preceding a fixed composition. Alkaei & Küssner's (2021) interview study with professional performers describes it as having "an introduction, the body of the taqsim, a kind of development, and a conclusion" in itself, with the opening measures constrained to the low register of the maqam's lower tetrachord (*ajnas*) specifically to "introduce the maqam to the listener" before the performer ranges freely; see [arabic-and-middle-eastern-music](arabic-and-middle-eastern-music.md).

### How long an intro should be

The best-sourced number here is also the most dramatic trend. Hubert Léveillé Gauvin's study of Billboard year-end top-10 singles from 1986–2015 found average time-to-first-lyric fell from just over 20 seconds in the mid-1980s to about 5 seconds by 2015 — a 78% reduction — alongside rising tempo and earlier title-drops. Gauvin attributes this to streaming's attention economy: skipping costs the listener nothing, platforms pay artists only once a listener has stayed roughly 30 seconds, and an intro has become a liability rather than a scene-setter. This is genre-specific, not universal: the catalog above spans roughly three orders of magnitude, from a single-digit-second pop intro to a Dhrupad alap lasting tens of minutes. EDM resists the compression pop shows because its intro is deliberately long (16–32 bars, four-on-the-floor, no melodic commitment yet) to give a mixing DJ room to beat-match before the track's identity arrives ([electronic-and-dance](electronic-and-dance.md)); alap and taqsim are not competing for skip-through attention at all. Intro length is set by what the intro is *for* — hook delivery, DJ tooling, or gradual modal revelation — and a style pack should set it accordingly. See [tempo-duration-and-pacing](tempo-duration-and-pacing.md) for the wider pacing implications.

## Transitions: the connective tissue between sections

### Cadential elision and the retransition

Section joins mostly reuse phrase-level machinery: [phrase-structure](phrase-structure.md) covers elision (a cadence bar doubling as the next phrase's downbeat) and [harmony](harmony.md) covers cadence mechanics; both generalize directly upward. One device sits above the phrase and is worth naming here: the **retransition**, familiar from sonata form's "standing on the dominant" before a recapitulation — a passage parked on an extended dominant, often thinning in texture and rising in tension, so the returning material lands as the strongest possible arrival. It is a half cadence at section scale: withheld resolution whose whole function is to make the next downbeat feel inevitable ([musical-expectation](musical-expectation.md)).

### Drum fills as section signposts

In backbeat-driven popular music the fill is the standard section-boundary punctuation: production pedagogy consistently places fills in the final bar (or final half- or quarter-bar) of a 4/8/16/32-bar phrase, immediately before a verse-to-chorus-type boundary, with density rising toward it — a groove's base subdivision giving way to eighth- or sixteenth-note figures, so the fill reads as an accelerando in event rate even at constant tempo. This is studio/pedagogical consensus rather than a peer-reviewed finding, so treat exact placement as a style-pack parameter to calibrate by ear; the invariant worth keeping is boundary-only placement on the hypermetric grid already established in [phrase-structure](phrase-structure.md).

### The EDM build/drop boundary

EDM's build-to-drop is the genre's dedicated transition device and is covered in full in [electronic-and-dance](electronic-and-dance.md) (risers, snare rolls, filter sweeps, bass/kick removal and return, the pre-drop pause). Worth adding here: the drop is a cadence substitute at section scale — a scheduled, prepared arrival exactly like a PAC, realized in texture and register instead of harmony, which is why techno's near-total absence of harmonic cadences does not cost it a strong sense of arrival.

### Attacca, the gap, and the crossfade

Whether a section follows the last with or without a seam has names in both directions. **Attacca** ("attach") is the score direction to begin the next movement immediately; Beethoven's Fifth runs its third movement into the fourth this way. Its opposite is simply **the gap** — silence, often filled by applause or an attention reset — the default between otherwise-independent pieces. In continuous genres the transition problem is neither but the **crossfade**, and this project has its own cautionary data point: Engine 02's ambient composer originally held a departing region's pad voicing at full level through an 8-second overlap with the arriving voicing, so two complete, dense modal chords sounded together and their partials beat audibly as sensory roughness; cutting the overlap to 4 seconds, so the old voicing's release finished before the new one reached full level, fixed it without shortening the felt transition ([findings-ambient-drift-engine](findings-ambient-drift-engine.md)). The general lesson: a safe crossfade overlap is bounded by the sustained voices' release time, not by an arbitrary duration.

### Modulation as a transition event: the truck driver's gear change

Modulating up a half or whole step near the end of a song — typically after a chorus, launching a repeat of the chorus in the new key — is common enough to have a mocking name, the **truck driver's gear change**, comparing it to a tired driver clunking up a gear. Wikipedia's "Modulation (music)" gives it real numbers: in every decade from the 1960s through the 1990s, roughly 25% of *Billboard* Hot 100 number-one hits contained a key change, falling to just one in the entire 2010s (Travis Scott's "Sicko Mode"). Chris Dalla Riva, who coded nearly 1,200 Hot 100 number-ones for his book *Uncharted Territory*, corroborates the timing in a 2025 interview, dating the key change's death to "around the year 2000" and linking it to hip-hop's rise and DAW-based composition replacing the live-band, key-transposing workflow that made a gear change cheap to execute. The device's reputation has soured with its use — fan taxonomy (TV Tropes, an informal but widely echoed source) calls it "neither difficult nor creative," reeking of "rookie songwriting" once overheard — a case study in a device wearing out through overexposure ([repetition-and-familiarity](repetition-and-familiarity.md)). Structurally the gear change and the fade-out (below) are siblings: both are mid-century devices for extending a chorus's payoff past where the song runs out of ideas, and both declined for overlapping reasons under skip-friendly digital listening.

### Stop-time and the solo break

Stop-time interrupts accompaniment with sparse, heavily accented hits (often beat 1 of alternate bars), leaving silence or a soloist in between; it dates at least to Scott Joplin's *Stoptime Rag* (1910) and earlier ragtime and tap practice. Wikipedia's "Stop-time" traces a direct line from this device to the **break** — stripped-down or absent accompaniment showcasing a soloist — and onward to the breakbeats hip-hop production is built on (see [hip-hop-and-beat-making](hip-hop-and-beat-making.md)). As a transition rather than a whole-chorus texture, a short stop-time break (one to four bars) marks a boundary by *removing* material, the rhythmic mirror of a fill's density increase.

## Endings: per-genre idioms

### Classical: the coda's rhetoric of arrival

A coda ("tail") adds weight after a piece has already reached its formal goal; per music theorist Charles Burkhart its job is to give the listener time to look back and process what they heard, converting a preceding climax into settled balance rather than an abrupt stop. Classical codas lean on a small device set: revisiting earlier thematic material rather than merely restating the tonic, growing to real scale when the material warrants it (Beethoven expanded the coda until, in the Symphony No. 8 finale, it rivals the exposition's weight), and closing with cadential confirmation, often more than one authentic cadence in a row. This project's launch engine works the same rhetoric in miniature: its coda broadens the harmonic rhythm (whole-bar I, then IV) before accelerating into a mid-bar ii–V that resolves to a lengthened final tonic, paired with a melodic descent to 1̂ and a closing ritardando — broaden, then compress, then confirm ([findings-tonal-classical-engine](findings-tonal-classical-engine.md)). Jazz and pop occupy the same slot under different names, "outro" or "tag."

### Jazz: ending clichés as a shared vocabulary

Jazz endings are a small, named, shared vocabulary rather than a problem solved fresh each time — a direct extension of the formula-based improvisation in [jazz-and-improvisation](jazz-and-improvisation.md). The workhorse **ii–V–I** that closes any jazz phrase needs dressing up at a piece's very end, since a plain major triad reads as unfinished: a sustained **I maj6/9** or **I maj7(#11)** under a fermata are the two default "stop here" sonorities; a **bII maj7(#11)** resolving down a half step into the tonic is a common substitute approach (its 3rd stepping to the tonic's 3rd, its #11 doubling the tonic's 5th); a rubato cadenza over a held **V7(alt.)** before the tonic is cued, or a held **I7(#9)** in blues/rhythm-changes contexts, are two more. Two named conventions recur: the **tag** — repeating the last four-ish bars of the **head** (the tune's main melody) two or three times, with a ritardando applied only on the final pass, so the slowdown itself signals this repeat of the head is the last — and the **Basie ending**, a bVII–VII–I approach (e.g., Eb–E–F in the key of F) voiced through the 3rd and 5th of each chord, associated with Count Basie's big band and borrowed widely as a stock finish. A vamp-and-fade on a static tag chord is jazz's own version of the pop fade-out.

### Rock: the big ending, the ring-out, and the cold stop

Rock's endings split along an intensity axis. At the high-energy end, fan taxonomy (via the Rock Band video game wiki, an informal but consistent source) names the **big rock ending**: the band abandons its established beat for an unmetered flurry — tom/cymbal rolls, improvised runs, in metal a held scream — resolving onto one final hit, a dramatized loss and recovery of rhythmic control. At the low-event end is the **ring-out**: a final chord struck once and left to decay. The extreme example is the Beatles' "A Day in the Life" (1967): its closing E-major chord was played simultaneously by four musicians across three pianos and a harmonium and left to ring over 40 seconds while engineers raised the recording level as the sound decayed, capturing room noise as it approached silence. Between these sits the **cold stop** — the ensemble cuts off together on a downbeat, no ritardando, no decay tail — the Beatles' own default before 1966 and, per production guides, "the most common way to end a song today." See [rock-and-pop](rock-and-pop.md).

### The fade-out: rise, dominance, and near-disappearance

The fade-out has an unusually well-documented history, via William Weir's reporting for *Slate* (2014, also covered by NPR the same week). Weir traces engineered fades to at least a 1930 recording ("Beyond the Blue Horizon," simulating a departing train), notes an 1894 Berliner Gramophone disc that acoustically fades a marching band into the distance, and cites Holst's "Neptune" (1918), whose offstage women's choir fades by having a door "closed very slowly." Magnetic tape from the 1940s–50s made the effect trivial, and by Weir's count of Billboard's year-end top 10 it dominated pop for three decades, peaking in 1985, when *every* song in the year-end top 10 ended in a fade. The decline is equally measurable: by 2005 only 3 of the top 10 had fades, and the 2011–2013 top-10 lists combined yield exactly one (Robin Thicke's "Blurred Lines"). Weir cites several compounding causes: a cultural turn toward "closure" as a value; "skip culture" enabled by the iPod (2001) and digital players, which let listeners jump past a fade rather than sit through it; club/DJ practice, where a track is mixed out before its fade would arrive; Pro Tools-era editing making a definitive composed ending easier than the old workaround of fading out an imperfect take; and, plainly, fashion — songwriter-producer Itaal Shur's explanation to Weir was "as soon as it becomes the way to do it, that's the way to do it." See [rock-and-pop](rock-and-pop.md) and [tempo-duration-and-pacing](tempo-duration-and-pacing.md) for the adjacent streaming-era pacing pressures that also shortened intros.

### EDM: the outro built for the next DJ

An EDM outro is not written for a listener's emotional closure; it is written for the next DJ. It mirrors the intro — a stripped, DJ-mixable 16–32 bar stub, typically kick/hat and maybe bass with lead and harmonic material already removed — so a second track can be beat-matched and blended in without an audible seam. Where a pop outro drives toward silence or a final chord, an EDM outro drives toward *neutrality*: minimal harmonic content left to clash with whatever comes next. A second data point, with ambient below, for a genre whose ending is defined by function rather than cadence; see [electronic-and-dance](electronic-and-dance.md).

### Ambient: dissolve rather than cadence

Ambient has no cadence to arrive at, so its ending is a **dissolve** — the reverse of sound-emergence, with sustained voices decaying independently rather than resolving together. [ambient-and-generative-genre](ambient-and-generative-genre.md) documents the mixing esthetic that enables this (long releases, no transients, narrow dynamic range), and this project's own ambient engine implements the ending as literally as the opening: a global fade shaped so the piece "emerges from and dissolves back into silence," with deliberately no meter, no ritardando, and no articulation shortening — the expressive-performance rule set that serves classical and jazz endings is switched off because a sustained texture gives it nothing to act on ([findings-ambient-drift-engine](findings-ambient-drift-engine.md)).

### Percussion traditions: the stop-cut

[percussion-music](percussion-music.md) documents the drum-circle **stop-cut** — Arthur Hull's term for a facilitator-signaled unison stop ("stop on a dime"), the harmony-free equivalent of a cold stop — alongside concert-tradition cousins: Varèse treating silence as a first-class formal event, and taiko's **oroshi**, an accelerating roll that gear-shifts a piece toward its close rather than decelerating into it. With the classical ritardando, that gives three distinct rhythmic endings — cut, decelerate, accelerate-then-cut — none involving a harmonic cadence, confirming that page's wider claim that density and dynamics substitute for harmony as percussion's structural currency.

### What listeners read as "ending": the psychological cues

Across every idiom above, the mechanism differs but the underlying cues converge on a short list, each already documented elsewhere in this wiki and worth naming together because their conjunction is what an ending *is*, perceptually: **deceleration** (the final ritardando, measured by Friberg & Sundberg as a square-root curve modeled on decelerating runners — [expressive-performance](expressive-performance.md)); **descent** (melodic motion toward a low, stable scale degree, typically 1̂ — [musical-expectation](musical-expectation.md)); **thinning** (fewer simultaneous voices, a texture cue listeners use for segmentation generally, not only endings); **dynamic decay** (falling loudness, whether an engineered fade-out or a natural instrumental decay — the inverse of the loudness rise [tension-and-release](tension-and-release.md) names as tension's strongest single driver); and **harmonic or rhythmic arrival** (a cadence, a unison stop-cut, a drop back to a stable ground). No genre here uses all five at once, and several — EDM, ambient, percussion circles — use none of the harmonic one, but nearly every genre uses at least two; redundancy across channels, more than any single cue, plausibly lets a listener recognize an ending in an unfamiliar style. This paragraph is this page's own synthesis of the evidence above rather than a single sourced claim — worth testing directly once the project has a listening panel ([listening-tests-and-feedback](listening-tests-and-feedback.md)).

## Implications for generative engines

- **Add beginning/transition/ending grammars as required style-pack fields.** [generative-music-design-patterns](generative-music-design-patterns.md)'s pattern P4 ("style pack as data") currently lists scale/tuning, chord vocabulary, rhythm feels, form archetypes, timbre palette, performance rules, and mix targets, but not how a piece starts, joins sections, or stops. Given how idiom-specific every example above is, these belong in the schema as required fields: an **intro spec** (type from the catalog, target length or length-determining function), a **transition spec** (allowed joins — elision, fill, drop, attacca, gap, crossfade-with-max-overlap, modulation-at-most-once), and an **ending spec** (mechanism, length, deceleration curve, final-sonority rule).
- **A per-style ending-idiom table**, generalizing the worked classical coda into the other launch styles:

| Style | Ending mechanism | Typical length | Deceleration curve | Final-sonority rule |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tonal classical | Coda: broaden harmonic rhythm, then accelerate into cadential confirmation | A few bars, ~5–10% of piece | Square-root ritardando, final 2–4 bars | PAC: tonic triad, melody on 1̂ |
| Jazz combo | Tag (ritard only on final repeat) or vamp-and-fade | Tag: 2–3 reps of ~4 bars | Ritard confined to the last repetition | Tonic maj6/9 or maj7(#11); or Basie bVII–VII–I |
| Rock/pop — cold stop | Full-ensemble cutoff on a downbeat | Instant | None | Tonic chord, cut clean, no decay tail |
| Rock/pop — ring-out | Final chord struck once, left to decay | 5–40+ s | Natural acoustic decay only | Tonic (or colored) chord, undamped |
| Rock/pop — fade (declining) | Engineered volume ramp over a repeating vamp | 10–25 s over an open loop | Volume ramp, front-loaded then longer tail | No resolution required — loop never cadences |
| EDM | Outro: subtract layers to a DJ-mixable stub | 16–32 bars | None (tempo locked); energy falls via layer removal | No cadence — ends on a rhythmic element |
| Ambient | Dissolve: independent per-voice decay to silence | Tens of seconds to minutes | Gradual, asynchronous, no pulse | No cadence — terminal state is silence or a bare drone |
| Percussion / drum circle | Stop-cut, or ritard/oroshi into a final hit | Instant, or a few bars | None (cut), or accelerando-then-cut | Unison final attack; no pitched cadence |

- **Transition insertion rules at form boundaries.** Restrict every transition device to a hypermetric boundary (4/8/16/32 bars, [phrase-structure](phrase-structure.md)), never mid-phrase. Use cadential elision only where the form plan calls for continuous flow (a re-approach into a varied return); use a full cadence elsewhere so boundaries stay legible ([form-and-structure](form-and-structure.md)). Cap once-per-piece devices — modulation chief among them, given how fast the truck driver's gear change reads as cliché. Bound crossfade overlap by the shorter voice's release time, per the ambient engine's own measured lesson. Make attacca-vs-gap a style-pack switch: continuous genres (EDM, minimalism, ambient) default to attacca; sectional/contrast-driven genres (classical multi-movement, verse-chorus pop) default to a brief gap so a textural reset can register.
- **Generalize R6 beyond the tonal engine — with a concrete next target.** [R6](project-open-questions.md) is closed for tonal-classical, but the other four shipped engines land at four different points on this page's spectrum, itself evidence for treating endings as style-pack data rather than an engine-level default. Engine 02 (ambient-drift) already has a genre-correct dissolve ([findings-ambient-drift-engine](findings-ambient-drift-engine.md)). Engine 05 (percussion) already implements three different endings for its three sub-styles in one codebase — concert ritardando, folk fade, circle stop-cut ([findings-percussion-engine](findings-percussion-engine.md)) — the strongest existing proof that ending idiom is a style parameter, not a constant. Engine 04 (cantabile) has a coda-shaped ending whose only open item is a listening judgment, not a missing mechanism ([findings-cantabile-engine](findings-cantabile-engine.md)). Engine 03 (groove-lofi) is the genuine gap: its own findings page states it "thins to an outro but does not cadence" and names a final turnaround or a filter-down/tape-stop as the fix ([findings-groove-lofi-engine](findings-groove-lofi-engine.md)) — this page's table points to the **DJ-outro** mechanism (layer subtraction to a stub) or a **tape-stop** (a combined pitch-and-tempo downward glide, a fourth mechanism distinct from the coda, the dissolve, and the cold stop) as the two genre-correct options, now scoped rather than open.

## Open questions

- Does a computable "ending-cue" detector (deceleration + descent + thinning + dynamic decay + harmonic/rhythmic arrival) belong in [computational-music-metrics](computational-music-metrics.md) alongside the cadence detector R4 already calls for, and how many of the five channels need to fire?
- How many convergent cues does a genre need before *inattentive* listeners register closure, versus attentive listeners who may track a single cue that background listening misses ([attention-and-background-listening](attention-and-background-listening.md))?
- This page's claim that the fade-out's and the truck-driver-modulation's declines share a cause (skip-friendly digital listening rewarding songs that resolve rather than re-sell their hook) is its own synthesis of two independently reported trends, not a claim either source makes explicitly — worth checking against a source studying both together.
- Does a badly-matched ending idiom (a fade-out grafted onto a genre whose listeners expect a cold stop) read as a specific "wrong genre" error or just as generically unpolished? Relevant to the pastiche discussion in [style-and-genre-overview](style-and-genre-overview.md).
