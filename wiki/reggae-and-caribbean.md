# Reggae and Caribbean musics

Jamaica's popular-music lineage — mento, ska, rocksteady, reggae, and dancehall — is the clearest real-world case this wiki has found of a music industry built explicitly on reusable rhythm templates: the riddim, an instrumental bass-and-drum(-and-chord) track voiced by dozens or hundreds of different singers and deejays, is template-based composition practiced as ordinary commercial process rather than as a compositional gimmick. Its studio counterpart, dub, turned the mixing desk itself into a live instrument for arranging by muting, filtering, and echoing — a working, pre-digital model of algorithmic form. This page traces the Jamaican lineage's dated stages and drum-pattern signatures, extracts the riddim system and dub practice as directly implementable engine architectures, and surveys the wider Anglophone and Francophone Caribbean — calypso, soca, steelpan, zouk, kompa, and the merengue/méringue split — for tempo, form, and harmony defaults. It complements [west-african-rhythm.md](west-african-rhythm.md) (the deeper cross-rhythm substrate), [electronic-and-dance.md](electronic-and-dance.md) (the loop-and-mute arrangement pattern dub anticipated by roughly a decade), and [effects-and-mixing.md](effects-and-mixing.md) (the delay/reverb mechanics dub exploits).

## Mento to reggae: the Jamaican lineage

### Mento (19th century-1950s): the folk root
Mento is Jamaica's indigenous folk dance music, documented from the 19th century and commercially peaking in the 1940s-50s before ska displaced it; it was often marketed abroad as "calypso" despite being musically distinct from Trinidadian calypso. It runs a steady 2/4 or 4/4 at a moderate tempo, roughly 90-120 BPM, on acoustic guitar, banjo, hand drums, and the rhumba box — a large box-mounted lamellophone (plucked-key bass, related to the West African mbira family) that carries the bass line by being sat on and played. Popular sources describe mento as fusing African rhythmic sensibilities with European (quadrille-derived) melodic and harmonic material; treat the specific causal lineage as an informed generalization rather than a settled ethnomusicological claim — [west-african-rhythm.md](west-african-rhythm.md) flags exactly this kind of pat "African rhythm" shorthand as suspect (per Agawu), and the more rigorously documented thread is narrower: Kenneth Bilby's research traces a specific, sourced line from African-Jamaican hand-drumming traditions (Kumina, Burru, and the Rastafari Nyahbinghi drumming that grew from them) into ska- and reggae-era studio percussion, distinct from any broad-brush claim about mento's own origins.

### Ska (c. 1959-1966): the afterbeat arrives
Ska emerged in Kingston in the late 1950s, fusing mento/calypso with American R&B and jazz. Its defining features: a guitar or piano "skank" — a short upstroke chop on the off-beats — horns (trumpet, trombone, saxophone) doubling or riffing on that off-beat comp, and a walking bass line. The drum kit pattern is kick on beats 1 and 3 with a snare backbeat on 2 and 4 (a shuffle-adjacent feel distinct from reggae's later one-drop). Tempo runs roughly 120-150 BPM for original Jamaican ska (the Skatalites' "Ska Boss" clocks in at 128 BPM); the UK 2 Tone revival (late 1970s) sat in a similar 120-140 band, and 1990s US third-wave ska sped up further to 150-190+. Many early sides were cover versions of obscure American R&B songs (e.g. "My Boy Lollipop," 1964) — Jamaica's effective absence of enforced copyright and the audience's taste for hearing local versions of foreign tunes (Manuel & Marshall 2006) is the direct historical seed of the cover/version culture that riddim reuse later formalizes into a system.

### Rocksteady (1966-1968): slowing down, bass moves to the front
A short-lived transitional style — roughly two years at its commercial peak — rocksteady drops tempo to about 70-90 BPM (sources place the range anywhere from 76-100) and thins the band, with less horn presence than ska. The structurally important change is the bass: rocksteady replaces ska's walking eighth-note bass with repeated, riff-like ostinato figures — fatter, darker, looser in tone — establishing the bass-as-riff norm that reggae inherits and that the riddim system will later treat as a track's stable identity.

### Reggae (from c. 1968): the one-drop and its variants
Reggae's drum-pattern family, all counted in 4/4:

- **One drop** — kick drum and snare (usually a cross-stick/rimshot) strike together on beat 3 only; beat 1 carries no kick at all — "the one is dropped." Hi-hat plays steady eighth notes, often with a shuffled feel. Popularized by Carlton Barrett of the Wailers (originator credit is disputed among Barrett, his brother Aston Barrett, and Winston Grennan). Tempo runs roughly 60-90 BPM, with several independent practitioner sources converging on 75-80 as the "classic" sweet spot — much above 90 the beat-1 silence stops registering as a felt gap. Note a real discrepancy in how sources describe this pattern: Wayne Marshall, in both the academic paper cited throughout this page and his own drum tutorial, glosses the same groove as "a kick drum (and often a snare) on beats 2 and 4," and Manuel & Marshall (2006) likewise write "kicks on beats two and four" for classic one-drop riddims. This most likely reflects describing the same physical pattern in a felt half-time, two-bar count (where a "beat" spans what other transcriptions call a full bar) rather than a different pattern in absolute time; for engine purposes, the actionable, unambiguous grid position is beat 3 of a single 4/4 bar, which is how the great majority of drum-programming sources (and this page) specify it.
- **Rockers** — denser than one-drop: kick fills in beat 1 as well as 3 (kick on 1 and 3), with the snare/rimshot accent still landing on 3, hi-hat in eighths. Crystallized by Sly Dunbar on the Mighty Diamonds' "Right Time" (1976, Channel One Studios) — Dunbar's syncopated kick/rim combination reportedly convinced some early listeners it was a sound effect rather than a drum part. Associated with a harder, more "militant" roots-reggae sound than one-drop.
- **Steppers** — kick on all four quarter-note beats, i.e. four-on-the-floor: the most driving, relentless variant, growing out of the late-1970s Channel One/rockers movement and becoming a staple of UK sound-system roots reggae. (A minority of sources swap the rockers/steppers labels; the descriptions above follow the majority of independent tutorial sources and are internally consistent with "four on the floor" being the name reserved for steppers specifically.)
- **The skank chop and the bubble organ.** The guitar/keyboard skank is a short, muted upstroke on the off-beat (the "and" of each quarter note), descended from the shuffle-guitar practice of 1940s-60s Jamaican blues parties. A denser "bubble" variant plays continuous 16th-note offbeat chords or arpeggios on organ — an "organ shuffle" closely associated with Studio One's musical director Jackie Mittoo, who as staff arranger fused Hammond-organ shuffle patterns onto rocksteady- and reggae-era rhythm sections.
- **Bassline as melody.** Reggae basslines function as a second vocal line rather than mere harmonic support: repetitive, singable, riff-based, favoring root/fifth motion colored by pentatonic (major or minor) runs, and mixed forward and "fat" relative to the rest of the band. This bass-forward mix balance, combined with the low-frequency timing-precision findings in [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md), is a large part of why reggae reads as groove-forward even at slow tempos.

## Riddim culture: template-based composition at industrial scale

This is the conceptual center of the page. A **riddim** is, in Manuel & Marshall's definition, "an autonomous accompanimental track, typically based on an ostinato" — not exclusive to any one song. "The riddim has its own name, its own producer and owner, and its own musical life independent of particular voicings." A dancehall song is built from two separable layers: the riddim (bass, drums, and often a chordal or melodic ostinato) and the **voicing** — a singer or deejay's lyrics and vocal melody performed over it. The same riddim recurs, largely unchanged, under dozens or hundreds of different voicings; more rarely, the same voicing is later reissued over a different riddim entirely. Riddim and voicing are composed, contracted, and often credited separately.

The system's roots reach back to 1950s Jamaica, where dances were driven by sound systems playing records rather than by live bands — Manuel & Marshall note the explicit contrast with the Dominican Republic, where 1950s dances featured live accordion-based merengue combos. Ska-era cover culture, Coxsone Dodd recording a vocalist over an existing imported record (early 1960s), and the rise of the deejay/toaster (King Stitt in the late 1960s, then U-Roy, produced by King Tubby, from around 1970) converged around 1970 in the convention of releasing an instrumental "version" on a single's B-side for toasting over. By 1980 this riddim-plus-voicing format was the dominant idiom in Jamaica, and "dancehall" became the name for this performance-oriented mode, as against the internationally circulated, more conventionally song-structured "roots" reggae of Bob Marley and Jimmy Cliff.

Canonical riddims: **Real Rock** (Sound Dimension, Studio One, 1967, notated at a quarter-note tempo of 80, built on the classic skank-plus-one-drop template) is documented on 269 separate recordings by the discography site reggae-riddims.com; the 1967 **Answer** riddim carries 249. **Sleng Teng** (1985; see Dancehall below) is credited with "a few hundred" versions (180 per the same discography). A riddim's name usually comes from its originating song (Real Rock) but sometimes shifts to a later, more popular cut recorded over it — the "Mad Mad" riddim (1967) is also known as "Diseases," "Johnny Dollar," and "Golden Hen" after three later hits built on it. Compositionally, a "beat-maker" builds and owns a riddim, then contracts deejays to voice on it for a flat fee and/or a rights split; by the 1990s-2000s a given scene typically has only a dozen or so riddims in active circulation at any time, released as multi-artist compilation CDs (Greensleeves, VP) with up to twenty different vocalists' cuts, and "juggled" — mixed together as a medley — by club selectors. Because riddim and voicing are independently composed, deejays' simple one- to three-note "reciting tone" melodies sometimes sit in genuine tonal tension with a riddim's actual key: Manuel & Marshall document Papa San singing a major-third-centered tune over the minor-key "Just Be Good to Me" riddim, and Buju Banton centering a tune on C over the F-major "Nanny Goat" ostinato — mismatches the scene tolerates and, by some accounts, does not even register as a flaw, because riddim and voicing are heard as genuinely separable layers rather than a fused composition.

Many riddims are not endless single loops but carry two or three sections in which instruments drop in and out — form is created, not just decorated, by which channels are active — and Manuel & Marshall trace this directly to dub practice (below). The system has drawn real criticism from within the scene (producer Gussie Clarke and drummer Sly Dunbar, both quoted in Lloyd Bradley's *This Is Reggae Music*, call excessive riddim reuse a driver of "more followers than leaders"), but its defenders note that top deejays record 30-40 songs a year across many riddims, and that hearing several radically different voicings ride one riddim is part of the pleasure for a dancehall audience, not a compromise. The lesson for an engine is precise: this is industrial-scale template-based composition — a fixed, ownable instrumental identity deliberately decoupled from the melodic/lyrical layer generated on top of it, practiced culturally at a scale (hundreds of "covers" of one backing track) with no real Western equivalent. Manuel & Marshall's paper is literally titled "the riddim method" because it names a *method*, not a genre trait.

## Dub: the studio as instrument

The standard historiography places dub's origin in 1968 at Duke Reid's Treasure Isle studio: engineer Byron Smith accidentally omitted the vocal from an acetate, sound-system operator Ruddy Redwood played the instrumental "version" anyway, and the crowd's enthusiasm revealed a market for stripped-down instrumental sides. King Tubby (Osbourne Ruddock) generalized the accident into a practice — dramatically remixing existing multitrack recordings by dropping instruments and vocal fragments in and out and drenching selected hits in reverb and delay, rather than simply omitting vocals. Michael Veal's *Dub: Soundscapes and Shattered Songs in Jamaican Reggae* (2007) is the standard scholarly account: Jamaican engineers — King Tubby, Errol Thompson, Lee "Scratch" Perry — treated the studio's mixing and signal-processing gear as an instrument of composition and real-time improvisation in its own right, a transformation Veal frames as directly analogous to hip-hop's later turn of the turntable into an instrument. Dub's aesthetic, on Veal's reading, is one of deliberately "shattered songs": a familiar multitrack recording is taken apart into its component tracks and reassembled as a new, mix-driven structure.

The concrete, literally implementable moves:

- **Mute-matrix arrangement as form.** The mixing desk's channel faders and mutes are played live, dropping instruments and vocal phrases in and out across a track — the sequence of which channels are open *is* the form. This is the same move [electronic-and-dance.md](electronic-and-dance.md) documents as EDM's loop-plus-mute-automation workflow, arrived at independently roughly a decade earlier.
- **Reverb throws as events.** King Tubby's signature technique: open an auxiliary reverb send for a fraction of a beat on a single hit (often the snare), then close it — the spring reverb's metallic decay continues after the dry hit has stopped, producing a sudden "thunderclap bloom." Tubby favored a valve-driven Pioneer SR-101 spring unit and was known to physically strike the spring pan itself for a percussive crash.
- **Tape-delay echo tails as transitions.** The Roland RE-201 "Space Echo" — tape delay plus an onboard spring reverb tank — became the standard Jamaican and UK dub-studio unit from the 1970s onward; feeding a vocal phrase or drum hit into a long, regenerating delay produces a trailing echo tail that bridges, or substitutes for, a formal transition. See [effects-and-mixing.md](effects-and-mixing.md) for the tempo-synced and filtered-feedback delay mechanics an engine would use to realize this.
- **Selective filtering.** Lee "Scratch" Perry's characteristic move at his Black Ark studio: route the full drum-and-bass bus through a spring reverb, then roll off the low end of the wet return before blending it back in, for a controlled metallic drum texture; more generally, dub engineers sweep a high-pass or low-pass filter across a channel to thin or darken it as a transition device rather than a static EQ setting.
- **Extreme, improvisatory processing.** Perry also stretched non-musical sounds — animal noises, spoken fragments — through delay and reverb for texture, and favored tape saturation and unconventional mic placement over hi-fi correctness. Scientist (Hopeton Brown), King Tubby's engineering successor, extended the same vocabulary into the early-1980s digital era.

Because dub is fundamentally a remix practice — recomposing an existing multitrack via the mixing desk rather than writing new material — Manuel & Marshall place it as the direct ancestor of the layers-dropping-in-and-out arrangement style now standard across riddims generally, and note that Jamaican deejays were toasting over records and treating turntables as instruments roughly a decade before their counterparts in the Bronx — a plausible, though more speculative, precursor line to hip-hop and remix/mash-up culture broadly (see [hip-hop-and-beat-making.md](hip-hop-and-beat-making.md)).

## Dancehall: the digital turn

In 1985, Wayne Smith's "Under Mi Sleng Teng" — produced by Prince Jammy (later King Jammy) with programmer Noel Davey — was built entirely on digital keyboards. Davey, given a consumer Casio MT-40 instead of the promised Yamaha DX7, rediscovered the keyboard's built-in "rock" bass preset (composed in 1980 by Casio sound engineer Okuda Hiroko for a different market entirely) and built the whole riddim from it, with no live musicians. It premiered in a February 1985 sound-clash against the Black Scorpio system; the crowd's demand to hear it replayed repeatedly signaled the hit before the record was even pressed. Manuel & Marshall frame its significance in three parts: it triggered the wholesale shift from studio-band riddims to short, digitally sequenced ostinatos; it proved any producer with a keyboard, sequencer, and drum machine could build a marketable riddim without studio time or musicians (the trend the UK calls "ragga"); and Sleng Teng itself became a riddim reused in hundreds of later songs.

Since the early 1990s, most dancehall riddims have moved from the classic roots skank-and-one-drop template toward a **3+3+2 pulse** at roughly **90-110 BPM** (Manuel & Marshall, surveying riddims from 1990-2003; they place contemporaneous soca, by comparison, at a faster 130-160 BPM — more recent scene usage splits soca further into "groovy soca" at 120-135 and "power soca" at 155-163). The 3+3+2 cell is the same tresillo family documented in [west-african-rhythm.md](west-african-rhythm.md) and standard across Latin rhythmic theory: three onsets grouped 3+3+2 across eight eighth-notes — beat 1, the "and" of beat 2, and beat 4 — with kick and snare/clap distributed across that skeleton in ways that vary by producer (some add a fuller four-on-the-floor kick beneath the 3+3+2 accent pattern, others keep it sparse). This pulse is the direct ancestor of **dembow**: Steely & Clevie's "Poco Man Jam"/"Fish Market" riddim (1989) underlies both Shabba Ranks' "Dem Bow" (1990, produced by Bobby Digital) and Dennis "the Menace" Thompson's "Pounder," together credited with the rhythmic core of the great majority of reggaetón productions once the sound traveled — via Spanish-language dancehall in Panama — to Puerto Rico, where reggaetón crystallized through the 1990s (see [latin-american-traditions.md](latin-american-traditions.md) for the Latin American side of this lineage). Contemporary reggaetón/dembow production typically runs 85-100+ BPM, commonly realized as a four-on-the-floor kick plus snare/clap accents on the tresillo's offbeat positions — practitioners often gloss the feel as "boom-ch-boom-chick." By the 2000s the Korg Triton and Akai MPC were the dominant dancehall studio tools, and software (Reason, then Pro Tools/Nuendo) displaced tape despite many engineers' preference — voiced by Sly Dunbar, among others — for tape's "warm, round" sound over digital's "cold and harsh" character.

## The wider Caribbean

**Calypso and soca (Trinidad and Tobago).** Calypso is an Afro-Trinidadian genre traceable to early-to-mid-19th-century Kaiso and French-Antillean plantation antecedents, built on call-and-response singing over a syncopated, offbeat-accented 2/4 or moderate 4/4 (individual calypso recordings span roughly 100-130 BPM; no single genre-wide figure is well established, and any more precise number should be treated as unverified). Its harmony is comparatively functional: verses commonly cycle a diatonic I-IV-V progression, with ii appearing as a secondary predominant chord in many changes — a more cadential, directionally resolving harmonic language than reggae or dancehall's static vamps. The standard ensemble is drum kit, Latin hand percussion (congas, bongos, timbales), bass, guitar, and a horn section that both punctuates phrases and states riffs. Soca ("the soul of calypso") is Lord Shorty's early-1970s fusion, layering Indo-Trinidadian rhythm instruments (dholak, dhantal) onto Afro-Creole calypso — an early, self-conscious act of genre hybridization worth reading against [hybridization-and-fusion.md](hybridization-and-fusion.md). Contemporary usage splits soca into "groovy soca" (120-135 BPM) and the faster, denser, horn- and synth-driven "power soca" (155-163 BPM), a considerably more relentless dance genre than calypso.

**Steelpan ensembles and the Panorama form.** Steelpan was invented in Trinidad in the 1930s-40s from tuned oil-drum percussion (the tenor pan is attributed to Winston "Spree" Simon, the tenor-bass pan to Ellie Mannette), and a full steel orchestra can run to a hundred or more players organized into a frontline of tenor/lead pans (melody, roughly C4-E6), a mid-range of double seconds and guitar pans (chordal and countermelodic support), and background bass pans (tenor bass, six-bass, nine-bass) carrying harmony and low end. Panorama, Trinidad Carnival's steelband competition, is the form-defining venue: a band arranges a single calypso or soca tune into an extended orchestral work, conventionally capped around eight to ten minutes, following a fairly fixed template — an introduction (roughly one-tenth of the total length), a statement of the melody, then several passes of increasingly developed variation on a short motif (each roughly 60-90 seconds, typically starting with a lower-register melody restatement and proceeding to a favorite phrase developed, modulated, and passed across sections), and a finale. This is a fixed formal template for elaborating one short tune at length, independent of steelpan timbre itself, and directly comparable to the density-ratio elaboration in [gamelan.md](gamelan.md) or the unfolding structure of a raga performance in [indian-classical-music.md](indian-classical-music.md).

**Zouk and kompa (Haiti, Guadeloupe, Martinique).** Kompa (konpa in Haitian Creole, also compas) emerged in 1950s-60s Haiti under real influence from Dominican merengue (Averill 1997), riding a steady 4/4 pulse carried by the tanbou drum; one non-scholarly practitioner source describes a characteristic five-strokes/three-strokes ("5-3") division of hand-and-stick drumming on the tanbou, a specific claim this page has not been able to corroborate against a second, more rigorous source and that should be treated as an informed starting point rather than settled fact. Zouk, pioneered by the Guadeloupean/Martinican band Kassav' in the early 1980s, deliberately synthesizes kompa, kadans (cadence-lypso), biguine, and gwo ka (the Guadeloupean drum-and-song tradition) with drum machines, synthesizers, and a loud horn section — another clear, named case of studio-era genre hybridization (see [hybridization-and-fusion.md](hybridization-and-fusion.md)). Original "zouk béton" ran a fast 120-145 BPM; the later, softer "zouk-love" ballad style slowed the tempo and leaned further into MIDI instrumentation.

**Merengue and méringue: a Hispanophone/Francophone split.** Dominican merengue and Haitian méringue (Haitian Creole *mereng*) share 19th-century ancestry — both grew from a fusion of African-diaspora dance rhythms with the European contredanse/ballroom tradition, and both were originally carried on stringed instruments (bandurria and guitar) — but diverged into distinct national musics, a divergence sharpened by mid-20th-century politics (the Trujillo regime explicitly promoted merengue as a Europe-adjacent Dominican national symbol over rival Afro-Dominican forms like palo). Modern Dominican merengue is built on the tambora, a two-headed drum played simultaneously with a stick on one head and the open hand on the other, articulating a syncopated figure called the quintillo, in a fast 2/4; instrumentation ranges from the rural *perico ripiao* style (accordion, tambora, güira scraper) to horn-driven big-band orchestra merengue. This page could not confirm a reliable, source-verified comparison of merengue's and méringue's relative tempo or character — the secondary sources consulted contradict each other on this point — and flags it as an open question below rather than asserting a direction.

## Harmonic and formal norms across these traditions

Reggae and dancehall genuinely favor one- and two-chord vamps and short ostinato loops over functional progressions — this is not folklore but a directly documented pattern: Manuel & Marshall's transcriptions show riddim chordal ostinatos "typically alternate a major tonic chord with ii, IV, V or ♭VII," almost always as one repeated pair of chords rather than a directional sequence, and they note many dancehall deejays voice simple one- to three-note "reciting tone" melodies that are harmonically indifferent to whatever chord content the riddim actually carries. Calypso, by contrast, is more harmonically directional, built on functional I-IV-V(-ii) cycles with real cadential motion — closer to Tin Pan Alley/functional-tonal practice than to reggae's vamps. Across the Jamaican lineage specifically, melodic interest concentrates in the bass: reggae and dancehall basslines are minor- or major-pentatonic and riff-based, prioritizing a small set of memorable, repeated figures over harmonic elaboration, with melodicism living in rhythm-section riffs and vocal hooks rather than chord-change density.

## Implications for generative engines

- **A riddim-template architecture generalizes this project's style packs** ([style-and-genre-overview.md](style-and-genre-overview.md), [generative-music-design-patterns.md](generative-music-design-patterns.md), pattern P4). Define a riddim object as an identity layer — one fixed bass-line-plus-drum-pattern(-plus-chord-ostinato) bundle, versioned and named — with an interchangeable "voicing" layer (the generated melody/lead) laid fresh on top each time. One riddim object should support many generated voicings, mirroring how a single Jamaican riddim supports dozens of real songs; this is a cheap, high-value way to multiply perceived variety from one well-tuned rhythm section, and it is a more literal, historically attested version of the style-pack pattern the wiki already recommends.
- **Add one-drop, rockers, and steppers as three named entries in the drum-pattern library**, sharing one afterbeat comp lattice (skank chop on the upbeats, an optional denser bubble/organ shuffle layer) and differing only in kick placement: one-drop = kick and rimshot together on beat 3 only; rockers = kick on beats 1 and 3; steppers = kick on all four beats. Layer this project's existing groove machinery on top rather than reinventing it — [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md)'s velocity hierarchy and structured-microtiming rules apply directly (e.g. a consistently placed "laid-back" snare/rimshot offset, never uniform random jitter).
- **Implement dub as an explicit performer mode, not just a mix preset.** Give an engine: (1) a seeded mute-matrix that turns instrument buses on and off across a piece as the primary form-generating device — the same mechanism as [electronic-and-dance.md](electronic-and-dance.md)'s loop-plus-mute-automation arrangement, one decade earlier and manually operated; (2) scheduled, sparse "reverb-throw" and delay-throw events — route a single hit to a hot send for one beat, then close it, using the send/return and tempo-synced delay mechanics in [effects-and-mixing.md](effects-and-mixing.md) — deployed specifically as transition markers between sections; (3) a filter-sweep move that thins a loop before dropping it out or brightens it on reintroduction, echoing the build-up filter sweeps in [electronic-and-dance.md](electronic-and-dance.md) but usable as a transition at any point, not only before a drop.
- **Set tempo and feel defaults per subgenre** from the ranges verified above: mento ~90-120 BPM; ska ~120-150 (afterbeat comp, walking bass); rocksteady ~70-90 (riff bass); reggae one-drop ~60-90, 75-80 as a classic default; rockers/steppers slightly brisker within the same band; dancehall/dembow ~90-110 (3+3+2 pulse), shading toward 85-100+ in reggaetón-adjacent production; calypso a moderate, imprecisely-documented tempo below soca; soca 120-135 (groovy) to 155-163 (power); zouk béton 120-145; merengue a fast 2/4 (specific BPM not confirmed — see Open questions).
- **Default reggae/dancehall harmony to one- or two-chord vamps**, deliberately resisting this project's classical-engine instinct toward functional progressions ([harmony.md](harmony.md)) — a static or two-chord ostinato is period-correct here, not a simplification failure. Reserve directional I-IV-V(-ii) motion for a calypso/soca style pack instead.
- **Give the bass a riff, not a walking line, in reggae/dancehall/rocksteady packs.** Compose (or select from a small figure library) a short, repeated, pentatonic-leaning bass figure as the piece's second melody and primary groove anchor before generating anything else in the riddim, consistent with the bass-precision findings in [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md).
- **Treat the Panorama form as a reusable elaboration template independent of timbre**: a fixed-ratio introduction, a melody statement, several passes of increasingly developed variation on a short motif, and a finale — usable as a form archetype alongside the gamelan and raga elaboration patterns already documented in this wiki.

## Open questions

- A reliable, source-verified comparison of Dominican merengue vs. Haitian méringue tempo/character: the secondary sources consulted here contradict each other (one claims merengue is the slower of the pair, which reads backward against merengue's common characterization as a very fast genre) — needs Austerlitz (1997, on merengue) and Averill (1997, on Haiti) actually read in full before this wiki asserts a direction.
- A precise, source-verified BPM range for calypso as a genre (as distinct from individual songs, or from soca, its faster descendant) was not found in this pass.
- The kompa "5-3" tanbou stroke pattern rests on a single non-scholarly source and needs a second, ideally scholarly, confirmation before being treated as settled.
- How much of the popular "African-derived afterbeat" narrative for mento/ska is well-evidenced specific lineage — per Bilby's documented Kumina/Burru/Nyahbinghi-to-reggae hand-drumming throughline — versus a looser, less rigorously sourced generalization, deserves a dedicated future pass reading Bilby (2010) and Veal (2007) in full.
- Whether an engine should ever model the riddim/voicing tonal "looseness" Manuel & Marshall document (deliberately letting a generated melody sit slightly out of key with its riddim) as an authentic stylistic feature, or whether it would just read as a bug to listeners outside the tradition, is untested.
