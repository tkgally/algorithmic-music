# Southeast Asian traditions

This page covers the mainland and maritime Southeast Asian traditions outside Java and Bali: Thai classical music (piphat, mahori, khruang sai), Khmer pinpeat, Burmese hsaing waing and saung gauk, Vietnamese chamber and solo traditions, Lao/Isan khene music, and Philippine kulintang, plus the region's major hybrid popular genres. [Gamelan](gamelan.md) already covers colotomic gong cycles and density-ratio elaboration for Java/Bali; the traditions here share gamelan's core insight — one skeletal melody, many simultaneous idiomatic realizations — under the more precise name ethnomusicologist Mantle Hood gave it, *polyphonic stratification*, while contributing material gamelan does not: contested "equidistant" tunings that reward the same caution as raga and maqam ([tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md)), a literal algorithmic variation form (Thai *thao*), a tuned-drum lead voice that is simultaneously melodic and percussive (Burmese *pat waing*), a monochord whose pitch material is entirely bent harmonics (Vietnamese *đàn bầu*), and a modal system in which ornament is not decoration but a defining component of mode itself (Vietnamese *điệu*).

## Thai classical music: the piphat ensemble and polyphonic stratification

*Piphat* is the core Thai ensemble, sized to occasion. The smallest, *piphat khrueang ha* ("five-instrument"), pairs *pi nai* (quadruple-reed oboe) with *ranat ek* (high xylophone), *khong wong yai* (a circle of tuned gong-kettles surrounding a seated player), *taphon* (a hand-played barrel drum) or other Thai drums, *klong that* (a pair of large stick-beaten barrel drums), and *ching* (small cup cymbals). *Piphat khrueang khu* ("paired") adds the lower *ranat thum* xylophone and the higher-pitched *khong wong lek* gong circle; *piphat khrueang yai* ("large") adds metal-keyed xylophone counterparts (Wikipedia, Piphat). Two related ensembles use the same skeletal-melody logic with different color: *mahori*, historically a women's court ensemble, softens piphat by dropping the piercing pi and adding the bowed and plucked strings of *khruang sai* — *saw duang* and *saw u* (high and low two-string fiddles), *jakhe* (plucked floor zither), *khlui* (duct flute) — for intimate indoor entertainment (Wikipedia, Mahori; Khrueang sai).

The ensemble's texture is the region's paradigm case of *polyphonic stratification*, a term Mantle Hood coined specifically to distinguish Southeast Asian gong-chime practice from generic "heterophony": each instrument does not merely add light ornament to a shared line but renders it through a fully idiomatic, register- and technique-specific transformation — thang — while all versions stay traceable to one skeleton. The *khong wong yai* carries that skeleton at moderate density, close in function to gamelan's balungan ([gamelan](gamelan.md)); *ranat ek* spins a faster, octave-leaping elaboration; *ranat thum* answers in syncopated counter-rhythm below it; the *pi nai*, freed from fixed pitch by circular breathing, glides between the skeleton's notes rather than sitting on them. Underneath, the *taphon* and *klong* play *na thap* — named, cyclically repeating drum-stroke patterns, closed by a *mong* gong stroke — that function less like a nested colotomic hierarchy and more like a library of formal signals: specific na thap patterns mark phrase endings, cue accelerandos, and signal transitions between sections, while the *ching* cymbals' alternating damped and open strokes mark the underlying pulse beneath them. Ensemble, na thap library, and thang-based part-writing are documented together as a system in Thai music pedagogy (Fiveable/Melodigging syntheses of standard Thai-music coursework; treated cautiously here as they are not peer-reviewed, but they agree with the more scattered scholarly citations found).

## Is Thai (and Burmese) tuning really equidistant?

Thai music is routinely described as built on seven *equidistant* steps of 1200/7 ≈ 171.4 cents — a claim worth exactly the scrutiny [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md) gives raga shrutis and maqam quarter-tones. The claim traces to Alexander Ellis's 1885 acoustic survey, reportedly built on a Siamese prince's description of the scale's intervals as "identically the same." David Morton's 1974 acoustic measurements of Thai xylophones found real instruments deviating from perfect 171.4-cent steps by only about ±5 cents on average — remarkably close for tuning done entirely by ear, and the finding that cemented "Thai = 7-TET" in textbooks. But Morton's averaging across instruments is itself the problem later scholars raise: John Garzoli's "The Myth of Equidistance in Thai Tuning" (*Analytical Approaches to World Music* 4/2, 2015) argues equidistance is a "theoretical ideal" that individual tuners do not actually target, with measured intervals reportedly varying by 10–20 cents depending on teacher, school, and region — averaging many such idiosyncratic tunings together manufactures a uniformity that no single ensemble exhibits. Jay Rahn's "'Equiheptatonic' Tuning in Thai Classical Music" (*Music & Science* 2, 2019, DOI 10.1177/2059204318802505), analyzing 28 Thai ensembles, found that single-step intervals form overlapping chains of sizes rather than falling cleanly into "small" and "large" categories, and proposed weaker "grid," "ruler," and Rothenberg-style "strict propriety" models as better fits than strict equidistance. This session could not obtain full text of either paper directly (PDF/paywall access failed); the summary above rests on abstracts and secondary reporting — worth a direct read before treating the specific cent figures as settled.

The same caution turns out to apply across the border: Robert Garfias's primary-source study of Burmese tuning ("Preliminary Thoughts on Burmese Modes," *Asian Music* 7/1, 1975, read in full for this page) measured seven-tone Hsaing-ensemble tunings with individual steps ranging roughly 143–204 cents against an idealized 171.4 — and Garfias himself, thirty years before Garzoli, wrote that it is "perhaps too tempting" to call the Burmese seven-tone system equidistant, noting the third degree runs a little low, the fourth a little high, and the seventh somewhat low relative to the Western diatonic series, for an overall impression of "neutral" intervals rather than a mathematical grid (see the Burmese section below). Three unrelated traditions on this page — Thai, Burmese, and (below) Philippine kulintang — each carry a folk or textbook claim of "roughly equal" tuning that closer measurement complicates. Treat "equidistant N-tone" claims anywhere in Southeast Asian sources as a starting hypothesis, not a specification.

## Thao: a melody performed at three densities

*Thao* ("stacked" or layered) is a Thai formal type built directly from the same density logic gamelan expresses through *irama* ([gamelan](gamelan.md)) — but packaged as a fixed, three-part composition rather than a continuously cued live transition. A thao piece presents one melodic identity in three named tempo/density levels, performed in a set sequence:

| Level | Relative tempo | Character |
|---|---|---|
| sam chan ("three-level") | slowest (½×) | broadest, most expanded and ornamented |
| song chan ("two-level") | medium (reference tempo) | the piece's "normal" form |
| chan dio ("one-level") | fastest (2×) | terse, compressed, driving |

Each level shares the same underlying melodic-structural skeleton; what changes is how many surface notes fill each skeletal beat, exactly gamelan's irama principle of roughly constant surface activity traded against tempo. Sources differ on compositional direction — some describe court composers "disguising" a known short tune (chan dio) by augmenting it outward into song chan and sam chan; others describe reduction from a song-chan reference down to chan dio — but the audible result is consistent: a full thao performance moves from slow/broad to fast/tight, an acceleration curve with a family resemblance to jo-ha-kyu's "build then rush to the end" (see [east-asian-traditions](east-asian-traditions.md)), arrived at by mechanism (fixed pre-composed density variants) rather than by a live intensity curve.

## Khmer pinpeat: the piphat system's close sibling

Cambodia's *pinpeat* is close enough to Thai piphat in instrumentation and function that Terry Miller and Sam-Ang Sam devoted a comparative study to distinguishing them (Miller & Sam, "The Classical Musics of Cambodia and Thailand: A Study of Distinctions," *Ethnomusicology* 39/2, 1995) — the two courts' repertoires share deep historical roots and cross-influenced each other repeatedly. The full ensemble (*pinpeat vong thum*) pairs *roneat ek* and *roneat thung* (high and low xylophones) with *kong vong thom* and *kong vong touch* (large and small gong circles), the piercing quadruple-reed *sralai* carrying the lead melodic line, paired barrel drums (*sampho*, *skor thom*), and *chhing* cymbals for timekeeping (Sam-Ang Sam, "The Pin Peat Ensemble: Its History, Music, and Context"; Wikipedia, Cambodian classical music). Cambodian sources report the same "roughly equidistant heptatonic, ≈171 cents per step" tuning claim made for Thai piphat, unverified here at the same measurement-versus-folklore level discussed above — treat it with the same caution. Two further Khmer genres are worth a line each: *smot* is unaccompanied solo Buddhist chant, the slowest and most ornamented of Khmer vocal styles, performed in free rhythm rather than metric time; *phleng kar* is Khmer wedding music, a fixed sequence of named songs, one per ritual stage across a traditional multi-day ceremony.

## Burmese hsaing waing: melodic percussion and modes that retune the ensemble

The *hsaing waing* ensemble is built around an instrument with no real equivalent elsewhere on this page: *pat waing*, a horseshoe of 18–21 individually tuned drums surrounding a seated player, spanning "a range of more than 3 octaves" (Wikipedia, Hsaing waing) — a full melodic instrument made of membranes, not bars or gongs. Pat waing tuning by applied paste is reportedly the last surviving Southeast Asian use of an Indian drum-tuning method (Wikipedia, Hsaing waing). Around it, *kyi waing* (a circle of small bronze gongs) and the *hne* (double-reed oboe) each carry the same tune in their own idiom; the composite texture is "characterized by dynamic, lively and sudden contrasts and shifts in rhythm, melody and tempo" (Wikipedia, Hsaing waing) — leaping registers and syncopation rather than the smoother heterophonic surface of piphat or pinpeat.

Garfias's fieldwork (1975, read in full for this page) gives the clearest account of how Burmese modal practice actually works. Burmese musicians name seven tones by the finger-holes of the hne, counted *descending* from the tonic (*than hman*) — the reverse of Western, Japanese, and Chinese numbering, matching a melody type that "tends to descend from a high pitch to a lower one." Two distinct tunings of this seven-tone set exist side by side — one for chamber instruments (saung, patala xylophone), one for the hsaing ensemble, whose tonic sits one degree lower. Every mode uses all seven tones but treats five as primary and two as secondary; the *pat waing* and *saung* physically retune between modes (the drums' lower range and the harp's strings are set to the five primary pitches, with secondary tones reached by hand-stopping), while the fixed-pitch *patala*, *kyi waing*, and gong-frame *maung waing* keep all seven pitches available yet still play the five primary tones in the low register by convention, reserving the other two for the upper register, where they take on "the character of passing tones, suspensions and appoggiaturas" — a pentatonic-core-within-a-heptatonic-gamut logic with real parallels to Japanese in/yo and Chinese gong-mode selection ([east-asian-traditions](east-asian-traditions.md)). At cadences, Burmese practice pairs each goal tone with a fixed consonant support a fifth below it (named *ado:*/*adaw*), alternating the two in the approach to a phrase ending so that instruments taking different melodic paths through a phrase land on the same goal pitch at the same instant — the same "diverge in the middle, align at the seams" logic as gamelan's convergence on the *seleh* tone ([gamelan](gamelan.md)). (Some secondary descriptions of this practice reportedly use the term "coterminous cadence"; that specific label could not be independently verified in the sources reached this session — see Open questions.) Deliberately, voice and hne parts run a hair off the fixed-pitch instruments' tuning in lightly ornamented passages — Garfias calls this "a matter of preference rather than a simple necessity," the same kind of controlled fine divergence that carries pathet in Javanese elaborating parts.

*Saung gauk*, the arched harp, is Myanmar's national instrument and — by frequently repeated but not independently verified claim — one of the world's oldest continuously played harp forms. Its modal fundamentals are conventionally described as forming a circle of fifths across all seven scale degrees, though in practice most harpists today use only four of the seven (Hnyin Loun, Auk Pyan, Pule, Myin Saing); moving between them, the player retunes strings by ear toward pure fifths above the new fundamental rather than reading from any fixed equal-step gamut (Garfias 1975). Muriel Williamson's monograph *The Burmese Harp: Its Classical Music, Tunings, and Modes* (2000) is the standard specialist source on this system; it was not independently reviewed for this page. Burmese music generally remains thin in accessible English-language scholarship — Garfias's 1975 fieldwork article is, remarkably, still one of the most detailed primary sources easily reachable online fifty years later, and claims here should be read with that scarcity in mind.

## Vietnamese traditions: the bend as the unit of pitch

The *đàn bầu* is Vietnam's monochord: a single string runs from a curved buffalo-horn or wooden rod (*cần đàn*) at one end to a small gourd resonator, tuned (in modern practice) to roughly C3 (~131 Hz). The right hand's fifth finger touches the string lightly at one of seven harmonic nodes — conventionally described as sounding the octave, fifth, fourth, major third, minor third, and two flatter "septimal" intervals above the unsounded fundamental — while plucking with the same hand; the left hand continuously flexes the rod to bend pitch up or down from each harmonic anchor and to add vibrato (Wikipedia, Đàn bầu; Lark in the Morning). The instrument is historically associated with blind itinerant musicians and, while its earliest written record dates only to 1770, is believed by scholars to be considerably older. The generative-design implication is unusually direct: every đàn bầu note is inherently a bend — there is no fixed-pitch "plain" state to decorate, only a harmonic anchor and a continuous trajectory through and around it.

The *đàn tranh* zither complements this with a different bend mechanism: originally 14 strings (13th century), it grew to 16 (19th century, standard into the 1970s–80s) and then to a 17-string standard, with modern virtuoso variants up to 21 or 25 strings (master Nguyễn Vĩnh Bảo). Movable inverted-V bridges set a base pentatonic tuning per string; the left hand then presses, slides, and vibrates behind the bridge — *nhấn* (press/bend), *rung* (vibrato), *vuốt* (slide), *vỗ* (tap) — to reach pitches the open strings do not have (Wikipedia, Đàn tranh). Both instruments belong to *nhạc tài tử* ("music for amateurs"), the southern Vietnamese chamber tradition built on a core "five treasures" (*ngũ tuyệt*) ensemble of đàn tranh, đàn tỳ bà (lute), đàn kìm (moon lute), đàn cò (spike fiddle), and đàn tam (three-string lute), recognized by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage. Tài tử's modal system, *điệu*, is defined by five interacting criteria — scale, characteristic intervals, prescribed ornaments, mood, and tempo — with three principal flavors: *hơi Bắc* (bright, festive), *hơi Nam* (warm, lyrical), and *hơi Oán* (plaintive, the slowest and most heavily ornamented). This is the page's sharpest generative insight: in tài tử theory, ornamentation is not applied on top of a mode, it is part of what constitutes the mode — the same scale played with different prescribed ornament sets is understood as a different điệu (synthesis via hoangvietkhanh.com's "The Mode in Vietnamese Folk Song" and corroborating search-level summaries; this claim would benefit from a peer-reviewed primary source — e.g., Trần Văn Khê or Phong Nguyen — before being treated as fully settled).

Tài tử and its descendants open with *rao* (southern term; *dao* in the north) — an improvised, unmetered prelude that establishes the mode's characteristic tones and ornaments before the metered piece proper begins, functionally parallel to alap and taqsim ([indian-classical-music](indian-classical-music.md); [east-asian-traditions](east-asian-traditions.md)). Recordings by Nguyễn Vĩnh Bảo and Trần Văn Khê, archived at Smithsonian Folkways under titles like "Improvised Prelude in the Modal Nuance Bac of the Bac Mode," document the form directly. The best-known tài tử-descended tune is *vọng cổ* ("longing for the past"), composed in 1919 by Cao Văn Lầu in Bạc Liêu and still the backbone of *cải lương* theater music. One line on the court tradition: *nhã nhạc* ("elegant music"), Vietnam's Huế-based court music (Trần dynasty origins, codified under the Nguyễn dynasty, UNESCO-recognized 2003), draws its largest outside influence from Ming-dynasty Chinese court music with some Cham elements, and stands apart from the chamber and folk traditions above as formal, large-ensemble ceremonial music.

## Lao and Isan: the khene's self-contained polyphony

The *khene* (khaen), Laos's national instrument and, since 2017, UNESCO-recognized Intangible Cultural Heritage, packs 14–20 bamboo pipes in two rows into a central hardwood windchest, each pipe carrying one finger hole and one free metal reed. Like its East Asian free-reed relatives — the Chinese sheng, the Japanese shō ([east-asian-traditions](east-asian-traditions.md)) — each khene reed reportedly sounds on both inhalation and exhalation, letting a single player sustain drone and melody together without a breath gap: a genuinely self-contained polyphonic instrument. Repertoire is organized into five *lai* (modes) across two families — *thang san* (*lai sutsanaen*, *lai po sai*, *lai soi*) and *thang yao* (*lai yai*, *lai noi*) — each with its own characteristic drone pitch; *lai sutsanaen* is treated as the source mode from which the others derive. One line on the vocal tradition it accompanies: *mor lam* is sung poetry whose melody bends to follow the tone contours of the (tonal) Lao/Isan language text rather than a fixed tune, over the khene's steady drone-plus-ostinato.

## Philippine kulintang: rhythmic modes over ostinato

*Kulintang* names both an instrument — a row of 5 to 9 graduated pot-gongs, lowest pitch at the player's left, with no standardized tuning between physical sets — and the ensemble music built around it among the Maguindanao and Maranao peoples of Mindanao. Sources disagree on the character of that tuning: Wikipedia's synthesis describes "similar interval relationships of more or less equidistant steps," preserving melodic contour across differently tuned instruments, while Cadar and Garfias's fieldwork is reported elsewhere as finding both groups favoring *pelog*-type (markedly unequal) interval patterns — a third instance, alongside Thai and Burmese tuning above, of an "equidistant" folk description sitting uneasily against measured or expert-observed practice. The kulintang gong-row is supported by *agung* (large suspended gongs), *gandingan*, *babandil*, and the *dabakan* drum, in ensembles the Maguindanao call *basalen* and the Maranao *kolintang*; the kulintang player leads from the melody chair, functioning as conductor as much as melodist, and "determines the length of each rendition and could change the rhythm at any time, speeding up or slowing down" (Wikipedia, Kulintang).

Crucially, kulintang pieces are not fixed compositions but named *rhythmic modes* — frameworks realized fresh through improvisation over the supporting ostinati every performance; ethnomusicologist Usopay Cadar held that "improvisation is tantamount to the constitution of kulintang" itself. Maguindanao practice recognizes five genres performed in a required order (*duyug*, *sinulog*, *tidtu*, *binalig*, *tagonggo*) and a style axis from *kamamatuan* ("old" — slow, dignified) to *kagungudan* ("new" — fast, showy); Maranao practice recognizes three genres with no fixed order and a comparable *andung*/*bago* (old/new) axis, with looser latitude for variation. José Maceda's pioneering documentation (*Gongs and Bamboo: A Panorama of Philippine Musical Instruments*, 1998) described the kulintang scale as pentatonic; a 2025 re-analysis of his framework argues instead for an octatonic structure — a dichord plus two stacked trichords — producing shimmering vertical dissonance between upper and lower registers reminiscent of gamelan (Zaldua et al., "The Layers of Strata," 2025; flagged here as a recent and not yet widely cross-checked reinterpretation).

## Hybrid and popular musics

*Kroncong* is Indonesia's clearest colonial-hybrid genre and a natural companion to [hybridization-and-fusion](hybridization-and-fusion.md): Portuguese traders and sailors brought the cavaquinho (a small guitar related to the ukulele) and fado-inflected singing to Malacca and the Moluccas from around 1512, taking root among freed-slave communities such as Kampung Tugu near Jakarta; the genre modernized sharply after 1880 with the Hawaiian ukulele, crystallizing its signature interlocking *cak* (fast strummed, up to eight strokes per beat) and *cuk* (arpeggiated, tremolo) rhythm pair. Its Javanese-language offshoot, *langgam jawa*, swaps that cak-cuk interplay for orchestral arpeggios approximating a pentatonic Javanese mode when playing gamelan-derived repertoire (Wikipedia, Kroncong). Thailand's *luk thung* (post-WWII, term popularized 1964) sets rural-life lyrics and heavy vocal ornament over Western brass and electric instruments alongside khaen and phin; when Isan migration to Bangkok in the late 1970s–80s carried *mor lam* into national pop visibility (pioneered by performers like Banyen Rakgaen), the two blended into *luk thung Isan*, layering mor lam's faster rhythms onto luk thung's song forms. One line on Vietnam: *V-pop* traces to 1930s Hanoi concert songs built on newly adopted Western instruments, and since the post-1986 Đổi Mới reforms has blended the melodic sensibility of Vietnamese bolero and *cải lương* with Western and regional (Cantonese, Thai) pop production.

## Implications for generative engines

1. **Polyphonic stratification generalizes gamelan's architecture with per-voice idiom, not just density.** Where gamelan's irama multiplies one figuration rule at 1/2/4/8/16×, Thai/Khmer/Burmese practice gives each voice its *own* transformation rule (thang) applied to the same skeleton — implement as one skeletal-melody generator feeding N "instrument renderers," each a distinct rule set (register range, permitted note-density, characteristic move: octave-doubling, off-beat anticipation, passing-tone insertion), all periodically re-synchronizing on shared cadence/goal tones. See [gamelan](gamelan.md) and [texture-and-density](texture-and-density.md) for the density-ratio groundwork this extends.
2. **Thao is a ready-made variation-form template.** Generate one melodic skeleton, then instantiate it at three fixed density/tempo multipliers (½×, 1×, 2×) as three sequential formal sections, from broad to terse — a discrete, low-risk alternative to continuous development for closing out a longer piece. Pair with a jo-ha-kyu-style overall acceleration ([east-asian-traditions](east-asian-traditions.md)).
3. **Melodic percussion (pat waing) is a distinct voice-design target.** A pitched drum circle spanning 3+ octaves that carries lead melody, not just rhythm, is a genuinely different instrument-design brief from both a "drum kit" and a "mallet synth" — worth a dedicated original percussion-melodic voice with per-mode retuning as a state change. See [percussion-music](percussion-music.md) and [original-sound-design](original-sound-design.md).
4. **The đàn bầu suggests a bend-only synthesized voice.** Build a lead voice whose pitch source is a small table of harmonic-ratio anchors plus continuous parameter automation (pitch-bend/portamento) rather than discrete note-on events at fixed frequencies — every "note" is generated as an approach-and-settle trajectory around an anchor, never a flat tone. Directly extends the original-instrument program in [original-sound-design](original-sound-design.md).
5. **Ornament-defined modality (điệu) is an implementable style axis.** Encode mode not as {scale} but as {scale, required ornament function(s), tempo/mood tag} — e.g., a "hơi Oán" mode object that *mandates* slow tempo and a specific slide/vibrato profile on structural tones, so switching mode changes both pitch material and how notes are played. This extends [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md)'s "scale = pitch set + hierarchy + grammar" design lesson by making ornament a required field, not a rendering afterthought.
6. **Rhythmic-mode-over-ostinato (kulintang) is a form generator, not a fixed piece.** Architecture: k fixed-ish supporting ostinati (agung/gandingan/babandil-style) plus one lead voice that both improvises the melody and controls global tempo/section changes in real time — the same "ostinato ensemble + one free agent" pattern as [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md), here with the added twist that the free agent is also the conductor.
7. **Treat every "equidistant N-tone" claim as a per-ensemble randomized table, never a hardcoded constant.** Thai, Burmese, and kulintang tuning all carry a folklore claim of mathematical regularity that measurement complicates; following gamelan.md's practice, store cents tables with small per-"ensemble" random deviations (Burmese-measured spread ran roughly 143–204 cents around an idealized 171.4) rather than shipping one clean 7-TET-like scale for "Thai-style" or "Burmese-style" output. See [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md).
8. **Na thap and Ado:/Adaw give a small library of formal-function drum/cadence cues.** Rather than one rhythm grid, keep a short table of named stroke-patterns keyed to formal roles (opener, mid-phrase, cadence-approach, section transition) that any generator can call by function — cheaper than a full colotomic hierarchy and still legible as structure.

## Open questions

- "Coterminous cadence" (as a named term for the Burmese convergence-at-the-goal-tone practice) could not be independently verified against a reachable source this session; the underlying practice (ado:/adaw fifth-below support alternation) is confirmed via Garfias 1975, but the specific label needs a direct check against Williamson 2000 or a more recent source.
- Garzoli 2015 and Rahn 2019 (Thai tuning) were read only via abstracts/secondary summaries in this pass (direct PDF and paywalled full-text fetches both failed); their specific cent figures should be confirmed from primary text before further claims are built on them.
- The tài tử điệu = scale+ornament+mood+tempo claim rests on a specialist website synthesis rather than a peer-reviewed source; Trần Văn Khê's or Phong Nguyen's own writing (per Garland Encyclopedia of World Music vol. 4) would be the right primary check.
- Kulintang tuning: "equidistant" (Wikipedia synthesis) versus "pelog-type" (reported Cadar & Garfias) is an unresolved disagreement in the sources reached here, not just a hedge — worth chasing Cadar's dissertation or Garfias's own kulintang writing directly.
- Burmese and Khmer scholarship in accessible English is thin generally; Garfias 1975 and the Wikipedia/UNESCO layer carried most of this page's Burmese and Khmer sections respectively. A pass through Sam-Ang Sam's and Williamson's book-length works directly would substantially strengthen both sections.
- Na thap's actual stroke-pattern tables (per genre/piece, analogous to gamelan's colotomic stroke tables in [gamelan](gamelan.md)) were not found at usable granularity — a concrete table would make the engine spec in point 8 above executable rather than aspirational.
