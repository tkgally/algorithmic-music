# Percussion music

Music whose primary content is *rhythm and timbre* rather than melody or harmony is a large, coherent tradition with its own ways of building beginnings, middles, ends, and climaxes. This page maps three worlds a generative engine should span — **formal concert percussion**, **folk percussion ensembles**, and **informal drum circles** — and extracts the devices they share. It is the structural companion to [percussion-sound-design](percussion-sound-design.md) (how the voices are built) and draws the rhythm technology from [rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md), [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md), and [gamelan](gamelan.md). The founding problem it answers: **with no chords to modulate and no tune to develop, what carries a percussion piece forward?** The recurring answer is a **density/intensity curve** plus a small set of structural polarities (fixed vs. free, timeline vs. lead, unison vs. displaced).

## World 1 — concert / art percussion

The Western percussion-ensemble repertoire (born ~1930) is *through-composed* and treats duration, density, register, and timbre as the compositional material harmony would otherwise supply.

- **Varèse, *Ionisation* (1929–31)** — generally cited as the first Western concert work for percussion ensemble alone (~13 players, ~37–40 instruments; counts vary by source). Sectional and built from short **rhythmic cells** that overlap and accumulate against silence, with **metal at the timbral center** and two sirens tracing long pitch glides. Definite pitch is **withheld until the last ~17 of 91 measures**, where piano, chimes, and glockenspiel enter — the first pitch functions as a **timbral climax and arrival substituting for a harmonic cadence** (the entering chords are chromatically saturated, not tonal).
- **Cage's Constructions (1939–41)** — "square-root form": the whole is divided into the same proportions as each phrase (*First Construction (in Metal)* uses **4-3-2-3-4 = 16**, sixteen 16-bar units each itself subdivided 4-3-2-3-4). Time is articulated by **shared proportion, not pitch** — a purely rhythmic architecture.
- **Reich — process music** — *Clapping Music* (1972) is a 12-beat cell (8 claps + 4 rests, a West-African-style timeline) that one player **rotates one eighth every 12 bars until the two return to unison** (the return *is* the close). *Drumming* (1970–71, after Reich studied Ewe drumming) runs one 12/8 pattern through **phasing** and **rhythmic build-up/reduction** (substituting beats for rests to grow a pattern from a single stroke), with section seams made by **doubling** — a new timbre enters on the identical pattern as the old fades, so rhythm holds constant and only color morphs. Its arc climbs in register: skins → wood/marimba → metal/glockenspiel → tutti.
- **Xenakis** — *Pléiades* (1978–79) has **four movements defined by material** ("Mélanges/Métaux/Claviers/Peaux" — mixtures, metals, keyboards, skins), the metals played on the custom **sixxen** (19 microtonally, *unequally* tuned bars built so six players never form a unison → shimmering beats). *Persephassa* (1969) seats six players in a ring and **passes accents around the circle**, then lets them **desynchronize from unison into six independent tempi**. *Psappha* (1975) generates rhythm by **sieves** (residue classes combined with Boolean operations).
- **Chávez, *Toccata* (1942)** and **Cowell, *Ostinato Pianissimo* (1934)** — a **timbre arch** (unpitched outer movements framing a pitched center) and **coprime-length ostinato layering** (each player loops an invariant cell of a different length; the ensemble realigns only at the least common multiple).

**Making beginning–middle–end and climax without harmony.** The available axes are: **density** (simultaneous events/voices), **register spread** (low→wide→high), **dynamics** (percussion "lacking harmony must depend on dynamics"), **rhythmic complexity/ambiguity** (unison → maximally displaced → unison), **timbral accumulation** (add/withdraw instrument families; doubling), **tempo** (structural accelerando; metric modulation by simple ratio), and **process state** (how far a stated transformation has run — completion or return = the cadence). **Silence is a first-class event** (Varèse, Cage). Recurring large-forms: **process form**, **additive/build-up** (grow a pattern ±1 unit per repeat, Glass), **square-root nested proportion**, **arch / timbre-arch (ABCBA)**, **moment form** (self-contained blocks), and **theme-and-variation on a rhythmic cell** developed by **augmentation** (×2), **diminution** (÷2), **retrograde**, **displacement**, and **rotation**.

## World 2 — folk percussion ensembles

Across cultures, folk drumming converges on one architecture: **a fixed cyclic timeline + a low anchor + interlocking mid ostinati + one improvising lead + a leader who signals breaks**, with **cyclic (not developmental) form built by adding layers and intensity**. Concrete instances:

- **Afro-Cuban rumba** — organized by the two-bar, five-stroke **clave** timeline (every part phase-locks to it; misalignment is "out of clave"). Three subgenres differ mainly by meter/tempo (yambú duple/slow, guaguancó duple/medium, columbia triple/fast). The **conga trio is low-anchor + mid-lock + high-lead**: *tumba/salidor* (lowest, foundational tumbao), *tres dos* (middle, counter-clave), and the *quinto* (highest, **the improvising lead** conversing with the dancer). Form builds cumulatively: *diana* (vocal opening) → *canto* (verse) → *montuno* (call-and-response coro + lead, dancers enter, quinto and dynamics escalate — the climax). The **batá** trio (iyá/itótele/okónkolo = leader/responder/timekeeper) interlocks six drumhead pitches into a composite that "speaks."
- **Brazilian samba (a bateria)**, in 2/4 — **surdos** in three interlocking pitches (marcação lowest on the strong beat 2, resposta answering on beat 1, corte the syncopated cutter) are the bass foundation; **caixa** (snare) runs continuous sixteenths (the "engine"); **agogô** (double bell) holds a fixed syncopated timeline; **tamborim/chocalho/cuíca** add high detail; the **repinique** is the calling **lead** that, with a whistle, cues entrances and **breaks** (*paradinha* = sudden synchronized stop then explode back; *virada* = turnaround). **Samba-reggae** (Olodum, 1986) spells a *melodic bass line* out of 4–5 interlocking surdos.
- **Arabic/Turkish** — the **iqā'** (rhythmic mode, the time-counterpart of the melodic *maqām*) is a skeleton of **dum** (low), **tek** (high), and rests that percussionists **ornament in real time** (e.g. Maqsūm `D T . T D . T .`, Baladi `D D . T D . T .`, Malfūf `D . . T . . T .`). The lead **darbuka** ornaments densely; the **riq** (tambourine) often leads, signaling the cycle and phrase ends. Turkish **usul** cycles and **aksak** ("limping") additive meters juxtapose 2- and 3-cells.
- **Japanese taiko (kumi-daiko)** — modern ensemble taiko (Oguchi, 1951) sets a **ji/jiuchi** steady base pulse (shime-daiko — taiko's timeline) under a mid **chū-daiko** and a low **ō-daiko** anchor/soloist; **oroshi** is an **accelerating roll** and **jo-ha-kyū** [序破急, "slow–break–fast"] the master pacing arc (fractal, from a note to the whole program). Vocables (*kuchi shōga*) give the patterns a symbolic alphabet.

The general principles ([west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md) documents the Ewe/Mande case in depth; [gamelan](gamelan.md) the Indonesian colotomic parallel): an **inviolable timeline**, **layered interlocking ostinati (hocket)**, a **low anchor drum**, a **free lead**, a **leader who signals transitions**, **call-and-response**, **cyclic form built by layering**, and **3:2 cross-rhythm** (best modeled as cross-beats within one felt meter, not truly independent tempi — see [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md) on Agawu's caution).

## World 3 — informal drum circles

A facilitated or leaderless drum circle produces coherent music with **no score**, from **entrainment on a shared pulse**. The mechanism is the physics of coupled oscillators (Huygens, 1665 — a stronger oscillator pulls a weaker into its frequency); the musical result is that a strong common pulse locks many simple interlocking parts into a groove. Real-time form-shaping moves: the **rumble** (a free, loud wash used to build), the **stop-cut** ("stop on a dime" — everyone cut off together, then re-enter), **dynamic swells** (group crescendo/decrescendo), and **call-and-response/echo**. A **facilitator** (Arthur Hull's Village Music Circles model) *sculpts* (signals subgroups in/out) and *showcases* (solos a subgroup), then follows the "get out of the way" principle so the groove self-sustains. Instruments split into **low = pulse/heartbeat** (djembe bass stroke, dununs), **mid = groove body** (djembe tones, congas), and **high = timeline + subdivision + accents** (bells, shakers, slaps) — one djembe alone spans low/mid/high via its three strokes.

## Cross-cutting: form, meter, and a subordinate melody

- **The density/intensity arc is the primary large-scale shape.** Model the macro-form as an **intensity envelope**; tension rises with density, dynamics, register spread, and rhythmic complexity, and releases as they thin. This unifies Varèse's accumulating cells, Reich's build-up, the folk "add layers" model, and **jo-ha-kyū**.
- **The fixed-vs-free polarity organizes almost everything**: an inviolable cyclic **timeline + low anchor + interlocking mid ostinati** on one side, a **free lead/soloist + signaled breaks** on the other (clave/salidor vs quinto; agogô/surdo vs repinique; bell/dunun vs master djembe; ji vs solo; even Reich's fixed vs shifting part).
- **Meter.** **Duple (2/4, 4/4) dominates, with hypermetric phrasing in powers of two** — 4/8/16-bar groups, a turnaround/fill at bar 8 and a different one at 16 ([rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md), [phrase-structure](phrase-structure.md)). **6/8 and 12/8** carry the great **bell patterns** and 3:2 hemiola. **Additive/aksak** meters juxtapose 2- and 3-cells: 5/8 = 2+3, 7/8 = 2+2+3 (rachenitsa), 9/8 = 2+2+2+3; the *order* of cells changes the feel.
- **Fills, breaks, and transitions are the punctuation** — they mark the ends of 4/8/16-bar phrases and signal section changes (the samba *paradinha/virada*, the taiko oroshi gear-change, the drum-circle stop-cut). They are transition *tools*, not random.
- **A melodic element can accompany without leading.** Idiomatic carriers: a repeating **pitched-mallet/lamellophone ostinato** (balafon, mbira, marimba, kalimba), a **bass riff**, a **drone**, or **colotomic pitched gong punctuation** ([gamelan](gamelan.md)). Two ways to keep pitch subordinate: **reserve real melody for a central section** (Chávez's middle movement; Varèse's final bars), or keep the pitched instruments **rhythmically identical to the drums** so pitch reads as color, not tune (Reich's marimbas doubling drum patterns). Keep it sparser and quieter than the drums.

## Generative techniques this repertoire hands you

- **Euclidean rhythms E(k,n)** (Bjorklund; Toussaint) — maximal-even onset spreads reproduce authentic world timelines from two integers (E(3,8) = tresillo, E(5,8) = cinquillo, E(7,12) ≈ the Ewe bell); one cheap function is a whole library of claves and support parts ([rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md); implemented in `lib/rng.js` `euclid()`, [findings-shared-lib-foundation](findings-shared-lib-foundation.md)). Famous timelines are often **rotations** of the raw output.
- **Xenakis sieves** — onsets = residue classes (x mod M = r) combined by union/intersection/complement: deterministic, periodic (period = LCM of moduli), non-obvious; per-voice sieves give instant polyrhythm.
- **Stochastic clouds** — drive onsets by a Poisson process whose rate follows a **density envelope over time** ("compose the statistics"); cross-fade against the deterministic patterns for an "arrhythmia" section.
- **Coprime-length ostinato layering** (Cowell) and **superposed tempo-ratio duplication** (Pléiades) for evolving polymetric texture.
- **Cell development** — a short motif transformed by rotation/displacement/augmentation/diminution across the ensemble is the concert-percussion engine's core generator ([composition-craft](composition-craft.md) economy of material).
- **Structured microtiming** — small, *fixed* per-voice timing offsets (keep the timeline tightest, let the lead breathe) make the grid feel alive; uniform per-note jitter is the failure mode ([groove-and-embodiment](groove-and-embodiment.md)).

## Implications for generative engines

1. **Implement a universal 4-layer voice architecture**: (a) a **timeline** layer — one inviolable high cyclic ostinato that fixes the cycle and never drops out; (b) a **low anchor** on the fundamental pulse; (c) 1–3 **interlocking mid ostinati** hocketed into the timeline's gaps (Euclidean is ideal); (d) a **lead/ornament** layer that improvises, fills, and executes signaled breaks. Add a "facilitator" agent that injects breaks, rumbles, stop-cuts, swells, and layer add/drops. This one structure serves folk, circle, and (with cell-development on top) concert styles.
2. **Carry form with a density/intensity envelope**, not harmony. Offer several forms: **cyclic/additive** (folk default), **process** (phasing/build-up), **square-root nested proportion**, **arch / timbre-arch**, **jo-ha-kyū intensification** (nestable), and **elastic/mosaic** (variable block order/length — a clean way to hit any target duration).
3. **Build and release tension** by modulating density, dynamics, register spread, rhythmic complexity (unison→displaced→unison), timbral accumulation (families in/out; doubling), and tempo (accelerando/oroshi; metric modulation by 3:2 or 4:3); treat silence/stop-cut and fills at 4/8/16-bar boundaries as punctuation; completion or return-to-unison is the cadence substitute.
4. **Support 2/4 and 4/4 by default with 4/8/16-bar phrasing**, plus 6/8 & 12/8 for bell patterns and aksak 5/8 (2+3) / 7/8 (2+2+3); realize odd meters as unequal beat-groups over a constant pulse, and polyrhythm as cross-beats within one felt meter.
5. **Keep any melody subordinate** — a mallet/bass/drone ostinato locked to the timeline, sparser and quieter than the drums, or real melody confined to one section.
6. Engine 05 ("Percussion Ensemble") realizes this program — see [findings-percussion-engine](findings-percussion-engine.md).

## Open questions

- **Per-tradition swing/microtiming ratios** (Malian jembe, samba caixa, taiko) — collect concrete numbers before hard-coding a feel ([groove-and-embodiment](groove-and-embodiment.md) open question).
- **Naming and attribution.** Following Agawu ([west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md)), an engine that generalizes across traditions should present its styles as *musical technology* (a clave, a bell timeline, a density arc), not as ethnic "modes" — the generic-folk approach here deliberately avoids claiming to reproduce any one named repertoire.
- **How faithfully can concert cell-development be automated** without a human's ear for when a transformation has "arrived"? A metric for process-completion salience is unexplored ([computational-music-metrics](computational-music-metrics.md)).
- Minor source conflicts flagged: *Ionisation*'s instrument count (~37–40), *Persephassa*'s peak tempo (~208 vs ~360 bpm), and Chávez *Toccata*'s premiere year (1947 vs 1948) vary across sources; Cowell's work is *Ostinato Pianissimo* (1934).
