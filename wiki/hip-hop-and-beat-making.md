# Hip-hop and beat-making

Hip-hop beat-making is the instrumental compositional system behind rap, and it is unusually well documented as a *system*: a loop-based unit of composition, an ethics and craft of transforming borrowed material, a swing-percentage culture precise enough to argue about in forum threads, and, in trap, a fully worked-out melodic and rhythmic grammar (the glide-tuned 808, the hat roll) built without any harmony textbook in the room. Almost none of it depends on the sampler as a hardware object — the craft conventions (loop-as-unit, chop-as-motivic-development, subtraction-as-form, glide-as-melody) survive translation to pure synthesis, which is what this page is for. It presupposes [groove-and-embodiment](groove-and-embodiment.md)'s backbeat/swing/velocity-hierarchy fundamentals and does not re-cover them, and it adds to rather than restates this project's own [groove-lofi engine](findings-groove-lofi-engine.md), which already implements boom-bap-style structured swing and backbeat over an LP-crackle bed. The throughline across every style below is loop craft, subtraction arrangement, and — increasingly since the late 2000s — a bass line doing melodic work that used to belong to a chord instrument.

## The loop as the compositional unit

Hip-hop's founding technical act was not sampling but *looping*: DJ Kool Herc, a Jamaican-born Bronx DJ, used two copies of the same record on two turntables to extend a track's most rhythmically intense passage (the "break") indefinitely, cutting between them as each ran out — the "merry-go-round" technique. Herc's method drew directly on the Jamaican sound-system culture he grew up around: massive speaker stacks, dub's stretching and re-editing of existing recordings, and *toasting* (rhythmic talk-over a record) as a performance role distinct from singing (Wikipedia, "DJ Kool Herc"; see [reggae-and-caribbean](reggae-and-caribbean.md) for that lineage in its own right). Samplers later automated what turntables did by hand, but the underlying unit stayed the same: a continuous 2–4-bar phrase, repeated, is the basic compositional building block hip-hop instrumentals are assembled from — closer to a minimalist ostinato ([minimalism-and-process-music](minimalism-and-process-music.md)) than to a through-composed line.

Joseph Schloss's *Making Beats* (2004), the field's ethnographic anchor (a decade of fieldwork with sample-based producers), documents that producers do not treat this raw material neutrally: they distinguish *looping* (repeating a phrase largely unaltered), *chopping* (dividing a phrase into small segments and reordering or recombining them into a new pattern), and *flipping* (transforming a source enough — new key, new tempo, new context — that it is no longer recognizable), and rank them by how much creative transformation each demonstrates (Schloss 2004, as characterized in the IASPM review and Wesleyan University Press's synopsis). Schloss frames the community's norms around this as a genuine ethical system, not merely a style preference: producers police "digging" for obscure, uncredited sources and reward audible transformation over minimal-effort lifting, treating skillful chopping as a moral as well as an aesthetic accomplishment. Justin Williams's *Rhymin' and Stealin'* (2013) generalizes the same instinct beyond audio sampling: hip-hop's "fundamental element... is the overt use of preexisting material to new ends," extending to lyrical quotation, structural borrowing, and cross-referencing across the genre's whole practice, not just crate-digging.

None of this requires an actual sample. What survives the move to synthesis is the *form* the ethics reward: real transformation of a motif — reordering, truncating, and recombining a phrase's own segments — counts as composition, not decoration. See "Implications" below for applying chop-style rearrangement to this project's own generated (never sampled) material.

## Boom bap: swing culture, the MPC/SP-1200 lineage, and DJ-derived arrangement

Boom bap — the 1988–96 New York-centered mainstream, named for its emphatic kick-snare ("boom... bap") — sits at roughly 85–95 BPM, a range that follows from the funk and soul breaks it was built from and gives rapped lyrics room to breathe (practitioner consensus; earlier Herc/Bambaataa-era breakbeats ran faster, up to ~100–119 BPM). Its two signature machines, the E-mu SP-1200 and the Akai MPC line, shaped the aesthetic as much as any producer's taste did:

- **The SP-1200's constraint became a style.** The SP-1200 offered about 10 seconds of total sample memory across its eight pads, with any single sample capped near 2.5 seconds (Sorcinelli, "Micro-Chopping"; Wikipedia, "E-mu SP-1200"). That ceiling made chopping a necessity, not just an aesthetic choice — producers sliced longer breaks into pad-sized fragments and resequenced them, and the SP-1200's crunchy, band-limited 12-bit conversion supplied the "dusty" low-fidelity texture the era prizes as a positive value, not a defect. This project's own [groove-lofi engine](findings-groove-lofi-engine.md) already implements that lo-fi patina (a vinyl-crackle bed, a warm FM electric piano) — this page does not re-cover it and points there instead.
- **The MPC formalized loop-as-composition into pattern chaining.** Producers assign chopped fragments ("stabs") to drum pads and build a track by chaining short patterns — a direct descendant of the DJ's mixer-and-mute workflow, now performed on pads instead of turntable crossfaders (Exarchos 2019, "Boom Bap ex Machina"). Sections (intro, verse, hook) become different pattern combinations muted in and out, functionally identical to the layer-mute-timeline arrangement documented for EDM in [electronic-and-dance](electronic-and-dance.md) — the same mute-matrix logic, arrived at independently from a DJ-culture rather than a studio-sequencer starting point.
- **Swing is a numbered, contested craft parameter.** MPC drum machines (designed by Roger Linn) implement swing by delaying every second sixteenth note by a percentage of the beat: 50% is no swing, roughly 54% "loosens" a straight pattern without reading as swung, 58–62% approaches — and on some units defaults to — a near-triplet feel, 66% is exact triplet swing, and values up to 71–75% are usable extremes (Linn, in a Brettworks interview; Melodiefabriek, "MPC Swing in Reason"; MPC-user forum consensus). Producers commonly work in the 54–62% band for a felt-but-subtle groove. J Dilla's celebrated "drunk" feel is a different mechanism entirely and worth distinguishing clearly: by most accounts he mostly **disabled quantization** and played beats by hand rather than dialing in a swing percentage, so his timing is off-grid in a way no single swing number reproduces (Brettworks/Gearspace, hedged — his exact settings are debated and not fully documented).

## Trap: half-time, the 808 as melodic bass, and hat grammar

Beyond sound design, scholars increasingly read trap as a documentary form rather than pure texture: Regina Bradley's work on Atlanta hip-hop frames trap as "critical storytelling" — a genre documenting labor, aspiration, and survival in the post-civil-rights Black South (Bradley, MODA lecture series) — a reminder that the sparseness described below (empty bars, a two-note loop) is a chosen expressive register, not just an economy of production effort.

### Half-time tempo

Trap instrumentals are typically produced at 130–150 BPM (some sources extend the range toward 160–180 for faster drill/phonk-adjacent work; treat the upper bound as contested), but the drums are programmed in **half time**: kick on beat 1, snare or clap on beat 3, so the track *feels* like roughly 65–75 BPM even though the transport reads double that (RouteNote; LowEndCandy; Native Instruments Blog). Producers write at the doubled tempo deliberately, because it gives the sequencer finer resolution for hi-hat subdivisions and rolls — the same musical result at half the notated tempo would leave 32nd-note hat detail almost unreachable on a 16th-note grid.

### The 808 becomes a melodic instrument

The genre's defining sound-design move is turning the Roland TR-808's kick/sub voice from a one-shot drum hit into a **legato, pitch-tracked bass instrument** that carries the hook's melodic motion. In practice: mono voice allocation, a glide/portamento time of roughly 80–150 ms between overlapping notes, and a decay tuned per note rather than fixed, so the "808" functions as the bass part of the arrangement, not merely its low end (RouteNote; WTMH Studio, "808 Production Guide"). Kanye West's *808s & Heartbreak* (2008) is widely credited as the turning point that popularized manipulating the 808's pitch and decay expressively rather than using it as a static drum sample (Rolling Stone; Roland Articles). Drill pushed the idea further into continuous glissando: the "sliding 808," where the bass audibly slides between pitches rather than re-triggering at each note, is credited to producers L.A. Beats and 808Razz and is now a genre-defining texture in UK and Brooklyn drill in particular (DJ Mag, "The evolving sound of UK drill").

This is a concrete synthesis target. [synthesis-recipes](synthesis-recipes.md)'s existing 808-style kick (an exponential pitch drop from about E2 (~82 Hz) to E1–G1 (~41–49 Hz), τ 0.3–0.6 s, with a touch of waveshaper drive) is the right starting timbre, but trap usage repurposes that same shape into a **sustained, glided, per-note-tuned voice** — the engine needs a mono legato bass module with portamento between scheduled pitches, not just a decaying one-shot triggered per hit. See "Implications" below.

### Hat grammar: a literal generative grammar

Trap hi-hats are the clearest case in this whole genre family of a rhythm pattern practitioners already describe in grammar-like terms: a base subdivision state interrupted by marked figures, exactly the state-plus-production structure of [grammars-and-rewriting-systems](grammars-and-rewriting-systems.md). The standard moves, cataloged consistently across production tutorials (LANDR; MusicRadar; Beat Production):

- **Base state** — straight 8th- or 16th-note subdivision, often the majority of the bar.
- **Triplet switch** — the base subdivision briefly reinterpreted in 3 instead of 2 (a local meter shift, not a new tempo).
- **Rolls** — an accelerating run of repeated hits at increasing subdivision (8th → 16th → 32nd), typically placed just before a downbeat or snare hit.
- **Stutters / pitch-shifted micro-repeats** — a very fast, often detuned or panned repeat of a single hat hit, used as a fill or transition.
- **Velocity ramps** — a crescendo built into a roll so it reads as building tension rather than just being "more notes."
- **UK-drill's "1-2-2" step pattern** — a specific triplet-flavored hat placement documented as a genre signature distinct from Chicago/Atlanta trap's hat vocabulary (DJ Mag).

Because these are a small, named set of interruption types applied to a base state with real (if informally documented) placement conventions, they translate almost directly into a finite-state or probabilistic grammar: a base-subdivision production with a handful of interruption productions, weighted and seeded. See "Implications."

### Sparse dark loops and drop-out form

Melodic material is deliberately minimal: two-to-four-note loops (bells, plucks, flutes, thin synth stabs) in **Phrygian** or **harmonic minor**, chosen for the tense, dark color the raised leading tone (harmonic minor) or the half-step above the tonic (Phrygian's ♭2) contributes, looped with little variation across a whole section (ProductionMusicLive, "Trap Beat Guide: Melodies"; see [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md) and [harmony](harmony.md) for the scales themselves). Form is then carved almost entirely by **subtraction**: a widely cited convention is dropping the 808/low end out for a bar or four immediately before a hook and letting it slam back in on the downbeat — the trap-specific instance of the general subtraction-arrangement device covered below.

## Regional and era variants

### G-funk

West Coast G-funk (early-to-mid 1990s, Dr. Dre foundational) pairs slow, syncopated grooves with a high, whistle-like lead synth played with portamento and light vibrato — the "G-funk whistle" of Dre's "Nuthin' but a 'G' Thang" (1992) is the archetype (Syntorial preset breakdown). The lineage runs straight back to P-Funk: producers sampled and, especially Dre, re-played Parliament-Funkadelic material on the same class of instrument P-Funk's Bernie Worrell used — Moog Model D, ARP Solina and 2600, Prophet 5 (Mudcake, "G-Funk Synth breakdown"; Wikipedia, "G-funk"). See [funk-soul-and-rnb](funk-soul-and-rnb.md) for the P-Funk side of that lineage. G-funk is the earliest widely-cited instance of the portamento-lead culture that resurfaces, transformed, in trap's glide bass.

### Miami bass and Dirty South bounce

One line each: Miami bass (1980s–90s) is built almost entirely on the Roland TR-808 — a sustained, often pitch-tuned kick doing double duty as bass, constant 32nd-note hats, and raised dance tempos (Wikipedia, "Miami bass"; Roland Articles on Dynamix II). Dirty South bounce fuses Miami bass with New Orleans bounce and hardcore Southern rap into the regional 1990s–2000s sound trap eventually grew out of (hiphopmusichistory.com).

### Drill: Chicago static, UK/Brooklyn sliding

Chicago drill (Young Chop's production template is the reference point) pairs dark minor-key synth or bell lines with sparse percussion over a **sustained, largely static** 808 — the bass holds a pitch rather than sliding — at a feel around 60–70 BPM (Orphiq; Wikipedia, "Drill music"). UK and Brooklyn drill diverge specifically on the bass: they adopted the continuous **sliding** 808 described above, plus "ghostly," reverb-heavy bell and string fragments layered over the bleakness, and are typically written nearer 134–144 BPM rather than Chicago's slower feel (DJ Mag; Orphiq). The Chicago/UK split is the cleanest real-world example in this whole family of the same core instrument (the 808) being used two structurally different ways — static-pitch anchor versus continuous melodic glide.

### UK drill and grime

Grime (early-2000s East London) crystallized around 140 BPM — reportedly because it was FruityLoops's default tempo and grime's production generation largely worked in FruityLoops — and a square/pulse-wave synth-bass culture nicknamed "eski," after Wiley's foundational "eskibeat" sound; the signature patch (popularized as a Korg Triton preset called "Gliding Squares") uses a pulse wave with monophonic glide, aggressive low-pass filtering, and unison detune (New Statesman, on Dan Hancox's grime histories; Attack Magazine, "Grime Synth Basics"; Wikipedia, "Grime"). UK drill emerged partly out of the same London sound-system-and-pirate-radio continuum, sits at a neighboring tempo (134–144 BPM), and has increasingly absorbed grime's faster hats and synth-bass stabs alongside its own sliding 808 (DJ Mag) — the two scenes cross-pollinate rather than staying cleanly separate, and both sit in the same tempo neighborhood as the dubstep/half-time-breakbeat family already mapped in [electronic-and-dance](electronic-and-dance.md).

### Cloud rap

One line: Clams Casino's beats for Lil B (2008 on) — reverb-drenched, chopped vocal samples over ambient/new-age-adjacent pads laid under trap drums — founded a hazy, atmospheric strand explicitly indebted to ambient and downtempo listening music, not just rap production (Wikipedia, "Cloud rap"; Hyperallergic). See [ambient-and-generative-genre](ambient-and-generative-genre.md) for the texture-over-event aesthetic it borrows.

### Instrumental hip-hop and trip-hop as listening music

Two records matter disproportionately here because they prove the beat-making toolkit above sustains full-length *listening*, with no rapper at all — the exact brief of this project. DJ Shadow's *Endtroducing.....* (1996) is built almost entirely from sampled vinyl assembled on an Akai MPC60 Mk II, and stands as the reference proof that an instrumental hip-hop album can carry a listener for 60 minutes on loop craft, mood, and arrangement alone (Wikipedia, "Endtroducing....."; uDiscover Music). Portishead's *Dummy* (1994) pairs the same hip-hop breakbeat sensibility — many tracks are literally one looped sample at their core — with live instrumentation and vocals, and is widely credited with popularizing "trip-hop" as a genre of mood and form rather than lyric (Classic Album Sundays). Both are working demonstrations that this page's structural devices (loop, chop, subtraction) are sufficient to carry an entire instrumental piece, independent of any words.

## The beat as arrangement: subtraction, switches, and loop fatigue

**Subtraction and addition of layers is hip-hop production's primary form device**, and producer pedagogy states this explicitly rather than leaving it implicit: build one loop, then arrange by removing and restoring elements across sections — a verse might carry only drums plus one harmonic layer, the hook adds every layer back at once for maximum density (beatstorapon.com; hiphopmakers.com; mixelite.com — labeled here as a producer-pedagogy convention, not a measured finding). It is functionally the same device as the layer-mute timeline already documented for EDM in [electronic-and-dance](electronic-and-dance.md), arrived at from loop-based sampler workflow rather than a DAW automation lane.

**The beat switch** — a wholesale mid-track change of tempo, key, or entire drum kit — is a modern, deliberately non-developmental form device: Kendrick Lamar's "XXX." (featuring U2) and Mike WiLL Made-It's beat switch on "DNA.", Kanye West's "Blood on the Leaves," and Travis Scott's multi-part "SICKO MODE" are commonly cited examples (Complex; DJBooth; District Magazine). It is the extreme end of [form-and-structure](form-and-structure.md)'s medley-vs-development spectrum: a beat switch deliberately reads as two pieces spliced together rather than one theme varied, trading the coherence of a through-line for a single high-impact surprise.

**Intro/outro conventions** follow the general craft covered in [beginnings-endings-and-transitions](beginnings-endings-and-transitions.md); hip-hop's specific contribution is the DJ-facing instrumental intro (drums-only or drums-plus-one-element, long enough to mix in on) inherited directly from the mixer-arrangement lineage above.

**Loop-fatigue management** is a named practitioner concern: a bare, unvaried loop is commonly said to grow fatiguing within roughly 90 seconds of continuous repetition (Lofi Weekly), a shorter horizon than the compressed-exposure satiation curve in [repetition-and-familiarity](repetition-and-familiarity.md) would predict for structurally varied material — consistent with that page's point that thin, synthetic, or minimally-varied loops satiate fastest. The practitioner countermeasures are "ear candy" (one-shot foley, vinyl surface noise, or a vocal chop used as punctuation rather than melody), scheduled fills every four to eight bars, and selective muting of individual elements (Soundtrap Blog; Audient) — cheap, well-documented variation-injection techniques.

## Harmony and melody norms

Across every style above, the harmonic vocabulary stays deliberately small: two-to-four-chord or single-note-pedal loops, Phrygian and harmonic-minor color favored for a dark, tense affect, and — from G-funk's whistle lead through trap and drill's glide bass — a persistent portamento-lead culture that treats pitch-bending between notes as the genre's default *melodic* gesture rather than a special effect. [groove-and-embodiment](groove-and-embodiment.md) flags, as an open question, Senn et al.'s (2018) finding that rhythmic and harmonic complexity jointly shape perceived groove rather than contributing independently — hip-hop's beat-making tradition reads as a decades-long practitioner-side bet on one resolution of that open question: keep the harmonic and melodic material minimal precisely where the rhythm (the hi-hats, above all) is busiest, and spend the listener's attention budget on rhythmic density instead of harmonic movement. That reading is informed speculation tying practitioner convention to the cited research, not a claim the research itself has settled.

## Implications for generative engines

- **A trap style pack**: a half-time grid (kick on 1, snare/clap on 3, full-rate hat lane); an 808-glide bass-line generator — mono legato voice, portamento 80–150 ms between scheduled pitches, per-note-tuned decay, built on [synthesis-recipes](synthesis-recipes.md)'s 808 kick shape but re-triggered as a sustained melodic part, not a one-shot; a hat-roll grammar (below) with seeded triplet/roll/stutter interruptions; a sparse 2–4-note Phrygian/harmonic-minor loop generator; and subtraction arrangement (drop the 808 for a bar or four before every hook).
- **A boom-bap pack** that reuses rather than reinvents: the swing (phase-warp) and laid-back-backbeat machinery already validated in [findings-groove-lofi-engine](findings-groove-lofi-engine.md), tuned toward the 54–62% swing-percentage band documented above, plus a **chop-style motivic rearrangement operator** applied to the engine's *own* synthesized material — generate an original 2–4-bar phrase, then reorder, truncate, and recombine its own segments (never an external sample) as a legitimate compositional move in Schloss's sense, tested against [melody](melody.md)'s establish-then-vary principle so the result still reads as a recognizable, varied motif rather than noise.
- **Hat-roll grammar as a reusable generator for any beat-driven style**: a small finite-state grammar over { straight-8th, straight-16th, triplet-switch, roll (accelerating subdivision), stutter, velocity-ramp }, with seeded, weighted transitions into interruption states placed preferentially in the last beat of a bar or phrase — directly implementable with this project's [stochastic-chaos-and-automata](stochastic-chaos-and-automata.md) toolkit (weighted choice, bounded walks) feeding a [grammars-and-rewriting-systems](grammars-and-rewriting-systems.md)-style production system, reusable well beyond trap (any groove-driven engine wants an occasional roll or stutter fill).
- **808-glide as a reusable generator**: any style with a synthesized low end can expose a "glide amount / glide time" macro on its bass voice; the mono-legato-plus-portamento module trap perfected is a general-purpose melodic-bass primitive, not a trap-only feature.
- **Subtraction arrangement as a general, site-wide form tool**: model a piece as a fixed roster of layers plus a per-section activation table (which layers are on) — hip-hop's loudest structural lesson is that *removing* layers for a verse and restoring them for a hook is often a stronger, cheaper form signal than composing new material, and it composes cleanly with the layer-mute-timeline model already specified for EDM in [electronic-and-dance](electronic-and-dance.md) and with [form-and-structure](form-and-structure.md)'s texture/register/instrumentation contrast principle.
- **A beat-switch operator**, used sparingly (at most once per generated piece, and only when explicitly enabled): a hard cut to a new tempo/key/kit at a structurally marked boundary, implemented as a deliberate contrast to this project's usual repetition-with-return form discipline ([form-and-structure](form-and-structure.md)) rather than a replacement for it.

## Open questions

- Does chop-style rearrangement of the engine's *own* synthesized material reproduce any of the aesthetic payoff Schloss documents for human digging and recontextualization, or is that payoff inseparable from the social act of transforming *someone else's* recording? This project has no way to test the socially-loaded half of that question, only the formal one (does self-chopping produce better motivic material than not chopping) — worth an actual listening-test comparison once a boom-bap-style engine exists.
- How rigorously documented are the specific MPC swing-percentage folklore numbers (54%, 58%, 62%...) versus how much is anecdote repeated across gear forums? Treat the 54–62% band as a practitioner-consensus estimate to tune by ear, in the same spirit as this wiki's existing ghost-note-velocity estimates in [groove-and-embodiment](groove-and-embodiment.md), not as a measured psychoacoustic constant.
- Where trap's tempo ceiling actually sits today: most sources converge on 130–150 BPM, but several extend the range to 160–180 for drill/phonk-adjacent output, and the genre's tempo center may simply still be drifting.
