# Thematic development and variation

Thematic development and variation is the toolkit by which a piece grows out of one or two small ideas instead of either repeating itself into monotony or stringing together unrelated new material (the "medley" failure named in [form-and-structure](form-and-structure.md)). [melody](melody.md) establishes that a motif must be stated and repeated before it is varied, and [composition-craft](composition-craft.md) establishes economy of material as a governing value; this page supplies the missing middle layer — the named operations themselves, defined precisely enough to implement, the historical forms built by chaining them, and, most importantly for a generative engine, the perceptual evidence for which of these operations a listener actually hears as "the same idea, developed" rather than as new material. Operations that survive contact with a listener's ear should be used liberally; operations that are theoretically elegant but perceptually opaque are paper transforms and should be used sparingly, or as background compositional logic rather than a foreground effect.

## The motivic operation catalog

A motif is an ordered sequence of the project's [Note events](engine-architecture.md) — `{beat, durBeats, midi}` — relativized so the motif's own first beat is 0. Every operation below is a pure function motif → motif, i indexing notes 1..n. Formal definitions of transposition, inversion, and retrograde as arithmetic on a pitch sequence follow standard usage (Open Music Theory's twelve-tone chapter states them as: transposition adds a constant to every pitch, inversion subtracts every pitch from a constant, retrograde reads the sequence backwards).

| Operation | Transform | What survives |
|---|---|---|
| Literal repetition | motif′ = motif | everything |
| Sequence, real | `midiᵢ′ = midiᵢ + k` (constant semitones) | exact interval pattern; only pitch level moves |
| Sequence, tonal | scale degree shifted by a constant diatonic step, respelled in-key | contour and approximate shape; interval qualities can change (a third can flip major/minor) |
| Inversion, exact | `midiᵢ′ = 2a − midiᵢ` (mirror around axis pitch *a*) | exact interval sizes; direction reverses |
| Inversion, diatonic | scale degrees mirrored around an axis degree, respelled in-key | contour reversal only; interval sizes can change |
| Retrograde | `(beat, durBeats, midi)ᵢ′ = (…)ₙ₊₁₋ᵢ`, beats renumbered | pitch and rhythm content; time order reverses |
| Retrograde inversion | retrograde ∘ inversion | interval sizes (if exact); both order and direction change |
| Augmentation | `durBeatsᵢ′ = durBeatsᵢ × k`, k > 1 | pitch content and rhythmic proportions; everything slower |
| Diminution (rhythmic) | `durBeatsᵢ′ = durBeatsᵢ × k`, 0 < k < 1 | same, but faster |
| Fragmentation | motif′ = a proper, usually distinctive, subsequence of motif | part of the original; length shrinks |
| Extension | motif′ = motif + appended/interpolated notes | the original in full, plus more |
| Intervallic expansion/compression | each successive interval scaled by k, direction kept | contour; interval sizes grow or shrink (a progressive version is the "wedge") |
| Rhythmic displacement | `beatᵢ′ = beatᵢ + offset` (mod bar length) | pitch/duration content; which beats read as strong/weak changes |
| Ornamentation-of-repeat | passing/neighbor notes inserted between original notes, which stay recoverable at their original beats | the original skeleton, audible under the added notes |
| Reharmonization | motif unchanged; the chord frame under it changes | the melody itself; only its harmonic meaning changes |

**A terminology trap worth flagging explicitly:** "diminution" has two unrelated senses in the older and newer literature. The modern, Schoenbergian sense (row 9 above) shortens note *durations* — the rhythmic mirror of augmentation. The older, 16th–18th-century sense means the opposite kind of density change: filling a melodic skeleton with many small ornamental notes without necessarily shortening its overall span. This project's own Engine 01 uses the *older* sense — its A′ return ornaments the basic idea with inserted passing/neighbor tones while its on-beat pitch skeleton stays untouched ([findings-tonal-classical-engine](findings-tonal-classical-engine.md)) — which is why the catalog above labels that operation "ornamentation-of-repeat" rather than "diminution," to keep the two senses from colliding in engine code or wiki prose.

## Developing variation and liquidation

Schoenberg's **developing variation** — introduced in [composition-craft](composition-craft.md) and detailed by Walter Frisch's *Brahms and the Principle of Developing Variation* (1984) — is the operational engine behind most of the catalog above: each new phrase changes some features of the motif while preserving others, so the piece *grows* rather than merely continuing. The mechanism Frisch adds beyond the general principle is directional and cumulative: Brahms's thematic material typically forms a **chain of derivations**, each new shape descending from the *most recently stated* variant rather than always returning to the original — identity can drift a long way over a movement while every adjacent link stays audibly connected. This is a materially different scheduling policy than always varying a fixed original (a hub-and-spoke pattern) and is worth distinguishing in an engine's development log.

**Liquidation** is the technique for spending that accumulated motivic energy and arriving cleanly at a cadence. Caplin's operational definition: liquidation "strip[s] the basic idea of its characteristic features, thus leaving the merely conventional ones for the cadence" — Schoenberg's own textbook illustration whittles a three-motive complex (a, b, c) down to b+c, then to c alone. Mechanically, liquidation is fragmentation (row 10) driven all the way to zero distinctive content, typically paired with harmonic acceleration (chords changing faster) — the same "continuation function" mechanics [phrase-structure](phrase-structure.md) already describes for the sentence's back half, and the same wind-down mechanism [composition-craft](composition-craft.md) names for ending sections. The Wikipedia summary of the sentence archetype states it as bringing "the statement to a properly 'liquidated' state and cadential conclusion" — liquidation is a *destination* (identity dissolves into generic cadential motion), not merely a variation.

**Model and sequence** is the development section's workhorse — the catalog's "sequence" operation chained several times in a row. Caplin's terms for this: a **model** (typically 2–4 bars) stated once, then repeated at new pitch levels (real or tonal sequence) two or three times in a row — a stretch he calls a **core** — usually accelerating and driving to a half cadence; material between the start of a development and the first core is a **pre-core**. This is the concrete mechanism behind [generative-music-design-patterns](generative-music-design-patterns.md)'s pattern P3 ("development: fragmentation, sequence, reharmonization"): a development section is model-and-sequence chains interleaved with liquidation, not free invention.

**Fortspinnung** ["spinning-forth"], coined by Wilhelm Fischer (1915) for Baroque practice, is a related but distinct three-part shape: a *Vordersatz* (opening idea) is *fortgesponnen* ("spun out") by sequencing it or a contrasting figure, then closed by an *Epilog*. The difference from the Classical continuation function above is closure discipline: Fortspinnung "deliberately avoids predictable endpoints" and can defer cadential arrival far longer than a Classical sentence, which converges on a cadence within a normative 4/8-bar span ([phrase-structure](phrase-structure.md)). Both are ways of generating extended music from a short cell by sequencing and continuation; an engine building a Baroque-flavored idiom should let sequences run longer and defer the cadence more than a Classical-flavored one would.

## Variation forms

**Ground bass, chaconne, passacaglia** fix a short bass line or chord progression (often 4 or 8 bars) as an ostinato and vary everything above it — melody, figuration, register, texture, added voices — on each cycle. The textbook distinction (chaconne = ostinato specifically in the bass; passacaglia = ostinato in any voice) is not consistently observed historically — Baroque composers used the two names "indiscriminately," and Girolamo Frescobaldi's redefinition treated passacaglia simply as continuous variation over a (possibly itself-varied) bass. Examples: Bach's Passacaglia and Fugue in C minor (BWV 582), Purcell's "When I am laid in earth" ground from *Dido and Aeneas*, Pachelbel's Canon (a ground bass under a canon). The ostinato-bass principle itself predates the Baroque, with roots in Renaissance dance and vocal practice (see [medieval-and-renaissance-music](medieval-and-renaissance-music.md)). This is the single most engine-friendly variation pattern in the whole catalog: fix a repeating harmonic/bass skeleton, then vary one or two surface parameters (figuration density, register, added counter-voice, mode mixture) per cycle — [form-and-structure](form-and-structure.md) already names passacaglia/chaconne as a form archetype; this page supplies what specifically to vary per cycle.

**Ornamental (figural) variations**, the Mozart-era default, keep phrase lengths and the harmonic outline fixed and elaborate the melody's surface with progressively denser figuration — Mozart's Twelve Variations on "Ah vous dirai-je, Maman," K. 265, is the textbook case. This is the theme-and-variations analog of the ornamentation-of-repeat catalog entry, scaled up to govern a whole movement instead of one phrase.

**Character variations** push further: each variation changes tempo, mode, meter, texture, or genre so hard that continuity depends entirely on the underlying skeleton, not the surface. Brahms's *Variations on a Theme by Handel* and Elgar's *Enigma Variations* (each variation musically portrays a different friend's personality — tempo, orchestration, and mood all shift) are the standard examples. Beethoven's *Diabelli Variations* sit at the far end of this spectrum, treating the theme as a pretext for exploration rather than an object to decorate.

**The Goldberg scheme** is the most elaborate pre-planned variation template on record and a genuinely useful model for an engine's macro-schedule. Bach fixes the aria's *bass line and harmonic progression* (not its melody) as the invariant across all 30 variations, then lays a rigid slot plan on top: every third variation (3, 6, 9 … 27) is a canon at a steadily widening interval (unison up through a ninth), with Kirkpatrick's observed three-part grouping — a genre piece, then a virtuosic hand-crossing "arabesque," then the canon — repeating nine times. Variation 30, where a canon at the tenth would be expected, is a quodlibet (a medley of folk tunes) instead — a deliberate, planned deviation from the pattern it just spent 27 variations establishing.

**Theme-and-variations as an engine-friendly form** generalizes all three of the above into one template: hold a **skeleton** constant (harmony progression, phrase lengths, and often the bass line) across every variation, and assign each variation a **policy** that varies one or two surface dimensions — figuration/rhythm, texture/voice count, mode (major ↔ parallel minor is the classic single-variation swap in Mozart and Beethoven), tempo/character, register. Concretely: `{ skeleton: {harmony[], phraseLengths[], bassLine?}, variations: [{index, opProgram, texture, mode, tempoFactor}] }`. The Goldberg scheme shows this slot plan can be authored as a full macro-schedule before any notes exist ("every third slot is a canon, the last slot breaks the pattern") — cheap to implement as a fixed array the composer module walks through.

## Thematic transformation and motivic improvisation

**Thematic transformation** takes a single referential idea through the *same* catalog of operations (rhythm change, mode change, fragmentation, re-orchestration, tempo change) across a whole multi-movement work, typically driven by a program or narrative rather than by purely musical-formal logic. Berlioz's *idée fixe* in *Symphonie fantastique* (1830) — a theme representing the beloved, recurring in every movement in an increasingly distorted guise (idyllic, then a ballroom waltz, then grotesquely parodied in the finale's witches' sabbath) — is the founding instance and anticipates both Liszt's thematic transformation in his symphonic poems and Faust/Dante symphonies (Liszt dedicated the *Faust Symphony* to Berlioz) and Wagner's leitmotif, which [film-and-game-music](film-and-game-music.md) already covers in depth (Wagner through John Williams) — see that page rather than duplicating it here. The distinction worth keeping straight: developing variation is driven by musical logic internal to a passage; thematic transformation is driven by extra-musical state (which character, which mood) applied to a fixed identity token — architecturally the same operator set, different scheduling driver.

**Jazz motivic improvisation** is [jazz-and-improvisation](jazz-and-improvisation.md)'s territory for formula-vocabulary recombination across solos (Owens, Love, Frieler on Parker); what's new here is the narrower and more contested claim of real-time motivic *development within a single solo*. The founding document is Gunther Schuller's 1958 essay "Sonny Rollins and the Challenge of Thematic Improvisation," which argued Rollins built his "Blue 7" solo by organically developing one composed motif's rhythm and intervals — a founding text of jazz analysis, still widely taught. Benjamin Givan's 2014 close re-investigation complicates it: the melodic pattern Schuller read as a developed theme was, Givan found, one of Rollins's stock formulaic licks, heard similarly across many of his other solos from the same period — "Blue 7" was likely formula deployment misread as motivic logic, not a real case of in-solo thematic development. The honest lesson for this project is symmetrical: analysts (human or automated) are prone to finding motivic coherence a generator never tracked explicitly, and conversely a constrained formula library of the kind [jazz-and-improvisation](jazz-and-improvisation.md) already recommends can produce passages that *sound* thematically developed purely because formulas recur — an engine does not necessarily need an explicit motif-tracker to read as coherent, but claims that it achieved "thematic improvisation" should be checked against its actual generation log, not against a plausible-sounding retrospective story.

## The perceptual reality check: what listeners can actually hear

Composers and theorists have named dozens of transformations; the question for an engine is which ones an ordinary listener — hearing the music once, not reading the score — actually perceives as "the same idea, developed," versus unrelated new material. The evidence here is thinner and narrower than the theory: much of it comes from short-term recognition-memory experiments on brief, often unfamiliar, isolated tone sequences, not full-length listening to real pieces — a caveat to carry through everything below.

**Contour is the load-bearing cue, but not sufficient alone.** Dowling & Fujitani (1971) found listeners rely on contour (the up/down shape, independent of exact interval size) to recognize a transposed repeat of a melody, but are also sensitive to exact interval information when no transposition is involved. Dowling's (1978) two-component theory adds a timescale distinction: immediate, short-term recognition of a novel melody is dominated by contour — a listener can retrieve the shape even after losing the exact intervals — while longer-term and tonal memory layers scale-degree/interval information on top. White (1960) showed the cost of losing that second layer directly: distorting a familiar melody's interval sizes while keeping its contour intact measurably impairs recognition compared with preserving both. Net for this project: contour is the first thing to hold invariant across a variation meant to be heard as "the same," and rhythm/interval size is the second-order safeguard — the same two anchors [melody](melody.md) already flags as carrying identity and [repetition-and-familiarity](repetition-and-familiarity.md) already names as the thing to keep invariant while varying around it; this page supplies the specific mechanism.

**The symmetry operations are harder, and rank-ordered.** Dowling's (1972) short-term recognition study — five-note sequences, narrow ambitus (intervals under a major third), a same/different judgment against an earlier standard — found inversion, retrograde, and retrograde inversion all recognized better than chance under easier conditions, but with a clear ascending order of *difficulty*: inversion easiest, retrograde next, retrograde inversion hardest. Within any one of these transform types, listeners showed no reliable extra benefit from versions that preserved exact interval sizes over versions that preserved contour alone — once a symmetry operation is in play, getting the mirrored shape right seems to matter more than getting the exact intervals right. Caveat this ordering heavily: the stimuli were tiny, abstract tone rows under a forced-choice lab paradigm, not idiomatic tonal motifs in a real piece, and Dowling reported large individual differences — treat the *ordering* (inversion > retrograde > retrograde inversion) as the portable finding, not the raw accuracy numbers. It matches the practical intuition behind twelve-tone technique's reputation: retrograde-inversion rows are closer to a paper transform than an audible one, while melodic inversion — the shape behind a fugue's real answer or a simple call-and-response — is the most recoverable of the three by ear.

**Augmentation/diminution: moderately recognizable, on indirect evidence.** Direct experimental evidence on *within-piece* proportional rhythmic augmentation (a theme returning in doubled note values against a steady beat) did not surface in this pass — a genuine gap, flagged rather than papered over. The nearest available evidence is recognition-memory work on tempo change of whole performances: Halpern & Müllensiefen (2008) found tempo change (alongside timbre change) measurably impairs both explicit and implicit tune recognition, while Schellenberg, Weiss, Peng & Alam (2019) found listeners retain surprisingly fine-grained implicit memory for a tune's original tempo, such that even small shifts are detectable. Read together, tempo/durational change is noticeable and costs some recognition accuracy without abolishing it — consistent with "moderately recognizable" — but this extrapolates from whole-performance tempo change to in-piece rhythmic augmentation of a single motif, which is a different manipulation; treat as informed inference, not a directly tested claim.

**Fragmentation, sequence, and reharmonization are the easy cases, mostly by inference rather than direct test.** A sequence (real or tonal) keeps contour, rhythm, and relative interval structure intact and only shifts pitch level, so by the contour/interval evidence above it should be highly recognizable — an inference, not a separately tested result, but a low-risk one. Fragmentation trades on the "uniqueness marker" mechanism [melody](melody.md) already documents from Huron: recognition should be high if the retained fragment is the piece's own previously-repeated distinctive cell, and low if it is generic filler — which is exactly why liquidation *works* as a way to genuinely erase identity while a sequenced head-motif fragment does not. Reharmonization leaves the melody itself completely untouched, so — definitionally — it is likely the single most reliably recognizable development operation available; it changes color and affect rather than delivering a sense of growth.

**Synthesis for engine policy:**

| Tier | Operations | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| High — safe to use often | Literal repetition, reharmonization, real/tonal sequence, fragmentation of the *distinctive* cell, ornamentation-of-repeat | Preserve contour and/or the whole melody; direct or strongly-inferred evidence |
| Medium — use deliberately, don't stack | Melodic inversion, augmentation/diminution | Recognizable above chance but at a real cost; inversion is the most recoverable symmetry operation; augmentation's in-piece evidence is indirect |
| Low — sparing, "paper" transforms | Retrograde, retrograde inversion, intervallic expansion/compression pushed far from the original, generic-fragment liquidation | Weakest recognition evidence, or definitionally erase the surface cues (contour, rhythm) that carry identity |

## Implications for generative engines

- **Build the catalog as a pure-function operations library over the Note-event schema.** Each op is `motif -> motif` on `{beat, durBeats, midi}` arrays: `repeat`, `sequenceReal(k)`, `sequenceTonal(d, scale)`, `invertExact(axis)`, `invertDiatonic(axis, scale)`, `retrograde()`, `retrogradeInversion(axis)`, `augment(k)`, `diminish(k)`, `fragment(range)`, `extend(tail)`, `scaleIntervals(k)`, `displace(offset)`, `ornament(density)`, `reharmonize(chordFrame)`. Keep them pure and composable so a "development" is a short, inspectable *program* (an ordered list of ops applied to a stored motif) — the auditable developing-variation log [composition-craft](composition-craft.md) already calls for.
- **Schedule development by section, escalating depth.** Exposition: literal repetition and sequence only — establish before varying ([melody](melody.md), [repetition-and-familiarity](repetition-and-familiarity.md)). Middle/development: chain model-and-sequence (Caplin's "core"), then unlock medium- and low-tier ops as a monotonically increasing "development depth" parameter, converging on liquidation approaching a cadence. Return/recap: near-literal restatement — restate harmony and the on-beat skeleton exactly, vary only via ornamentation-of-repeat, exactly the policy [findings-tonal-classical-engine](findings-tonal-classical-engine.md)'s A′ already validates and [form-and-structure](form-and-structure.md) motivates ("listeners track themes, not keys").
- **Enforce an identity-preservation constraint.** On any repeat meant to read as "the same idea," hold at least one of {contour, rhythm} exactly invariant, per the perceptual reality check. Reserve simultaneous heavy transformation of both for deliberate unrecognizability — true liquidation, or a hidden foreshadowing quotation in the leitmotif style ([film-and-game-music](film-and-game-music.md)'s Imperial-March-inside-Anakin's-theme example). A cheap guardrail: never chain more than about two low-tier operations on one pass without an intervening literal or high-tier restatement.
- **Ship a variation-form template spec** for an explicit theme-and-variations mode: `{ skeleton: {harmony[], phraseLengths[], bassLine?}, variations: [{index, opProgram, texture, mode, tempoFactor}] }`, with the slot plan (which variation gets which treatment) authored as a fixed macro-schedule up front, Goldberg-style, rather than decided variation-by-variation at generation time.
- **The operations library is style-neutral** — the same functions instantiate as jazz formula-vocabulary recombination ([jazz-and-improvisation](jazz-and-improvisation.md)), leitmotif transformation driven by game/story state ([film-and-game-music](film-and-game-music.md)), or a Baroque-flavored engine's longer-breathed Fortspinnung sequences, by changing only the scheduling policy and how far cadential closure is deferred — a natural point of leverage when this project starts building out a genuine [style-invention-and-style-space](style-invention-and-style-space.md).

## Open questions

- No direct evidence (as opposed to extrapolation from whole-performance tempo-change studies) was found on the recognizability of within-piece proportional rhythmic augmentation/diminution of a motif — worth a targeted search or a first-party listening test once the operations library exists.
- Does Dowling's (1972) small-interval, five-note recognition ordering (inversion > retrograde > retrograde inversion) hold for longer, idiomatic, tonal motifs rather than abstract short tone rows? A natural first experiment for [listening-tests-and-feedback](listening-tests-and-feedback.md) once an engine ships the catalog.
- How many chained low-tier operations can a passage sustain before listeners stop hearing "development" and start hearing "new material" (the medley failure)? No direct evidence found; a candidate for this project's own [computational-music-metrics](computational-music-metrics.md) or listening tests.
