# Memory, earworms, and catchiness

Every other psychology page in this wiki asks how a listener responds to music unfolding in real time — what they expect next ([musical-expectation.md](musical-expectation.md)), or why hearing something again changes how much they like it ([repetition-and-familiarity.md](repetition-and-familiarity.md)). This page asks a different question: what actually gets *stored*, what gets stored so well it resurfaces uninvited, and what separates a melody a listener forgets in ten seconds from one they cannot get out of their head for a decade. Memorability is not a bonus feature — the project's stated goal is music that is pleasing, inspiring, and interesting, and interesting-but-forgettable is a real failure mode distinct from the ones already catalogued in [generative-music-failure-modes.md](generative-music-failure-modes.md). This page covers how melodic memory is encoded moment to moment, how precisely long-term memory retains specific recordings, why earworms happen and to whom, what the best empirical attempt to quantify "catchiness" actually found, and what all of this implies for building hooks — and for knowing when not to.

## Encoding a melody: contour first, interval later

William Dowling's two-component theory (1978) splits melodic memory into an overlearned scale schema (the steps a culture's music moves through) and **contour** — the up/down shape of a melody independent of exact interval size (see the contour discussion in [melody.md](melody.md)). Dowling & Fujitani (1971), the classic demonstration, played listeners short melodies followed by comparison melodies that either preserved contour while changing exact intervals, or preserved intervals exactly via transposition; non-musician listeners discriminated contour changes far more reliably than interval-only changes, showing that short-term recognition of a novel tune leans heavily on shape rather than precise pitch distance.

That balance shifts with delay and familiarity. Dowling & Bartlett (1981), testing recognition of unfamiliar Beethoven-quartet phrases, found contour information dominated recognition after brief delays, while interval information — harder to encode but retained efficiently once it is — carried more weight after longer, filled delays. Dowling (1991) sharpened this with a direct comparison: listeners confused same-contour, different-interval melodies with the original at an 11-second delay but not at a 39-second delay. The practical reading: a melody's contour is what a listener recognizes an instant after first hearing it; its exact intervals are what a listener recognizes once a piece has become familiar. Both stages matter and they are not the same skill.

Rhythm is a real but secondary identity cue. Hébert & Peretz (1997) tested recognition of well-known tunes reduced to pitch-contour alone versus rhythm alone and found melody the far stronger cue — listeners named more tunes and reported higher confidence from pitch information than from timing information, and rhythm alone was a poor retrieval cue even though the best performance came from the two combined. Volkova, Trehub, Schellenberg, Papsin & Gordon (2014), testing children's recognition of familiar TV themes, similarly found rhythm-only cues sufficient for above-chance identification but reliably weaker than pitch-contour cues or the combination. Rhythm alone can carry identity — a drum-only "We Will Rock You" stays recognizable — but pitch contour does most of the work generally.

Encoding also leans on **chunking**: the classical limit on how much unstructured material working memory holds at once (Miller's "magical number seven," 1956) means a melody must be parsed into a handful of higher-order units to be retained at all, and material that fits a listener's learned schemas chunks more readily than material that does not (the schematic/dynamic expectation machinery in [musical-expectation.md](musical-expectation.md); the galant-schema chunk vocabulary in [melody.md](melody.md)). A phrase built from a recognizable shape is not just easier to predict moment to moment — it is easier to encode as one memorable unit rather than a string of unrelated notes, which is the cognitive substrate the earworm-feature findings below sit on top of.

## Long-term memory for recordings and for fragments

### Near-absolute pitch and tempo for known recordings

Untrained listeners are usually assumed to lack absolute pitch, yet Daniel Levitin's classic study (1994) found ordinary memory for specific recordings is far more precise than that assumption predicts. Forty-six volunteers with no special pitch training were asked to sing two familiar pop songs from memory with no reference pitch given; their sung opening pitch was compared against the actual commercial recording. About 40% reproduced the recording's pitch exactly (within a semitone) on at least one of their two songs, 12% did so on both, and close to half of productions came within two semitones — far more precision than chance guessing across a vocal range would produce. A comparative replication across six European labs (Frieler, Fischinger, Schlemmer, Lothwesen, Jakubowski & Müllensiefen 2013, N=277) confirmed the phenomenon at a smaller effect size: about 25% of participants were exact on at least one song and 4% on both, with roughly 69% landing within one semitone. The core finding — long-term memory retains absolute, not just relative, pitch for known recordings — held up; the size of the effect did not fully replicate, a "decline effect" worth flagging rather than ignoring.

The companion finding is tempo. Levitin & Cook (1996), analyzing productions from the same singing-from-memory paradigm (N=46), found 72% of sung tempos fell within 8% of the recording's actual tempo across two trials — close to the just-noticeable difference for tempo, meaning tempo memory is nearly as precise as moment-to-moment tempo perception. Together (sometimes called the "Levitin effect") these findings mean that for a recording a listener knows well, both its pitch level and its speed are part of what gets remembered, not just its melodic and harmonic shape — a specificity of memory a generative engine, which by construction has no single canonical recording of a piece, cannot leverage directly but should understand as a ceiling on what human memory is capable of.

### Recognizing a song from a fraction of a second

Carol Krumhansl's "plink" experiments (2010) pushed the question of how little audio is needed for recognition to an extreme: 300 ms and 400 ms clips ("plinks"), randomly windowed from pop recordings spanning the 1960s–2000s across ten genres, played with no other context. At 400 ms — shorter than a spoken syllable — participants correctly named both artist and title on more than a quarter of trials, with confidence ratings showing the identifications were consciously recognized rather than lucky guesses; performance rose further when the fragment happened to catch part of a lyric from the title. Even on trials where the song went unidentified, listeners gave consistent, non-random judgments of the excerpt's genre, era, and emotional tone. This is a striking demonstration that the features carrying a recording's identity — timbre, production texture, instrumentation — are picked up almost instantly, well before anything resembling a melodic phrase has unfolded; it is a corpus-relative, enculturated skill of the same kind [musical-expectation.md](musical-expectation.md) describes for statistical learning of style, operating on a sub-second timescale.

### Aging and dementia (brief, hedged)

Musical memory is often reported as unusually well preserved in Alzheimer's disease and related dementias even as episodic and verbal memory deteriorate. Baird & Samson's review (2009) synthesizes this as a **dissociation between implicit and explicit musical memory**: the felt sense of familiarity with a known tune, and the ability to sing or continue it, tend to survive much further into the disease than the ability to explicitly name the song, recall facts about it, or learn genuinely new melodies. Hold this picture loosely — the underlying literature is a mix of case studies and small samples, "preserved" is relative rather than absolute and does not extend uniformly to every task or patient, and mechanisms are debated. The narrow, safe takeaway: familiarity-based recognition of music looks like an unusually robust memory system, one more data point for treating familiarity (see [repetition-and-familiarity.md](repetition-and-familiarity.md)) as a serious design lever rather than a crutch.

## Earworms: prevalence, features, triggers, and liking

Involuntary musical imagery (INMI) — a fragment of music replaying in the mind unbidden — is close to a universal experience. Liikkanen's large Finnish internet survey (2008, N ≈ 12,000+) found roughly 90% of respondents (estimates in the 89–92% range depending on question wording) reported an earworm at least weekly, with women and people with more musical practice/listening reporting them more often. [repetition-and-familiarity.md](repetition-and-familiarity.md) already summarizes Jakubowski, Finkel, Stewart & Müllensiefen's (2017) melodic-feature analysis briefly; the precise result is worth restating here since it is the single most actionable finding in this literature. Comparing 100 songs frequently named as earworms against 100 matched non-earworm songs across 83 corpus-derived features, earworm tunes had **faster average tempo**, a **more common, generic global melodic contour** (the ordinary rise-then-fall shape), and — the counterintuitive part — **less common, statistically unusual pitch intervals between the melody's contour turning points**: a familiar overall shape carrying one locally distinctive detail that breaks the pattern (their own examples: the "Smoke on the Water" riff, the "Bad Romance" chorus hook).

Earworm *onset* has been studied separately from earworm *content*. Williamson, Jilka, Fry, Finkel, Müllensiefen & Stewart (2012), coding open-ended reports, grouped triggers into four categories: recent **music exposure** (having just heard the song or something similar), **memory associations** (a person, place, or event cueing the tune), **affective states** (mood, stress), and **low-attention states** (boredom, mind-wandering, low cognitive load) — no single trigger dominant, INMI arising across "a broad range of situations" rather than from one clean cause. Critically, INMI is **not the same as liking**. Williamson, Liikkanen, Jakubowski & Stewart's "Sticky Tunes" study (2014, combining a Finnish sample of 11,833 with an English sample of 5,989) found most INMI episodes rated neutral-to-pleasant, but around a third rated as disturbing or annoying — and the same coping behavior (singing the tune out loud, seeking out the full recording) could follow either a positive or a negative evaluation of the same episode. Annoyance and catchiness are separable reactions to the same stuck-tune phenomenon, not opposite ends of one scale.

## Hooks and catchiness: the Hooked on Music project

The most direct empirical attack on "what makes a fragment catchy" is Burgoyne, Bountouridis, Van Balen & Honing's citizen-science game *Hooked* (2013), later played by roughly 200,000 people worldwide by some accounts. The game plays a song from a random internal section, mutes it for a few seconds while the player tries to keep singing along mentally, then either resumes the audio at the correct point or jumps elsewhere — the player's job is to recognize the song as fast as possible and then judge whether playback resumed correctly. The team's working definition: a **hook** is "the most salient, easiest-to-recall fragment of a piece of music." The headline finding is simple but important: different sections of the *same* song differ significantly in how quickly they are recognized — a song is not uniformly catchy, specific fragments are. Follow-on corpus analysis of the game's data (Van Balen, Burgoyne, Bountouridis, Müllensiefen & Veltkamp 2015) linked faster recognition to within-song repetition of the fragment and prominence of the vocal line, and a related chorus-detection analysis found timbral features tracked "chorus-ness" more strongly than harmonic or pitch content — catchiness rides on production texture and repetition structure at least as much as on note choices.

Two further findings sharpen the concept. First, catchiness is not the same thing as measured memorability: Bechtold, Curry & Witek (2024), testing 240 short instrumental clips (drum/bass/keys-guitar excerpts) against both a recognition task and subjective catchiness ratings, found objective recognition performance only weakly predicted how catchy listeners *rated* the same clip (R² ≈ 0.037); perceived catchiness is a multidimensional, subjective bundle of memorability, positive affect, engagement, and distinctiveness, and it drives the pleasurable urge to move (see [groove-and-embodiment.md](groove-and-embodiment.md)) mainly through the pleasure it generates rather than directly. Second, catchiness is not the same thing as judged musical quality (see [what-makes-music-good.md](what-makes-music-good.md)): a fragment can be extremely sticky without being well-regarded, and vice versa — correlated in the culture's biggest hits, but conceptually and empirically separable.

Where the science stops, practitioner convention picks up (labeled here as craft convention, not research finding): pop songwriting norms place a song's hook early — often within the first 15–30 seconds, an economically-driven convention sharpened by streaming platforms counting a play only after roughly 30 seconds of listening — and repeat it at structurally prominent points (the chorus, and increasingly a "post-chorus" hook after the chorus). Verse-chorus(-bridge) is the dominant modern pop form precisely because it reaches the hook quickly and then reinforces it through scheduled return, the same establish-then-vary logic covered in [repetition-and-familiarity.md](repetition-and-familiarity.md).

## Singability and motor simulation (hedged)

A melody sung by a voice — even a wordless "la-la" rendition — is remembered better than the identical melody played on an instrument: the **vocal advantage** (Weiss, Trehub & Schellenberg 2012). It holds across ages, holds for trained pianists tested on piano versus vocal versions of the same tune (Weiss, Vanzella, Schellenberg & Trehub 2015), and — tellingly — holds despite listeners liking the vocal versions less in some of these studies, which rules out "vocal melodies are simply preferred" as the explanation. One account is **motor/sensorimotor simulation**: hearing a voice recruits the listener's own vocal-motor system as part of encoding. Wood, Rovetti & Russo (2020) found the vocal advantage disappeared when listeners were forced to whisper (vocal-motor interference) while encoding a melody but survived ordinary manual tapping, consistent with a motor account. The story is contested, though: Weiss, Bissonnette & Peretz (2021) found the advantage persisted even when listeners silently mouthed "la-la" or hummed a *different* melody during encoding — interference that should have blocked simple subvocal rehearsal — leading them to argue the voice's advantage is not purely rehearsal but something closer to heightened engagement with a biologically significant signal. Treat "melodies stick better when the voice, real or simulated, is involved" as reasonably solid and the exact mechanism as open.

This raises an obvious question for an instrumental-only project: why do purely instrumental hooks (guitar riffs, sax lines, synth stabs) work at all? Jakubowski et al.'s own earworm examples include a guitar riff ("Smoke on the Water") alongside vocal hooks, and the hook/catchiness literature above is never restricted to vocal melodies — repetition, a generic-shape-plus-distinctive-detail contour, rhythmic distinctiveness, and structurally prominent placement do the load-bearing work regardless of sound source. It is a reasonable but untested inference — informed speculation, not a directly studied claim — that listeners partly extend vocal-style engagement to non-vocal hooks by covertly singing, humming, or air-playing them; most people can hum a famous riff on request. An instrumental engine can plausibly recapture part of the vocal advantage by giving a hook voice-adjacent qualities (a sustained, breath-shaped, or formant-colored timbre; see [timbre-and-orchestration.md](timbre-and-orchestration.md)) even without literal singing. See [rock-and-pop.md](rock-and-pop.md) for the riff as this project's clearest instrumental-hook case study.

## Implications for generative engines

- **Build hooks from the earworm recipe, not intuition.** A conventional, easy-to-parse global contour (arch or simple rise-fall, per [melody.md](melody.md)) carrying one rhythmically and intervallically distinctive local gesture is the best-evidenced shape for stickiness (Jakubowski et al. 2017). Do not make every note unusual — distinctiveness against a familiar backdrop is the mechanism, not novelty throughout.
- **Weight rhythm and tempo, not just pitch.** Faster tempo and rhythmic distinctiveness are earworm/catchiness correlates independent of pitch content (Jakubowski 2017; Hébert & Peretz 1997 on rhythm as a real, if secondary, identity cue); a hook-authoring routine should treat the rhythmic cell as at least as load-bearing as the pitch contour.
- **Place the hook early and return to it often, with variation.** Convention (pop songwriting) and evidence (the establish-then-vary guidance in [repetition-and-familiarity.md](repetition-and-familiarity.md)) agree: state the hook near the start of a piece or section, repeat it at structurally strong points, and vary secondary parameters on repeats rather than looping it identically forever.
- **Expose a "hook prominence" style-pack parameter.** Catchiness is now reasonably well operationalized — repetition of a short fragment, rhythmic/melodic distinctiveness, and prominent, salient timbre/placement (the Hooked-project correlates above) — so it can be a tunable dial: how sharply one motif is foregrounded, timbrally distinguished, and repeated relative to the surrounding texture, from near-absent (ambient/background styles) to dominant (pop/EDM lead lines). This is exactly the kind of engine-original, evidence-motivated control [control-surfaces-and-user-parameters.md](control-surfaces-and-user-parameters.md) should catalogue.
- **Treat catchiness as a dial, not a default maximum.** A prominent hook is, almost by definition, a locally salient, timbrally-changing event — precisely the "changing-state" acoustic profile that the irrelevant-sound-effect literature in [attention-and-background-listening.md](attention-and-background-listening.md) identifies as disruptive to background/cognitive-work listening. High hook prominence serves foreground, attentive listening; background/functional modes should default hook prominence low or off, independent of how good the hook is.
- **Consider voice-adjacent timbre for hooks that want maximum stickiness.** The vocal-memory advantage (Weiss et al. 2012, 2015, 2021) suggests a synthesized hook voice with breath noise, formant shaping, or portamento (voice-like continuous pitch motion; see [timbre-and-orchestration.md](timbre-and-orchestration.md) and [original-sound-design.md](original-sound-design.md)) may be remembered better than a flat synth-pluck rendition of the identical notes — an untested but low-cost hypothesis worth an engine A/B test (see [listening-tests-and-feedback.md](listening-tests-and-feedback.md)).
- **Do not confuse recognizability with quality.** Catchiness and judged quality are empirically separable (Bechtold et al. 2024; [what-makes-music-good.md](what-makes-music-good.md)); optimizing purely for a hook-prominence score is not the same project as optimizing for "pleasing, inspiring, interesting." Hook engineering is one lever among the many this wiki documents, not a replacement for them.

## Open questions

- Does a browser engine's inherent lack of one canonical, repeatedly-heard recording (most listeners hear a given generated piece once, or a few times at most) mean the near-absolute pitch/tempo memory findings are simply inapplicable, or does even a single listen leave a coarser version of the same trace? Untested for generated music.
- Whether voice-adjacent synthesis timbre actually improves recall of a synthesized instrumental hook, as hypothesized above, is a concrete, cheap experiment this project could run.
- How does a "hook prominence" dial interact with the repetition-satiation ceiling in [repetition-and-familiarity.md](repetition-and-familiarity.md)? A very salient hook may satiate faster under compressed repetition than a low-salience texture — untested for algorithmically generated material.
- The Hooked project's corpus-level predictors (repetition, vocal-line prominence, timbral "chorus-ness") come from human-recorded pop; whether they transfer to fully synthesized, non-human-performed audio is unverified.
