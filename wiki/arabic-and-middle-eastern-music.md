# Arabic and Middle Eastern music

The art-music traditions of the Arab world, Turkey, and Iran share a family of ideas descended from a common medieval theoretical root but long since diverged: melody is generated from small pitch-cells (tetrachords and pentachords) chained into a modal path with its own habitual direction, cadence points, and modulation routes, realized over cyclic rhythmic patterns and, centrally, through real-time improvisation. This page treats maqam (Arabic), makam (Turkish), and dastgah (Persian) as generative grammars in the same sense [indian-classical-music](indian-classical-music.md) treats raga: not scales to sample from, but rule systems with a start, a course, and an end. [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md) already covers the quarter-tone intonation debate and gives Rast's cents; this page does not repeat that math and instead covers the melodic and rhythmic *practice* — the gap the wiki previously flagged in `index.md`'s coverage list.

## Maqam as a system

### Jins: the melodic building block

A **jins** (Arabic "kind, type, gender," plural **ajnas**; cognate with Greek *genus*) is a 3-, 4-, or 5-note melodic fragment — "the basic melodic unit in Arabic music, as a maqam is really a pathway among many ajnas" (MaqamWorld, "The Jins"). Each jins has a **tonic** (its main resolution point, usually the first note), a **ghammaz** (the secondary emphasis point and the note from which a new jins most commonly begins — loosely analogous to a dominant), and, outside that basic tonic-to-ghammaz span, **jins baggage**: extension notes above or below that are part of the jins's idiomatic vocabulary without belonging to its core scale (MaqamWorld, "The Jins"). Intervals are fixed under transposition — a jins keeps its character wherever it starts — and are conventionally measured in tones, three-quarter-tones (neutral seconds, the interval with no Western equivalent), and semitones; see [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md) for exact cents and the century-long argument over whether a stable 24-quarter-tone grid even exists in performance (it does not — Maalouf 2002, cited in Abu Shumays 2013, reports this was demonstrated at the 1932 Cairo Congress of Arab Music).

### The principal ajnas

The table gives each jins's conventional reference spelling in scientific pitch notation (as notated by MaqamWorld, cross-checked note-by-note against its published pitch data), tonic first:

| Jins | Notes, tonic→ghammaz | Approx. interval pattern | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rast | C4 D4 E4-half-flat F4 G4 (5 notes) | tone, ¾-tone, ¾-tone, tone | bright but softened by the neutral 3rd; the single most common maqam family |
| Bayati | D4 E4-half-flat F4 G4 (4 notes) | ¾-tone, ¾-tone, tone | the quintessential neutral-second sound; arguably the most-used maqam overall |
| Sikah | E4-half-flat F4 G4 (3 notes) | ¾-tone, tone | tonic itself sits on a neutral pitch with no 12-TET equivalent at all |
| Hijaz | D4 E4-flat F4-sharp G4 (4 notes) | semitone, augmented 2nd (~300¢), semitone | the augmented-second sound borrowed into klezmer and flamenco |
| Nahawand | C4 D4 E4-flat F4 G4 (5 notes) | tone, semitone, tone, tone | close to the Western melodic-minor lower pentachord |
| Kurd | D4 E4-flat F4 G4 (4 notes) | semitone, tone, tone | the Western Phrygian tetrachord — the one common jins built entirely from 12-TET steps |
| Saba | D4 E4-half-flat F4 core, continuing irregularly to G4-flat/A4-natural/B4-flat | ¾-tone, ¾-tone [core]; then an unusual near-augmented continuation | famously unstable, "unresolved" quality; MaqamWorld calls its size "ambiguous" |
| 'Ajam | C4 D4 E4 F4 G4 (5 notes) | tone, tone, semitone, tone | the Western major pentachord; named "foreign" (*'ajam* = non-Arab) for its Western-major color |

Hijaz's outer semitones are conventionally played narrower than notated in practice — the 2nd degree raised slightly, the 3rd lowered — while the augmented 2nd stays wide (MaqamWorld, "Jins Hijaz"). Kurd, notably, needs no quarter-tone at all, which makes it the cheapest jins to realize convincingly in a plain 12-TET synth.

### Maqam: root jins + upper jins at the ghammaz

A **maqam** (plural **maqamat**) is "a system of scales, habitual melodic phrases, modulation possibilities, ornamentation techniques and aesthetic conventions" (MaqamWorld, "The Arabic Maqam") built by chaining two — occasionally three — ajnas: the first jins sets the tonic at scale-degree 1, and the next jins begins at the first jins's ghammaz. Maqamat are grouped into **families** by shared root jins (the root jins "plays the largest role in defining the maqam's character"); the family's namesake maqam is usually its most common member, with **branch maqamat** built on the same root but a different upper jins. Worked examples, all from MaqamWorld's own maqam pages: Maqam **Rast** = Jins Rast (tonic) + [Upper Rast **or** Nahawand] on the 5th degree; Maqam **Bayati** = Jins Bayati (tonic) + [Nahawand **or** Rast] on the 4th; Maqam **Hijaz** = Jins Hijaz (tonic) + [Nahawand **or** Rast] on the 4th; Maqam **Saba** (family-less) = Jins Saba (tonic), *overlapped* by Jins Hijaz on the 3rd degree, continuing to [**'Ajam** or **Nikriz**] on the 6th — three ajnas, one of them overlapping rather than simply chaining at the ghammaz, which is already a documented exception to the tidy two-jins model.

That tidiness is itself contested. Sami Abu Shumays' 2013 *Music Theory Spectrum* analysis argues the classical chained-tetrachord picture undersells what performers actually do: he reclassifies jins "size" by its typical modulation degree rather than fixed note-count (Rast and Nahawand behave as pentachords because their practical secondary tonic sits on the 5th, not the 4th), and represents a maqam's full melodic vocabulary as **a graph** — nodes are (jins, scale-degree) pairs, edges are the modulations performers actually use — rather than one fixed scale. His summary claim: "there are no maqam scales made from disjunct ajnas; all are made from conjunct (or overlapping) ajnas of different sizes." Record this as a live disagreement between the pedagogical model (MaqamWorld, and the older Al-Hilu 1958 / Allah Werdi 1948 theory) and Abu Shumays' practice-derived refinement, not a settled question.

### Sayr and qafla

**Sayr** ("path, course") is "the maqam's melodic course (in both composed or improvised music)" (MaqamWorld) — which tones open a piece, which are emphasized mid-course, which ajnas it habitually visits and in what order, and where it closes. Classical descriptions are often thin ("starts at the bottom" vs. "starts at the top" — Abu Shumays 2013); Turkish theory is more explicit about direction (below). Sayr is not fixed per maqam: Maqam **Muhayyar** is a sayr-variant of Bayati that opens at the octave, ascends through Jins Rast on the 4th degree, lingers on the octave Bayati, then descends — same scale, different itinerary, different name (MaqamWorld, "Maqam Bayati"). A **qafla** (colloquially "lock" or "closure") is the cadential phrase that closes a section on the tonic — "a cadential point usually followed by pauses," typically melodically or rhythmically ornamented, that "confirm[s] the previously settled...maqam" and discharges the phrase's tension (Irtijal/tarab performance-practice literature, via ResearchGate). One scholar in that literature goes so far as to argue the Arab qafla's expressive density "ha[s] no parallel even in Turkish taqsim" — a strong claim, flagged here as a single source's assessment rather than consensus.

## Modulation: the jins-graph and taqsim

Modulation — changing jins or maqam mid-phrase — happens mostly **between ajnas**, not by jumping whole maqamat, and follows well-worn routes: ajnas of the same effective size modulate most readily to each other on the same tonic, and each jins has one or two habitual degrees at which a new jins typically enters (Abu Shumays 2013). MaqamWorld's per-maqam pages and Abu Shumays' graphs (his Example 23 for Bayati, for instance) both document these routes explicitly — which is exactly the "jins-graph" idea: a maqam is not one scale but a small directed graph of ajnas-on-degrees, with edges weighted by how common that particular pivot is in the repertory (some pivots are core to the maqam's identity; others are rare, one-piece idiosyncrasies). The **taqsim** (plural *taqasim*) is the vehicle that tours this graph out loud: "an instrumental improvisation guided by a traditional framework that very closely follows a maqam's sayr (including intonation, phrasing and modulation)... [it] must follow a narrative arc and consist of sections separated by mini cadences and brief pauses" (MaqamWorld, "The Taqsim"); oud, violin, nay, and qanun are its most common vehicles. Rhythmic cycles have their own small modulation habits too: Maqsum trades with Baladi and Wahda, Baladi with Maqsum and (2:1) Malfuf, Saidi with Maqsum and Baladi — an iqa'-level echo of the same graph idea (MaqamWorld, iqa' pages).

## Iqa'at: rhythmic cycles

An **iqa'** (plural **iqa'at**) is a repeating rhythmic skeleton built from two strokes — **dum** (deep, at the drumhead center) and **tak** (dry, at the rim) — plus rests, which MaqamWorld's own notation marks with the syllable **s** (silence). Over a hundred iqa'at are named, but a handful cover most of the repertory (MaqamWorld, "Arabic Iqa'at"; Wikipedia, "Rhythm in Arabic music"). Patterns below are transcribed directly from MaqamWorld's published notation graphics and cross-checked against an independently compiled practitioner reference ("Carmine's Rhythm Cheat Sheet," credited to Raquy Danziger, Karim Nagi, Daveed, and Jas) — the two sources agree stroke-for-stroke on every pattern below:

| Iqa' | Meter | Skeleton (D=dum, T=tak, · =rest) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Maqsum | 4/4 | D T · T \| D · T · | the single most common iqa' in Arabic music (MaqamWorld) |
| Baladi (Masmudi Saghir) | 4/4 | D D · T \| D · T · | "little Masmudi"; the core Tarab-song vocal cycle |
| Saidi | 4/4 | D T · D \| D · T · | Upper-Egyptian; associated with the tahtib stick-dance |
| Malfuf | 2/4 | D · T T (approx.) | fast "Dum-Tek-Tek"; entrances and folk dance; sources place the exact subdivision slightly differently but agree on the shape |
| Wahda | 4/4 | D · · T \| · · T · | only one dum per bar — deliberately spacious, "leav[ing] a lot of room for the singer to ornament" (MaqamWorld, "Iqa' Wahda") |
| Samai Thaqil | 10/8 (3+2+2+3) | D · · \| T · \| D D \| T · · | the classical instrumental signature; = Turkish *aksak semai* |
| Masmudi Kabir | 8/4 | D D · T \| D · T T | "big Masmudi," Baladi's slow, ceremonial big sibling; the usual Bashraf cycle |

Performance never stays at the skeleton. "In practice, percussionists ornament an iqa' (flesh it out) with additional beats: dum-s, tak-s and whatever other sounds the instrument is able to produce" (MaqamWorld, "Arabic Iqa'at"). Comparing MaqamWorld's and Carmine's "basic" vs. "filled" versions of the same iqa' shows the mechanism concretely: fills are inserted almost entirely into the *rest* slots as quiet ghost strokes (a soft left-hand "ka," or extra taks), while the load-bearing dum/tak positions that define the iqa's identity stay put — ornamentation is additive, not a rewrite of the skeleton.

## Forms and ensemble

**Taqsim** (above) is unmetered and instrumental; its vocal counterparts are **layali** (a melismatic prelude on the fixed filler phrase "Ya Layl Ya 'Ayn") and **mawwal** (solo improvisation on a colloquial poem, e.g. the seven-line AAABBBA "Baghdadi" type) — all three are, per MaqamWorld, vehicles for a maqam's sayr and the performer's ornament and voice as much as for any fixed text.

The **samai** (*sama'i*) is the best-preserved Ottoman-derived instrumental form: four sections (**khana**, plural *khanat*) each followed by the same refrain, the **taslim** — structure K1-T-K2-T-K3-T-K4-T. The first three khanat and the taslim are set in Samai Thaqil (10/8); the 4th khana conventionally switches to "a short odd rhythmic cycle like Iqa' Sama'i Saraband [3/8] or Iqa' Sama'i Darij [3/4]" in the Arabic tradition (MaqamWorld, "Ottoman Instrumental Forms"). This is a place to be precise about a commonly blurred claim: the "10/8 drops to 6/8 for the last khana" convention is real, but it specifically describes the *Ottoman/Turkish* **saz semaisi** (instrumental semai), whose 4th **hane** conventionally moves to the *yürük semai* usul (6/8 or 6/4), not the Arabic samai's more common 3/8 or 3/4 (Wikipedia, "Yürük semai"; MaqamWorld). Both traditions keep the taslim/teslim itself unchanged across the meter switch. The **longa** is faster and lighter (khanat in the "short and jumpy" Iqa' Fox, taslim in Malfuf), often in a semitone-heavy maqam like Nahawand, Hijaz, or Nikriz "evok[ing] Eastern European music" (MaqamWorld). The **bashraf**, least common today, keeps one long, unchanging iqa' throughout (Masmudi Kabir 8/4 or longer Ottoman cycles) rather than varying meter by khana. The **tahmila** is a small-takht form that alternates a full-ensemble refrain with a verse "part-composed and part improvised," each player taking a turn — composed heterophony and turn-taking improvisation in the same form (MaqamWorld, "The Tahmila").

The **takht** is the traditional chamber ensemble: oud and qanun (the *naqr*, "plucking/hammering" family) plus nay and violin (the *sahb*, "pulling/stretching" family), with riqq (tambourine) as the core percussion, "meant to complement each other in order to create a richer and more complete sound" (MaqamWorld, "Arabic Musical Instruments"). All instruments typically render **the same melodic line at once**, each ornamenting it in its own idiom — heterophony, not harmony: the oud with fast slides and turns, the qanun with tremolo and lever-driven microtonal bends, the nay with breath-shaped glides, the violin with continuous portamento. This texture is the direct Middle Eastern analogue to the East Asian and gamelan heterophony discussed in [east-asian-traditions](east-asian-traditions.md) and [gamelan](gamelan.md), and a natural point of comparison for [ornamentation-and-embellishment](ornamentation-and-embellishment.md).

**Tarab** — roughly "musical ecstasy," with no exact English equivalent — is A. J. Racy's subject in *Making Music in the Arab World*: an emotional state co-created by performer and audience through modal improvisation, vocal ornament, and precise intonation, historically documented (Sawa counts over 500 references in the 10th-century *Kitab al-Aghani*) in audiences weeping, calling out, and physically responding in real time. Racy names *saltanah* — a state of "modal ecstasy" close to a flow state — as the performer's inspired condition, reached partly through the qafla's cadential release and partly through the live feedback loop with an engaged audience (Racy 2003, via AramcoWorld 2017 and secondary reviews). Tarab is the esthetic goal that sayr, qafla, and taqsim are built to serve; a maqam engine that gets the grammar right but has no equivalent of a "build, ornament, release" arc will produce correct-sounding but inert output.

## Turkish makam

Turkish **makam** shares the tetrachord/pentachord (Turkish *dörtlü*/*beşli*, collectively *cinsler*, "genera") architecture with Arabic maqam but formalizes it differently. Tuning is referred not to quarter-tones but to the **Arel-Ezgi-Uzdilek (AEU) system**: the octave divided into 53 near-equal Holdrian commas, with a whole tone equal to 9 commas — a finer, Pythagorean-derived grid distinct from the Arab 24-quarter-tone convention (Wikipedia, "Turkish makam"; see [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md) for the general comma/JI math, not repeated here). Formal theory even distinguishes cognate scales: Turkish Rast's 3rd degree is specified as a Pythagorean-derived just major third rather than the neutral third of Arab Rast (Wikipedia, "Turkish makam") — though, consistent with the theory-vs-practice gaps documented throughout this page, how strictly performers hold that line in practice is not something this page can verify.

Turkish theory makes **seyir** (the same word as Arabic *sayr*) an explicit, named category rather than a loose description: makamat are classed as **çıkıcı** (ascending — opens low, develops upward), **inici** (descending — opens high, works downward), or **inici-çıkıcı** (mixed) (Wikipedia, "Turkish makam"; Signell 1977/2004, *Makam: Modal Practice in Turkish Art Music*). This is a directly usable, coarse-grained parameter that Arabic sources leave mostly implicit. **Usul** is "the rhythmic counterpart of makam" (Wikipedia); the *aksak* ("limping") family of additive cycles built from unequal 2s and 3s — 5/8, 7/8, 9/8 and beyond — is already covered generically in [rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md) and not re-derived here, but named Turkish cycles are worth having on hand: **Türk Aksağı** (5/8, 3+2), **Devr-i Hindî** (7/8), and **Aksak** proper (9/8, 2+2+2+3).

Ottoman instrumental forms extend the samai family upstream. The **peşrev** is the grander opening form: four hane (movements) plus an unvarying **mülazime**/teslim refrain, commonly with the 1st hane and the refrain fixed in the piece's principal makam, the 2nd and 4th visiting "neighboring" makamat, and the 3rd pushing to a higher register before the refrain restores the tonic — set in one long usul (e.g. *devr-i kebir*) held constant throughout, unlike the samai's meter-switching 4th khana. The **saz semaisi** ("instrumental semai") is the samai's closer Turkish relative: four hane and a mülazime in a lighter usul, with the 4th hane conventionally in yürük semai (6/8/6/4) as already noted above.

## Persian dastgah

Persian classical music replaced an earlier maqam-descended system with the **dastgah** framework, standardized in the 19th century and substantially fixed by Mirzā Abdollāh Farāhāni (1843-1918) — "no new dastgah or large gusheh" has been added since (Wikipedia, "Dastgāh"). Twelve systems are in standard use: seven full **dastgah** (Shur, Mahur, Homayun, Nava, Segah, Chahargah, Rast-Panjgah) and five subsidiary **avaz** (Abu Ata, Bayat-e Tork, Afshari, and Dashti, all attached to Shur; Bayat-e Esfahan, attached to Homayun) — with Shur and Mahur regarded as the "mother" modes from which the others branch (Wikipedia, "Dastgāh"; Foundation for Iranian Studies). The **radif** is the complete memorized corpus of **gushehs** — short melodic units, each with "its particular contour, name, pitch centers, optional modulations, and expressive idioms" — that make up all twelve systems; different masters' radifs (Mirzā Abdollāh's, Musā Ma'rufi's, and others) disagree somewhat on count and content, so cited totals genuinely vary (roughly 250-365 gushehs depending on source and compilation) — hedge accordingly. Hormoz Farhat's influential *The Dastgah Concept in Persian Music* insists this is not a scale system at all: "there is no concept of scale in Iranian music" — a dastgah performance moves through a network of gushehs rather than filling in a fixed pitch set, uses as few as four or five pitches at a time (occasionally up to seven), and, notably, uses no interval smaller than about 90 cents — coarser than the Arab neutral second (Farhat, via Ableton Tuning summary). A performance's **forud** ("descent") is its cadential return: after ranging through related gushehs and modal excursions, a descending, often accelerating phrase re-lands on the home tonic and closes the modal episode — functionally close to the Arabic qafla and the raga tihai's role as a mechanical, reliable cadence-generator (see [indian-classical-music](indian-classical-music.md)).

Persian performance alternates **rhythmically free** and **metric** material rather than blending them: gusheh and daramad (the mode's opening) are unmetered and prose-like; tasnif (composed song), pishdaramad (metered prelude), reng (dance finale), and **chaharmezrab** ("four plectrum-strokes" — a fast, virtuosic metered solo, often in 6/8) are pulsed. A typical recital sequence runs pishdaramad → chaharmezrab → (free) avaz → tasnif → reng — pulse bracketing a free improvised core, the mirror image of the alap-to-gat acceleration in [indian-classical-music](indian-classical-music.md) rather than a continuous free-to-metric arc. Core timbres: **santur** (hammered box zither, bright and shimmering), **tar** (long-necked double-bowl skin-topped lute, warm and resonant), **setar** (its smaller four-string cousin, softer and more intimate), **ney** (end-blown reed flute, breathy with wide pitch-bend range), and **kamancheh** (spike fiddle, a warm, voice-like bowed tone).

## Implications for generative engines

1. **Model a jins, not a scale.** Represent each jins as `{tonic, ghammaz, coreNotes: cents[], baggage: cents[]}`; build a maqam as `rootJins + attachDegree + upperJins` (occasionally a third, possibly overlapping — don't hard-code exactly two). Use [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md)'s cents-array machinery for pitch; this page supplies the note-level content (the table above), not new intonation math.
2. **Sayr as a weighted graph, not a walk.** Encode a maqam's sayr as Abu Shumays' jins-graph: nodes are (jins, scale-degree), edges are the maqam's habitually used modulations, weighted by how common each pivot is (core-identity pivot vs. rare/single-piece pivot). Generate melody as a biased random walk over this graph, constrained by per-node emphasis weights (tonic/ghammaz high, jins-baggage notes low) — directly reusable machinery from [grammars-and-rewriting-systems](grammars-and-rewriting-systems.md) and [markov-and-statistical-models](markov-and-statistical-models.md), with the graph itself doing the work a plain Markov table can't (see that page's critique of note-level chains).
3. **Qafla/forud as parametrized cadence templates**, not ad hoc endings: a short library of stepwise-descent-to-tonic shapes (with a characteristic turn or approach figure) attached per jins, invoked whenever the graph walk needs to close a phrase or section — cheap, high-payoff, and exactly the kind of "generate backward from the landing point" device already validated for tihai in [indian-classical-music](indian-classical-music.md).
4. **Iqa' as skeleton-plus-fill.** Store each iqa' as a fixed-length `{dum, tak, rest}` array (the table above gives seven ready to ship); render performance by leaving skeleton hits untouched and probabilistically filling *rest* slots with quiet ghost strokes, density as a single knob. This is a direct, verified mechanism (not a guess) and composes cleanly with [rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md)'s timeline/Euclidean-rhythm machinery and the West African timeline concept in [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md) — an iqa' skeleton is functionally the same "positional address system" idea as a bell-pattern timeline, just realized as dum/tak/rest rather than a single stroke type.
5. **Heterophonic ensemble = one line, many idiom transforms.** Generate a single authoritative pitch/rhythm line, then run it through a small set of per-instrument transform functions (oud: turn/slide ornaments; qanun: tremolo + discrete microtonal lever-bends; nay: continuous breath-glide; violin: portamento) instead of composing separate parts — cheap richness, and the correct texture (contrast the fixed-interlocking-parts model of [gamelan](gamelan.md)/[west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md), which is a different, non-heterophonic answer to "how do multiple voices share one identity").
6. **The central contrast with the raga engine ([indian-classical-music](indian-classical-music.md)): no drone.** Raga's coherence leans heavily on a fixed tonic drone against which one mode is explored in registral depth; maqam has no drone convention at all, and its coherence instead comes from the jins-graph's own internal tonic/ghammaz hierarchy, which stays legible precisely *because* modulation is frequent, habitual, and graph-constrained rather than rare. An engine porting raga's "drone plus one mode, deepen over time" architecture to a maqam piece will misrepresent the tradition; port the graph-and-modulation architecture instead.
7. **Turkish/Persian-specific hooks:** seyir's explicit ascending/descending/mixed category is a free, coarse global parameter (bias early melodic material low or high accordingly) cheaper than deriving direction from the graph walk itself; the dastgah's free-rhythm-then-metric (or metric-bracketing-free, per the recital sequence above) alternation is a ready-made two-state form template, distinct from and simpler than raga's one-way alap-to-gat acceleration; usul's aksak families are already covered structurally in [rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md).
8. **What generalizes to ORIGINAL (non-maqam) styles.** The reusable, culturally-unmoored pattern here is "small pitch-cells (ajnas) linked at pivot tones into a navigable, weighted graph, closed by parametrized cadence formulas, over a skeleton-plus-fill rhythm timeline" — this works with invented pitch-cells and an invented tuning just as well as with Rast and Hijaz, and is a stronger foundation for a genuinely new original style than borrowing a single exotic scale (the failure mode [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md) already warns against). See [generative-music-design-patterns](generative-music-design-patterns.md) and [style-and-genre-overview](style-and-genre-overview.md).

## Open questions

- How much of Jins Saba's "unresolved" character is a real structural fact (its irregular, near-augmented continuation past the core trichord) versus received folk-theory framing that Abu Shumays' footnote already partly debunks (he argues Saba's non-octave-equivalence is not actually unique among maqamat)? Needs a closer read of Abu Shumays 2013 in full, and ideally Marcus and Touma directly rather than via citation chains.
- What is the smallest jins-graph (how many nodes/edges) that reliably reads as "this specific maqam" rather than "generically Middle Eastern," mirroring the open pakad-size question already logged in [indian-classical-music](indian-classical-music.md)?
- Exact sub-eighth placement of Malfuf's two teks varies across the sources consulted here; worth resolving against a primary notated score before an engine ships it as a precise timing template rather than a qualitative shape.
- This page leans heavily on MaqamWorld and one 2013 journal article for the Arabic material; Touma's *The Music of the Arabs* and Marcus's own publications (both cited only secondhand here, via Abu Shumays' footnotes) should be ingested directly in a follow-up session.
