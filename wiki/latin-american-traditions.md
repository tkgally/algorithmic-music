# Latin American traditions

Latin America's popular and folkloric musics share deep roots with the West African timeline practice covered in [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md), but most of what follows pairs that rhythmic infrastructure with fully tonal harmony, walking or vamping bass, and clear song form — a different engine problem than the harmony-free percussion ensembles in [percussion-music](percussion-music.md). This page fills this wiki's longest-declared coverage gap (`index.md`, "Known coverage gaps"). It complements rather than repeats [percussion-music](percussion-music.md), which already covers the Afro-Cuban rumba and Brazilian samba percussion *ensembles* (conga trio, batá, surdo/caixa/agogô/repinique) in depth — this page links there for those roles and instead supplies the clave theory, the bass/piano/guitar layer, salsa's full song form, and the regions percussion-music.md does not touch (bossa nova, choro, baião, tango, candombe, Andean, cumbia, mariachi, reggaetón). Every rhythm pattern below is given as onset positions on a numbered grid so it can be encoded directly in this project's timeline architecture ([findings-shared-lib-foundation](findings-shared-lib-foundation.md), [findings-percussion-engine](findings-percussion-engine.md)); grid conventions are stated at each pattern's first use, since some patterns are one bar of sixteenth notes and others (the two-bar claves) are two bars of eighth notes.

## Afro-Cuban: the clave system, tumbao, and montuno

The **clave** [Spanish, "key"] is the asymmetric two-bar timeline that every other part in son, rumba, and salsa locks to — the same structural role the standard bell pattern plays in Ewe music ([west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md)). Ethnomusicologist David Peñalosa, whose *The Clave Matrix* (2009) is the standard scholarly reference, calls it "the key that unlocks the enigma" of the rhythmic structure, and argues son and rumba clave are the same deep structure realized with more-duple versus more-ternary pulse feel. On a 16-position grid (two bars of 4/4, 8 positions per bar, each position one eighth note — the convention already used for son clave in [rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md)):

| Pattern | Bar 1 ("three-side") | Bar 2 ("two-side") | Onsets (of 16) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Son clave, 3-2 | beat 1, "and" of 2, beat 4 | beat 2, beat 3 | 1, 4, 7, 11, 13 |
| Son clave, 2-3 | beat 2, beat 3 | beat 1, "and" of 2, beat 4 | 3, 5, 9, 12, 15 |
| Rumba clave, 3-2 | beat 1, ≈"a" of 2, beat 4 | beat 2, beat 3 | 1, ~4.5, 7, 11, 13 |

Rumba clave keeps son clave's outer three-side onsets and both two-side onsets; only the three-side's middle note moves, delayed by one sixteenth note from the "and" of beat 2 to a sixteenth note later (commonly notated as the "a" of 2) — the single change that breaks the clean 3+3+2 symmetry and gives rumba clave its characteristic "rolling" feel. This specific placement is corroborated by two independent pedagogical sources but not cross-checked here against Peñalosa's own transcriptions directly; treat the exact sixteenth-note position as informed, not primary-verified. Clave has **direction**: 3-2 opens with tension (the syncopated three-side) and resolves into the two-side; 2-3 reverses this. A song, and every phrase within it, must stay "in clave" — melodic and harmonic accents align with one direction — and arrangers deliberately flip 3-2 to 2-3 (or vice versa) at section boundaries, most often into the louder mambo/montuno sections. The three-side's first three eighth-note onsets (1, 4, 7 of an 8-position single-bar grid) are the **tresillo** [Spanish, "triplet," here "3+3+2"] — Toussaint's Euclidean E(3,8), already covered in [rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md) — which recurs across nearly every tradition below. The **habanera** cell (one bar of 2/4, 8 sixteenth-note positions: onsets at 1, 4, 5, 7 — a dotted eighth, a sixteenth, and two eighths) is the tresillo with its second span split in two, and is the ancestor pattern for tango (below) and much of 19th-century Cuban dance music via the *contradanza*.

**Tumbao** is the anticipated bass pattern that gives Afro-Cuban and salsa bass its forward pull: instead of striking a chord's root on the downbeat, the bassist anticipates it — plays the root ahead of the beat where the harmony would "belong," on the off-beats musicians call **bombo** (the "and" of beat 2) and **ponche** (the "and" of beat 4, often tied over the barline into the next downbeat). Ethnomusicologist David García, in his study of innovator Arsenio Rodríguez, documents this as clave-dependent: the bombo accent falls on the clave's three-side bar, the ponche accent on the two-side bar, and Rodríguez's 1940s innovation was extending the older single-cell tresillo bass into a two-celled, clave-aligned tumbao that "contributed melodically" to the arrangement — a change García credits as influential well beyond Cuban music (he cites Motown, rock, and funk as later beneficiaries of anticipated bass). The harmonic point is structural: because the bass anticipates the chord change by an eighth note, listeners feel the harmony arrive *before* the downbeat confirms it, generating continuous rhythmic tension that never fully resolves until the pattern loops.

**Piano montuno and guajeo** [a Cuban ostinato pattern, "peasant-style"] construction follows the same clave logic one layer up: a *guajeo* is a repeating, syncopated arpeggiated ostinato, typically built in one- or two-bar cells whose syncopated figures lean into the three-side and resolve through the two-side, with beat 4 of the two-side bar landing in sync with the clave. In a full conjunto or salsa arrangement, piano, tres (Cuban guitar), and horns each play their own guajeo, single- or double-celled, interlocking into a dense but clave-locked composite texture — the horn/tres/piano equivalent of the interlocking ostinati in [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md). Ensemble roles for the percussion layer (conga tumbao/marcha, bongo's driving eighth-note **martillo** [hammer] pattern that switches to a handheld **campana** [cowbell] for louder sections, the **cáscara** [shell] pattern on the timbale shell derived from rumba guaguancó's stick patterns, and the mambo bell for climactic sections) are covered in depth in [percussion-music](percussion-music.md); what that page does not cover is that salsa additionally requires piano, bass, and horns locked to the same clave.

**Salsa's song form** is architecturally the most engine-relevant fact in this section: a **verso** [verse/canto, narrative, can be brief or developed] is followed by the **montuno** — an open-ended vamp, riding a short (typically 4- or 8-bar) repeating harmonic cell, built on **coro-pregón** [chorus-and-call] — a fixed choral hook (the *coro*) alternating with an improvised lead vocal (the *pregón*). The coro-pregón montuno in turn alternates with instrumental **mambo** (also called *moña*) sections and percussion breaks; changing the coro conventionally means changing the mambo that answers it. Structurally this is an **open vamp with alternating layers**, not a through-composed form — the piece can be extended indefinitely by cycling coro-pregón against mambo, which is exactly the shape of a generative loop-with-variation architecture (see Implications). For an instrumental-only engine, the coro-pregón's vocal call-and-response needs an instrumental substitute — a lead melodic voice standing in for the pregón against a fixed riff standing in for the coro.

**Danzón and cha-cha-chá**, briefly: danzón (from 1879, credited to Miguel Faílde) descends from habanera/contradanza and is built on the **baqueteo**, a composite that pairs the **cinquillo** [Spanish, "little five," the 5-onset cell E(5,8) = onsets 1, 3, 4, 6, 7 of 8 sixteenths — already given in [rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md)] on the clave's three-side with a tresillo-derived two-side; it is a slow, formal ballroom genre performed by an *orquesta típica* or charanga (flute, strings, timbales, güiro). Cha-cha-chá, developed by violinist Enrique Jorrín in the early 1950s, simplified the syncopated montuno rhythms of mambo into a steadier, more dancer-legible pattern — the genre's name is onomatopoeia for the dancers' shuffling footfall.

## Brazil: samba, bossa nova, choro, baião, maracatu

Samba's **bateria** (surdo, caixa, agogô, tamborim, repique, and their break vocabulary — *paradinha*, *virada*) is covered in [percussion-music](percussion-music.md); this page adds the layer that links percussion to harmony instruments. **Partido-alto** and its close relative/substitute **telecoteco** are the guide rhythms that connect the *cavaquinho* (small four-string guitar) and guitar comping to the percussion in urban Rio samba (from the 1930s, the Estácio-neighborhood musicians who founded modern samba carioca); telecoteco is commonly given as a 9-onset pattern on a 16-position sixteenth-note grid — onsets at positions 2, 4, 6, 7, 9, 11, 12, 14, 15 — with an "up side" starting on the second sixteenth note and a "down side" starting on the downbeat, a two-sided structure directly analogous to clave (this specific 16-position transcription rests on a single pedagogical source and should be treated as illustrative rather than definitive). Samba's family tempo runs roughly 80–130 BPM for song-form samba, with parade-ground samba-enredo/batucada pushing toward 120–140 BPM.

**Bossa nova**, invented by João Gilberto in the late 1950s, transposes samba's syncopation onto solo nylon-string guitar. The documented **batida** [Portuguese, "beat/stroke pattern"] pattern, thumb picking the bass note and fingers striking syncopated chord voicings, runs across two bars: bar 1 strikes on beat 1, the "and" of 2, and beat 4 (identical to the tresillo/son-clave three-side); bar 2 strikes on the "and" of 1, beat 3, and beat 4 — six onsets total (grid positions 1, 4, 7, 10, 13, 15 of the 16-position, eighth-note-per-slot, two-bar grid defined above), giving a genuinely two-bar, clave-like accompaniment figure even though, unlike son/rumba clave, it is a compound bass+chord guitar technique rather than a single percussive stick pattern, and performers vary it considerably. Bossa nova's other defining move is harmonic: Tom Jobim's writing leans on **ii–V–I** chains with constant chromatic passing motion, extended tonic chords (maj7, 9, 13), and chromatic-mediant substitutions such as a bII major-seventh resolving down a half step to the tonic (the "Girl from Ipanema" move) — a harmonic vocabulary imported from jazz/cool-jazz practice grafted onto samba's rhythm; see [hybridization-and-fusion](hybridization-and-fusion.md) for the fusion story this exemplifies.

**Choro** [from *chorar*, "to cry/weep," though the etymology is debated], Brazil's older instrumental salon tradition (from the 1870s), is typically built in rondo-like forms such as A-A-B-B-A or A-A-B-B-A-C-C-A, usually modulating from tonic (A) to dominant or relative (B) to subdominant (C). Its defining device is **baixaria** [bass-line filigree], a constantly improvised contrapuntal bass, traditionally on the 7-string guitar, that runs melodic lines and fills comparable in function to a Baroque continuo bass; the standard ensemble adds a solo melody instrument (flute, mandolin/*bandolim*, clarinet), *cavaquinho* for rhythm, and *pandeiro* [tambourine] for percussion.

**Baião**, the signature genre of Brazil's Northeast, popularized nationally by accordionist Luiz Gonzaga from the mid-1940s, runs a driving 2/4 groove from the trio **pé-de-serra**: *sanfona* [accordion], *zabumba* [double-headed bass drum, struck with a mallet on one head and a thin stick on the other], and triangle. The zabumba's syncopated pattern is commonly described as a low tone on beat 1, a damped high tone on beat 2, and an open high tone on an off-beat, with accordion and guitar echoing the same syncopation in the chords; this specific onset breakdown comes from secondary teaching sources rather than a scholarly transcription and should be treated as an approximation. **Forró** is the faster, more danceable branch of the same family and shares its instrumentation. **Maracatu** (specifically *maracatu de baque virado*, "turned-around beat," from Pernambuco) is a processional Afro-Brazilian tradition built around the deep-toned **alfaia** [rope-tensioned wooden bass drum, played with two beaters], its ensemble also including a snare-like *tarol*, bells (*gonguê*/*agogô*), and *abê* [beaded gourd shaker]; its hallmark is the *virada* — a syncopated cadential "turn" where the drums answer the *mestre*'s call — a break-based device structurally parallel to samba's *virada* and *paradinha*.

## Argentina and Uruguay: tango, milonga, vals, and candombe

Tango's rhythmic ancestor is the **habanera** cell defined above; the same dotted-rhythm shape, sped up and reworked, produces the **3-3-2** figure that is one of tango's (and later Astor Piazzolla's) signature patterns — structurally the tresillo (onsets at 1, 4, 7 of an 8-eighth-note bar). Tango accompaniment is built from a small named vocabulary: **marcato** — the default, march-like mode of straight, accented quarter notes ("in 4," one per beat); **síncopa** — a one-bar "question and answer" figure, e.g. two eighth notes opening the bar (the question) answered by a single note landing on beat 3 (the answer); and **arrastre** [Spanish, "drag"] — an anticipatory technique where the bandoneón opens its bellows, the piano runs an ascending scale, or the strings slide into the downbeat, dragging the ensemble forward into the next bar and producing tango's characteristic momentum. A typical *orquesta típica* pairs two to four **bandoneóns** (the reed free-bass accordion central to tango's timbre) in tight sectional writing and antiphonal exchange with the strings, over a rhythm team (piano and bass) articulating marcato, síncopa, and arrastre.

Argentine dance music divides into three named rhythmic feels sharing the same instrumentation: **tango** proper (4/4, moderate tempo, commonly cited around 120–135 BPM in practical dance contexts, though source tempo counts vary with counting convention); **milonga** (a faster, bouncier 2/4 built on the habanera-derived pattern, felt with a "hesitation" or *traspié* — a quick double-step against the beat); and **vals criollo** (Argentina's take on the 3/4 waltz, continuous and flowing). Sources disagree substantially on exact BPM figures for milonga and vals because some count beats and others count measures or dance steps — treat any single cited number cautiously and prefer the *relative* fact that milonga and vals both move distinctly faster and lighter than tango proper. **Piazzolla's tango nuevo** (from the late 1950s) kept this rhythmic vocabulary but layered in jazz-derived extended harmony and chromaticism, contrapuntal writing, and even fugal forms, plus rhythms he drew from Stravinsky and jazz — a deliberate, controversial fusion; see [hybridization-and-fusion](hybridization-and-fusion.md).

**Candombe**, Uruguay's Afro-descended drumming tradition (UNESCO-recognized intangible heritage, 2009), is carried by three drums of different size and pitch, each with a distinct role: **chico** (smallest, highest, a fast, staccato counterpoint), **repique** (middle, the improvising/conversational lead), and **piano** (largest, lowest, syncopated and downbeat-anchored — the name is unrelated to the keyboard instrument). All three additionally share a timeline called the **madera** [wood] pattern or *clave*, played on the drum shell as an introductory synchronization device before the main rhythm begins — functionally identical to the West African bell timeline and the Cuban clave, and literally called "clave" by scholars analyzing it (Jure & Rocamora document this formally). This is a third, independently-arrived-at instance of the timeline principle from [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md).

## Andean traditions: huayno and the siku hocket

**Huayno** (also *huaynho*/*wayno*), the most widespread Andean genre (Peru, Bolivia, parts of Ecuador and Chile), runs a brisk duple meter (2/4 or 4/4) with a stressed downbeat and a characteristic short-long lilt, typically over pentatonic melodies sung in Quechua, Aymara, and/or Spanish; ensembles pair *charango* [a small, originally armadillo-shell-bodied ten-string lute with a bright, percussive attack] and guitar strumming with soaring *quena* [notched-end, rim-blown cane flute] or *siku* melodic lines and a driving *bombo* (or *wankara*) drum pulse; tempo commonly cited around 100–120 BPM, though this figure is drawn from aggregator data rather than an ethnomusicological source and should be treated as approximate.

The **siku** [Aymara/Quechua panpipe, also *zampoña*] is the deepest engine-relevant device here: a full melodic or harmonic line is built from **two players, each holding half the scale**, called **ira** ["leader"] and **arca** ["follower"], who interlock their notes in strict alternation (hocket) so that neither player's part is melodically complete alone — the full line exists only in the combination, the same auditory-streaming trick documented for the Ugandan amadinda in [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md). Large ensembles of paired sikuri players (*sikuris*) produce dense heterophony this way, often moving in parallel octaves between ira and arca sections, for processions and open-air festivals. Andean pentatonicism is a genuinely widespread melodic tendency — many huayno and sikuri melodies are built on anhemitonic (no-semitone) five-note scales — but this project treats "Andean pentatonicism" as a description of common practice, not a rule: source coverage here is thin and secondary, five-note frameworks vary in exact scale-degree content across regions and repertoires, and some Andean repertoire is diatonic or uses other scale types. Treat any specific pentatonic collection as a starting default to vary, not a law; see [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md) for pentatonic scale construction generally.

## Cumbia and Mexican son: pan-Latin spread and metric ambiguity

**Cumbia** originated on Colombia's Caribbean coast from a documented three-way mixture: African-descended rhythmic practice and hand drums (**tambora**, **alegre**, **llamador**), Indigenous (Kogi) cane flutes (the paired **gaita hembra**, five-holed and melodic, and **gaita macho**, one-holed and rhythmic/droning) and maraca-type shakers, and Spanish-colonial social-dance framing. Traditionally a moderate 2/4 (often felt as a slower 4/4 today, roughly 90–110 BPM), its texture layers interlocking hand-drum parts under call-and-response gaita or, in the modern popular form, accordion or synthesizer melody, with **güiro** [scraped gourd idiophone] or maracas driving continuous subdivision and the bass outlining a cyclical, syncopated, offbeat-emphasizing line that doubles as the rhythmic anchor. From this Colombian base, cumbia spread and localized across nearly every Latin American country from the mid-20th century on (Mexican, Peruvian, and Argentine cumbia are all now distinct popular subgenres), making it one of the most pan-Latin rhythmic exports in this page.

**Mexican son** (regional families include son jarocho, son huasteco, and mariachi's son jalisciense) frequently uses **sesquiáltera** [Spanish, "one-and-a-half," i.e. a 3:2 ratio] — the deliberate, sustained juxtaposition of a 6/8 feel (two beats, each divisible into three eighths: 3+3) against a 3/4 feel (three beats, each divisible into two eighths: 2+2+2) over the *same six eighth-note pulses*. In mariachi practice this is often literally distributed across the ensemble — the *vihuela* strumming the quicker 6/8 grouping while the *guitarrón* (the large acoustic bass guitar) marks the slower 3/4 pulse — producing a genuine, sustained metric ambiguity (a "push-and-pull") rather than a single agreed-on downbeat. This is exactly the **polymeter** case defined in [rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md): the same shared eighth-note pulse train, grouped two different ways at once, realigning every six pulses (the bar) rather than every measure of a longer cycle — a cheap, precise, and highly idiomatic engine device (see Implications).

## Reggaetón and dembow

Reggaetón's rhythmic backbone, the **dembow** riddim, is a "boom-ch-boom-chick" pattern built from the tresillo (3+3+2) with extra syncopation filled into the off-beats; it descends from a specific Jamaican dancehall riddim (Steely & Clevie, ca. 1989, first used on Gregory Peck's "Poco Man Jam"), was popularized by Shabba Ranks's 1990 song "Dem Bow," and was recut, looped, and spread through Puerto Rico via DJ Playero's and DJ Negro's underground mixtapes in the mid-1990s, becoming the loop nearly every reggaetón and dembow track since has recycled. Because this is fundamentally a Jamaican-dancehall-derived pattern and lineage, its fuller treatment — and reggae/dancehall rhythm generally — belongs to [reggae-and-caribbean](reggae-and-caribbean.md); this page notes only that it is, structurally, one more tresillo-family descendant alongside son clave, habanera, and baião.

## Implications for generative engines

1. **Ship the clave family as timeline library entries**, exactly as specified above: son clave 3-2 `{1,4,7,11,13}` and 2-3 `{3,5,9,12,15}` on a 16-slot/eighth-note/two-bar grid; rumba clave as son clave with onset 2 shifted a sixteenth note later; tresillo `{1,4,7}` and cinquillo `{1,3,4,6,7}` (already in [rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md)) as one-bar, 8-position cells; habanera `{1,4,5,7}` on the same one-bar grid. These slot directly into the Euclidean/timeline machinery documented in [findings-shared-lib-foundation](findings-shared-lib-foundation.md) and the folk-style timeline layer plus percussion voice catalog built in [findings-percussion-engine](findings-percussion-engine.md) and [percussion-music](percussion-music.md) — clave is not itself a pure Euclidean output (tresillo is E(3,8), but son clave is a tresillo-family cell concatenated with a 2-onset cell, not a single E(k,16)), so it needs a small hand-authored library rather than a single generator call.
2. **Encode tumbao, montuno, surdo, and marcato as accompaniment-pattern templates**, each a function from (clave direction, harmonic rhythm) to onsets: tumbao anticipates the chord root at bombo/ponche ("and" of 2 / "and" of 4); montuno/guajeo repeats a syncopated 1–2 bar arpeggiated cell locked to the same clave side; marcato is a near-invariant accented-quarter-note bed that a síncopa or arrastre pattern interrupts at phrase boundaries. These are accompaniment *roles*, reusable the way [groove-and-embodiment](groove-and-embodiment.md)'s backbeat/ghost-note vocabulary is reusable across styles.
3. **Implement the montuno as an open-vamp form template**, generically useful beyond salsa: a short (4–8 bar) harmonic cell repeats indefinitely while a "coro" layer (a fixed instrumental hook) alternates with a "pregón/mambo" layer (a freer, more improvisational instrumental voice) — a call-and-response scheduling pattern already recommended in [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md), here elevated to a whole-section form that can extend a piece to any target length. Because this project makes instrumental-only music, substitute an instrumental lead voice for the vocal pregón and an instrumental riff for the coro; do not attempt sung call-and-response.
4. **Use sesquiáltera as a general meter device**, not a Mexican-son-only trick: represent a 6-pulse bar and let different voices group it 3+3 (compound/6-8 feel) versus 2+2+2 (simple/3-4 feel) simultaneously — this is [rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md)'s polymeter (shared pulse, different beat-groupings), realizable cheaply by giving two voices the same 6-slot grid with different accent/chord-change schedules.
5. **Style-pack sketches** (tempo, ensemble, harmony — per [style-and-genre-overview](style-and-genre-overview.md)'s three-part pack format): **Son montuno/salsa** — tempo roughly 85–110 BPM felt (sources disagree by up to 2x depending on whether they count the felt pulse or a doubled "cut" pulse used on some lead sheets); ensemble piano guajeo + anticipated bass + conga/bongo/campana + horns; harmony a short vamp, commonly I–IV–V–IV or a minor i–iv–V, repeated under the montuno. **Bossa nova** — tempo roughly 60–100 BPM; ensemble nylon guitar batida + brushed kit + upright bass + a solo horn/flute; harmony ii–V–I chains with maj7/9/13 extensions and chromatic-mediant substitutions. **Samba (song form)** — tempo roughly 80–130 BPM (faster for parade batucada); ensemble surdo trio + caixa + agogô/tamborim (see [percussion-music](percussion-music.md)) plus cavaquinho/guitar on partido-alto/telecoteco; harmony ranges from simple triadic vamps to (in samba-canção/bossa-adjacent writing) jazz-inflected extensions. **Tango** — tempo roughly 120–135 BPM; ensemble bandoneón section + strings + piano/bass rhythm team; harmony minor-key with chromatic voice leading and secondary dominants, articulated through marcato/síncopa/arrastre rather than a fixed vamp.
6. **Treat "Andean," "Latin," or "Afro-Cuban" as a family of named systems, not a genre slider** — per the Agawu caution already adopted in [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md) and the generic-folk decision in [findings-percussion-engine](findings-percussion-engine.md), name the specific tradition a style pack draws from (son montuno vs. rumba vs. songo are musically distinct) rather than shipping one averaged "Latin mode."

## Open questions

- The exact sixteenth-note placement of rumba clave's shifted onset (`~4.5` above) is corroborated by two secondary pedagogical sources but not checked here against Peñalosa's own transcriptions or an audio-derived measurement — worth a direct primary-source pass before an engine treats it as authoritative.
- The telecoteco 16-position onset list rests on one source; a second independent transcription (or an audio-onset-detection cross-check against a known recording) would firm it up before it becomes a shipped pattern.
- Baqueteo (danzón), the baião zabumba pattern, and candombe's three-drum interlock are described here only qualitatively; none has a verified numbered grid in this page — a follow-up pass modeled on this page's clave/tumbao treatment could add them.
- Tango, milonga, and vals BPM figures conflict across sources by roughly 2x depending on counting convention (beat vs. measure vs. dance-step); a session with access to metronome-marked scores or measured recordings should resolve this rather than relying on secondary aggregator sites.
- How much of "Andean pentatonicism" reflects real corpus statistics versus outsider generalization is genuinely unclear from the sources gathered here; this deserves the same scrutiny [west-african-rhythm](west-african-rhythm.md) gives Agawu's critique of "African rhythm" as a category.
