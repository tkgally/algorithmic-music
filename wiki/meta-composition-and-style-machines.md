# Meta-composition and style machines

The [history of algorithmic composition](algorithmic-composition-history.md) is usually told chronologically. This page cuts across it differently: it dissects the systems that did not merely generate one piece but tried to parameterize *composition itself* — to expose a set of dials such that setting the dials specifies a style or a work. For a project whose goal is a single engine covering a *space* of styles ([comprehensive-site-vision](comprehensive-site-vision.md), [style-invention-and-style-space](style-invention-and-style-space.md)), these are the machinery precedents. The useful questions are the same for each: **what axes did it expose, what did a "style specification" actually contain as data, what realization procedure turned that data into notes, and — judged by results, not rhetoric — what worked?** The recurring answer echoes the rest of this wiki: the systems that produced convincing music put the composer's knowledge into *thick supply tables and constraints*, and let the exposed parameters choose among pre-validated material rather than conjure it.

## What a "style machine" is, as an object

Strip away idiom and every system here has three layers: (1) an **alphabet / supply** — the raw material a style may draw on (a scale, a set of rhythmic cells, a corpus of phrases, a bank of patterns); (2) a **selection / combination procedure** — how material is chosen and ordered (serial, weighted-random, tendency-bounded, grammar-driven, recombinant); and (3) a **control surface** — the parameters a user or seed sets to pick among (1) and (2). A style, in this framing, is a *point or region* in the space those parameters span. The designs below differ mainly in where they put the intelligence: Schillinger in generative arithmetic, Koenig in selection principles, Xenakis in the alphabet, Cope in a corpus, the consumer systems in hand-authored pattern banks. See [control-surfaces-and-user-parameters](control-surfaces-and-user-parameters.md) for the control-surface question in isolation.

## The Schillinger System: composition as arithmetic

Joseph Schillinger (1895–1943), a Russian-American theorist, taught a system — published posthumously as the two-volume, ~1,640-page *Schillinger System of Musical Composition* (1946) — that recast every musical dimension (rhythm, scale, melody, harmony, counterpoint, density, even form) as arithmetic and geometric operations, explicitly to replace intuition with a "scientific," reproducible procedure applicable to any style (Wikipedia, "Schillinger system"; Absil 2015).

### Rhythm by interference of periodicities

The most durable idea is his theory of rhythm. Two (or more) pulse-streams of different periods, started together, *interfere*; the pattern of combined attack-points over their common cycle is the **resultant**. Concretely, for generators a and b over their least common multiple, mark every attack of the a-stream and every attack of the b-stream, then read off the durations between successive marks. Generators 3 and 2 over LCM 6: the 3-stream attacks at {0, 3}, the 2-stream at {0, 2, 4}; the union of attack points is {0, 2, 3, 4}, giving the resultant **2 1 1 2** — a symmetric, self-contained rhythmic cell (Absil 2015; the algorithmicpattern.org 2025 paper re-derives the same family via binomial expansion of `(a+b)²`). Schillinger then generates variety by permuting, rotating, fragmenting, and superimposing resultants. This generator is *genuinely implementable in a few lines* and *musically useful*: interference of small integers is exactly how additive and cross-rhythmic feels (3-against-2, clave-like patterns, [aksak meters](rhythm-and-meter.md)) arise, and the output is a compact integer duration-list that drops straight into a beats-based note schema.

### Pitch, scale, and melodic geometry

Schillinger encoded scales as **interval vectors** — C major as the semitone sequence (2 2 1 2 2 2 1) grouped into "tetrachord" units — and generated new scales by systematically enumerating interval combinations within an octave (Wikipedia; techpeterburg). Melody he treated geometrically: a melodic line is a trajectory over a pitch-axis and a time-axis, subject to *axes of symmetry* and transformations (transposition, inversion, rotation, expansion/contraction of the pitch geometry). The pitch material is thus another combinatorial supply table; the melodic "geometry" is a set of transformations over it.

### Combinatorial exhaustiveness — and its critics

Schillinger's rhetorical claim was exhaustiveness: that his enumerations covered *all* possibilities in each dimension, so composition became selection from a complete catalog. This is where the system drew fire. Physicist and music theorist John Backus, in "Re: Pseudo-Science in Music" (*Journal of Music Theory* 4(2), 1960, pp. 221–232), attacked precisely the scientific pretension — that the mathematics is often trivial, mis-defined, or dressed in undefined jargon, and above all that "completely unverified assertions are made regarding the aesthetic value of the numerical manipulation which fills the book" (Backus 1960, as quoted in the Schillinger-reconstruction literature). Backus labeled it both pseudo-science and pseudo-mathematics. Composer Elliott Carter is likewise reported to have reviewed it critically in 1946 as pseudo-algebraic apparatus fit for competent-but-unoriginal music (attribution via secondary summaries — worth verifying against Carter's original). The honest reading: the combinatorics are real but *do not confer musical value*; enumerating all scales or all rhythmic resultants tells you nothing about which are good. Yet the influence was real and practical — Gershwin studied with Schillinger for roughly four years (1932–36) and consulted him during *Porgy and Bess* (though the *extent* is disputed: Schillinger claimed large direct influence, Ira Gershwin denied it — Wikipedia, "Porgy and Bess"); and the system seeded Berklee College of Music, whose founder Lawrence Berk taught it (Berklee 2000).

### What is salvageable

The generators, divorced from the metaphysics: **interference rhythms** (resultants as a cheap, controllable cross-rhythm/aksak cell generator), **scale-as-interval-vector enumeration** (a formal way to *invent* modes for original styles, tied to [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md)), and **geometric melodic transforms** (inversion/rotation/augmentation over a stored contour). Take the machinery; discard the "exhaustive science" claim.

## Koenig's Project One and Project Two: the selection principle as the parameter

Gottfried Michael Koenig's Project 1 (PR1, 1964) and Project 2 (PR2, 1966) made a subtler move: the user does not set the notes, or even the material directly — the user sets **how material is selected**. PR1 is organized on a single axis Koenig called the **regular ↔ irregular (RI) principle**, with seven graded "processes" between the poles of exact serial non-repetition and free grouping (koenigproject.nl, "Project 1"). PR2 generalized this into a menu of **selection principles applied per parameter**: *ALEA* (arbitrary/random choice), *SERIES* (serial, non-repeating until the set is exhausted), *RATIO* (fixed weighted proportions), *GROUP* (selection with deliberate repetition of a value), *TENDENCY* (choice from a range that itself moves over time), and *SEQUENCE* (composer-specified explicit order) (koenigproject.nl, "Project 2"; Roads interview 1978). The composer supplies **lists** (pitches, durations, dynamics, entry delays, rests, instrument assignments) and, for each parameter, chooses which selection principle governs it and at what structural level (whole piece, section, or layer; PR2 also assembles polyphony from independently-specified layers).

Two ideas transfer directly. First, **TENDENCY** — bounds that glide over time — is the ancestor of the *tendency mask*: a time-varying `[min, max]` (or probability envelope) from which a parameter is drawn, later ubiquitous in granular synthesis and in Truax-lineage systems (composerprogrammer.com, "Strategies for Algorithmic Composition"). A tendency mask is the natural way to make a parameter (register, density, dissonance, tempo) *evolve* deterministically-yet-non-repetitively across a form. Second, **selection-principle-per-parameter** is itself a style axis: "pitch is serial but rhythm is weighted-random and dynamics follow a rising tendency" is a compact, expressive style descriptor. Koenig, like Xenakis, hand-edited the output — his design left a deliberate "transcription gap" — which this project cannot ([algorithmic-composition-history](algorithmic-composition-history.md)); the fix is to make every selection principle fully specified so nothing awaits a human.

## Xenakis: sieves and screens as style alphabets

*Formalized Music* is usually read for its stochastic distributions ([stochastic-chaos-and-automata](stochastic-chaos-and-automata.md) covers those — not repeated here). Two *other* constructs in it are style-machine parts.

**Sieves (cribles)** are Xenakis's formalism for building the *alphabet* of a style. A sieve is assembled from **residue classes** written `modulus_residue` (e.g., `3₁` = all integers ≡ 1 mod 3 = …1, 4, 7, 10…) combined with the Boolean operations **union (∪), intersection (∩), and complement** (iannis-xenakis.org, "Sieve theory"; Ariza 2005). Mapping the resulting integer sequence onto semitones (or quarter-tones, or onset-grid ticks) yields a **scale or a rhythmic sequence** — an arbitrary, precisely-specified, periodic-or-aperiodic pitch/time alphabet that is *not* limited to the diatonic or 12-TET defaults. Xenakis considered sieves "outside-time" structures: the abstract set that defines a mode independent of how it is deployed in time — exactly a **style's pitch/rhythm vocabulary as a compact logical formula**. This project already banks the idea: **Engine 05 uses a Xenakis sieve as one of its selectable timeline types** for percussion ([findings-percussion-engine](findings-percussion-engine.md)). For a style-space engine, a sieve is a one-line encoding of "this style's usable pitches/onsets," and small changes to a modulus or a residue produce audibly related-but-distinct alphabets — a knob for style variation.

**Screens and books of screens** are the complementary, *in-time* construct, from Xenakis's grain theory. A **screen** is a snapshot of the frequency × intensity plane at an instant, quantized into cells and characterized by the density and loudness of grains in each register (Xenakis, *Formalized Music*; iannis-xenakis.org). A piece is a **book of screens** — an ordered succession of such snapshots, with the *transitions between screen-states* (which Xenakis governed with probabilities/matrices) carrying the sonic evolution. Read abstractly, a screen is a **style-state vector** (here-and-now density, brightness, register spread, intensity) and a book of screens is a *trajectory through style-state space* — a direct precedent for representing a piece as a path a style vector walks over time, which is exactly the shape a form template in a style-space engine should take.

## Cope's EMI: a style as a database

David Cope's Experiments in Musical Intelligence (EMI/Emmy, from 1981) is the richest answer to "what is a style, as data?" ([algorithmic-composition-history](algorithmic-composition-history.md) covers its place and the controversy; here, its anatomy). EMI treats a style as a **segmented corpus plus derived structures**:

- **Signatures.** Cope defines a signature as a pattern of roughly **1–8 contiguous melodic intervals** (optionally with harmonic content) that **recurs across more than one work by the same composer** — "style-dependent but work-independent" (Cope, "Signatures and Earmarks," 1996; da Silva 2003). Signatures are found by pattern-matching *with controlled variance* (allowing small deviations), then cataloged by the musical roles they can play. They are what makes a recombination *sound like* the target: insert the composer's fingerprints at the right junctures.
- **Earmarks.** A related class marking *structural location* rather than identity — patterns that signal "this is a development," or foreshadow a climax or an ending (da Silva 2003). Earmarks encode *where you are in the form*.
- **SPEAC.** A hierarchical analytic labeling — **S**tatement, **P**reparation, **E**xtension, **A**ntecedent, **C**onsequent — assigned to units at every structural level, describing each unit's *tension/function* in context (not a fixed label but a relational one; Cope weighted tension by metric placement and agogic duration). SPEAC is EMI's **tension grammar**: it lets recombination respect structural function, so a "consequent" object is only substituted where a consequent belongs.
- **Recombinancy.** Generation segments the corpus into beat-scale objects, groups them into **lexicons** by SPEAC function, and reassembles them under an **augmented transition network** grammar, matching each object's ending pitch/voice-configuration to the next object's beginning so **voice-leading stays idiomatic** (da Silva 2003; Computer History Museum). "A style," operationally, is: *the segmented database + the signature dictionary + the SPEAC labeling + the recombination grammar.* Emily Howell (later) built on Emmy's output but let Cope's interactive feedback steer an evolving personal style rather than emulate a dead composer — the same machinery, different corpus and objective.

The transferable lesson for a no-corpus project ([machine-learning-music](machine-learning-music.md) explains why runtime corpora are closed to us): EMI proves that **a small dictionary of signatures + a functional (SPEAC-like) grammar + voice-leading-preserving recombination** is enough to read as a style. The project's move is to have the LLM *author* the signature bank and the functional grammar at engine-writing time instead of mining a corpus.

## Style as data in consumer and game systems

Three shipping, non-academic families independently converged on "style = a small structured record + a realization engine."

### Band-in-a-Box style files

A BiaB `.STY` file *is* a style, as fielded data: it holds, per instrument (bass, drums, piano, guitar, strings…), a bank of **patterns** (up to ~600) tagged with **masks** that gate when each may fire — substyle (A/B), chord-duration, bar, beat, chord-type, and roman-numeral masks — plus **substyles** ("a" for verses, "b" busier for bridges; MultiStyles allow up to 24), and dedicated **fills** and **endings** (PG Music manuals; Band-in-a-Box wiki). The realization engine picks compatible patterns for the current chord/section and transposes them. This is the dice-game architecture ([algorithmic-composition-history](algorithmic-composition-history.md)) as a product: **pre-validated fragments + compatibility masks + substyle switching + fills at boundaries** — a concrete, proven field list for a style-pack schema.

### SSEYO Koan → Noatikl → Wotja (Eno's chosen tool)

Brian Eno coined "generative music" working with SSEYO Koan (Generative Music 1, 1996); Koan interpreted roughly **150 parameters per piece** set by the author, "an envelope of possibilities … outside which the music will not go" (medienkunstnetz; intermorphic). The successor engine (Noatikl/Wotja) exposes its parameters through a clean **object model** worth copying: **Voices** (six voice types, e.g., following/rhythmic/ambient) governed by four kinds of **Rule** — **Scale Rule, Harmony Rule, Rhythm Rule, and Next-Note Rule** — plus **Piece** and **File** objects, with **patterns** typed as rhythm-only, both-pitch-and-rhythm, or fixed-frequency (intermorphic, NME guide). The ~150 parameters fall into recognizable categories: pitch/scale, harmony rules, rhythm/duration, next-note (melodic motion) behavior, per-voice micro-timing and probability, and timbre/effects. That factoring — **material rules × per-voice behavior × probability envelopes** — is close to the style-pack layering this project needs, and it is validated by a tool a major artist used for real work.

### Game-audio middleware (FMOD, Wwise)

Adaptive game scores encode "musical state" as **real-time parameters** — Wwise RTPCs / FMOD parameters — continuous game variables (intensity, health, region, danger) mapped by curves onto audio behavior, realized through two structural techniques: **vertical layering** (stems added/removed to change intensity without changing the tune) and **horizontal re-sequencing** (switching between musical segments at sync points) (Audiokinetic/FMOD docs; middleware surveys). This is a live demonstration that a *style/mood* can be driven by **a few continuous state parameters plus a switching structure** — directly relevant to the three-tier control surface, and to representing "where the piece is now" as a low-dimensional state that evolves.

### ML style conditioning (brief)

Learned systems give a fourth kind of evidence — that style behaves **vector-like**. MusicVAE learns a latent space in which sampling and **interpolating between two melodies yields smooth, musical intermediates** (Roberts et al. 2018); text-to-speech "global style tokens" and music genre/style embeddings similarly treat style as a continuous conditioning vector. This project ships no neural nets ([machine-learning-music](machine-learning-music.md)), but the finding motivates a **continuous style vector** as the internal representation, with presets as named points and invented styles as sampled points.

## Interpolating and extrapolating between styles — what blends, what doesn't

If a style is a vector, a style-space engine will want to *move* between styles. The evidence on what interpolates *perceptually* is uneven, so hedge:

- **Interpolates well (continuous, ordinal dimensions):** tempo, event density, dynamic level, register/spread, timbral brightness and mix balance, dissonance level, rubato/swing amount. These have perceptual midpoints; a value halfway between is heard as halfway. DJ practice is the field proof — **beatmatching** blends two tracks by aligning tempo and beat-phase, and crossfading level, so continuous dimensions merge seamlessly (Wikipedia, "Beatmatching"). Game RTPC intensity curves are the same principle in software.
- **Interpolates badly (categorical / structural dimensions):** meter (there is no perceived state "between" 3/4 and 4/4 — you hear one or the other), tuning system / mode (no audible midpoint between 12-TET and a specific raga's intonation; [tuning-and-scales](tuning-and-scales.md)), and large-scale form template. These behave like *categories*, not scalars; the working move is to **snap** (choose one endpoint) rather than average. DJ practice again illustrates: harmonic mixing **matches keys** (or moves by compatible fifths) rather than gliding between them, and forcing tracks with very different tempo/meter "usually results in a trainwreck" (harmonic-mixing/beatmatching guides).
- **The nuance and the caveat:** even a "snap" dimension can be *transitioned* (modulate the key, execute a metric modulation, cut to a new section) — that is composition, not interpolation. And MusicVAE shows that in a *learned* manifold even melodies interpolate smoothly, so "what interpolates" depends on the *representation*: quantities interpolate, symbols must be re-chosen. Where a hand-built engine's parameter is categorical, treat blends as *choices weighted by the mix*, not averages. This whole section is **partly informed speculation** — direct perceptual studies isolating each dimension are thin — and should be tightened as the project runs listening tests.

## Comparative anatomy (summary)

| System | Alphabet / supply | Selection / combination | The exposed parameter | What survives for us |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Schillinger | interval-vector scales; integer rhythm generators | interference, permutation, geometric transform | generators a, b; scale vector; transforms | resultant-rhythm & scale-invention generators |
| Koenig PR1/PR2 | user supply-lists per parameter | six selection principles; RI axis; tendency | selection principle **per parameter/level** | tendency masks; principle-per-parameter as a style axis |
| Xenakis | **sieve** (logical pitch/rhythm alphabet) | screens → book of screens (state trajectory) | moduli/residues; screen density/intensity | sieves as compact alphabets; screens as style-state path |
| Cope EMI | segmented corpus | signatures + SPEAC grammar + ATN recombination | the corpus + labels | LLM-authored signature bank + functional grammar |
| Band-in-a-Box | pattern banks per instrument | mask-gated pattern selection; substyles; fills | the `.STY` field record | fielded style schema; substyle/fill structure |
| Koan/Wotja | scale/harmony/rhythm rules per voice | rule-governed per-voice generation | ~150 params in rule categories | material-rules × per-voice-behavior × probability layering |
| FMOD/Wwise | stems + segments | vertical layering + horizontal re-sequencing | RTPC state variables | few continuous state params + switching structure |

## Implications for generative engines

- **Adopt a fielded, layered style-pack schema** synthesizing the convergent designs above. Minimum fields, grouped: **(material)** pitch alphabet (as an interval vector *or* a sieve), tuning, scale/mode, harmonic-motion type, rhythmic-cell supply (incl. interference resultants), meter/groove; **(per-voice behavior)** ensemble roles, next-note/melodic-motion rule, register range, density, and a *selection principle per parameter* (à la Koenig: serial / weighted / grouped / tendency); **(evolution)** tendency masks and a form template expressed as a *trajectory of a style-state vector* (à la books of screens) plus fills/transitions at boundaries (à la BiaB); **(surface)** timbre palette, dynamics/tempo ranges, expression profile, and beginning/ending idioms. Preset genres are hand-tuned points; invented styles are seeded samples ([style-invention-and-style-space](style-invention-and-style-space.md)).
- **Define a "new style" operationally** as EMI-minus-corpus: **a seeded sample of the schema fields + coherence constraints + two or three *audible signature rules*** (a Cope-style fingerprint the listener can latch onto — a characteristic interval cell, a rhythmic motto, a voicing habit). Signatures + a functional (SPEAC-like tension) grammar + voice-leading-preserving recombination is the historically strongest route to "reads as a coherent style," and the LLM authors the signature bank for free at build time.
- **Put three historical generators into the shared libraries** ([shared-libraries](shared-libraries.md)), each small and deterministic: **(1) Schillinger interference rhythms** — `resultant(a, b)` returning a duration-list, for cross-rhythms/aksak cells feeding [rhythm-and-meter](rhythm-and-meter.md); **(2) Xenakis sieves** — a residue-class builder with union/intersection/complement returning a pitch/onset alphabet (Engine 05 already has a sieve timeline to generalize from — [findings-percussion-engine](findings-percussion-engine.md)); **(3) tendency masks** — a `[min,max]`-over-time (or probability-envelope) sampler, the Koenig/Truax device for making any parameter evolve without repeating. All three are deterministic from the seed, satisfying the same-URL-same-piece rule ([engine-architecture](engine-architecture.md)).
- **Represent the running piece as a style-state vector on a path.** Xenakis's book-of-screens and game middleware's RTPC state independently endorse "the piece is a low-dimensional state that moves over the form." This is the natural home for tension shaping ([tension-and-release](tension-and-release.md)) and for the three-tier control surface: Start-level dials nudge a few state axes; Advanced-level dials expose the schema's original parameters ([control-surfaces-and-user-parameters](control-surfaces-and-user-parameters.md)).
- **Interpolate quantities, re-choose symbols.** Continuous fields (tempo, density, brightness, dissonance, mix) may be blended for style morphing and [hybridization-and-fusion](hybridization-and-fusion.md); categorical fields (meter, tuning, mode, form) must be *selected* (snap or weighted-choice), and transitions between them composed, not averaged.
- **Keep the anti-rhetoric lesson.** Schillinger's exhaustive combinatorics and Xenakis's/Koenig's formal apparatus generate *possibility*, never *value*; Backus's critique generalizes to every generator here. Every exposed axis must feed a constraint/selection layer carrying the musical judgment ([generative-music-design-patterns](generative-music-design-patterns.md), [what-makes-music-good](what-makes-music-good.md)), or the engine repeats the naive-generativity failure ([generative-music-failure-modes](generative-music-failure-modes.md)).

## Open questions

- Does a Cope-style signature dictionary work *without* a corpus to mine — i.e., can an LLM author fingerprints that listeners actually latch onto, and how many per style are enough? (Testable once the evaluation harness exists — [listening-tests-and-feedback](listening-tests-and-feedback.md).)
- Which schema fields are truly categorical vs. secretly ordinal? Meter and tuning feel categorical, but "amount of syncopation" or "diatonic↔chromatic" may interpolate — worth an A/B once the engine can render midpoints.
- How many state axes does a book-of-screens-style trajectory need to carry a satisfying form (the [form-and-structure](form-and-structure.md) problem) — 3? 6? — and can one trajectory representation serve genres as different as ambient and groove?
- Are the specific Backus (1960) and Carter (1946) quotations and the *extent* of Schillinger's influence on *Porgy and Bess* accurately reported by the secondary sources here? (Flagged for verification against the primary reviews.)
