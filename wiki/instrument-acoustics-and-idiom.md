# Instrument acoustics and idiom

This page is the acoustics-and-idiom half of the timbre knowledge this project needs: not how to wire a Web Audio graph ([synthesis-recipes.md](synthesis-recipes.md)) or how to escape the generic-synth tell ([original-sound-design.md](original-sound-design.md)), but how real instrument families physically make sound and what a human body's limits do to the phrases they can play. None of this project's voices imitate a specific instrument, but a generated part reads as *played* only if it obeys the physical grammar of some plausible sound-producing body: a bow that must reverse direction, lungs that must refill, a harmonic series that makes some leaps easy and others nearly impossible. For each family below: mechanism, measured spectral/temporal signature, what can change within a note, what limits a phrase, and the idiomatic figures that follow from all of it. [timbre-and-orchestration.md](timbre-and-orchestration.md) covers the perceptual side (why the attack identifies an instrument, register strengths, blend); this page supplies the physical cause underneath it.

## Bowed strings

Sustained tone comes from **stick-slip friction**: rosin grips the string until the string's restoring force overcomes static friction, the string slips back, and the cycle repeats, producing **Helmholtz motion** — a kink that travels a parabolic path between bridge and nut once per period (Fletcher & Rossing 1998) — the physical reason a sawtooth is the default bowed-string starting waveform ([synthesis-recipes.md](synthesis-recipes.md)).

- Bow force, speed, and bridge distance jointly gate Helmholtz motion: **Schelleng's diagram** plots minimum and maximum playable bow force against bow-bridge distance for a given speed — too little force and the string won't speak, too much produces raucous multiple-slip noise. Both bounds fall roughly in inverse proportion to bow-bridge distance, so the playable window **narrows near the bridge** (*sul ponticello*: brighter, glassier, harder to control) and **widens toward the fingerboard** (*sul tasto*: darker, softer, more forgiving) — a brightness control independent of loudness (McLeod thesis; Guettler).
- **Attack noise**: bow onset is not instantaneous — irregular multiple-slipping precedes locked Helmholtz motion, audible as a brief scratch; a fast, weighted start ("martelé") sharpens this into a chirp, a gentle "brush" start nearly suppresses it.
- **Vibrato**: general rates/depths are in [expressive-performance.md](expressive-performance.md); the string-specific mechanism is the stopping finger rocking to modulate string length. Measured violin vibrato: mean rate 5.9 Hz, mean excursion ±15.2 cents, well modeled as a single sinusoid (Mellody & Wakefield 2000) — consistent with that page's general range but mechanically distinct from a wind player's breath-pulse vibrato or a singer's laryngeal one.
- **Double/triple/quadruple stops**: the bridge is curved, so the bow fully contacts at most **one or two strings** at once; three- and four-note chords are physically rolled or arpeggiated (the bow presses briefly through a middle string to catch both neighbors, or the whole chord breaks fast) rather than struck as a true simultaneity.
- **Open-string resonance**: open strings ring with minimal damping and are strong sympathetic resonators — e.g., a stopped D5 (third finger on the A string) rings the open D string (its octave) and the open G string (its 3rd harmonic) sympathetically, an uncontrolled extra overtone real players work around or exploit.
- **Bow-length phrase budget**: a full-size violin bow is roughly 74–75 cm; one stroke direction is a finite tone budget, so a sustained long note needs either a slow bow or a (skilled-players-hide-it) **bow change** — the point where a real line's breaths and micro-breaks naturally fall.
- **Pizzicato**: plucked, not bowed — percussive attack, decay dominated by fast high-harmonic loss (upper partials damp faster than the fundamental), shorter on stopped than open strings; higher string tension trades sustain for brightness.
- Idiomatic figures: cantabile long bows, tremolo (fast unmeasured bow repetition), détaché, spiccato/bounced staccato (true silence between notes, rarer for winds), double-stop drones under a melody, continuous finger-slide glissando.

## Plucked strings

A pluck's **position along the string sets the spectrum**: the initial triangular displacement acts as an FIR comb filter on the harmonic series, notching out harmonics whose node falls at the pluck point (plucking at 1/5 of the length suppresses the 5th harmonic and its multiples) — near the bridge for a bright, nasal attack, mid-string for a fuller fundamental (Euphonics, *On the Synthesis of Guitar Plucks*).

- **Decay rate rises with harmonic number** in every plucked string (guitar, harp, lute alike): high partials damp faster than the fundamental, so the timbre audibly darkens through the note's life; both internal and air damping are frequency-dependent, and decay is generally longer on open/lower strings than fretted/higher ones (JSV plucked-harp-string study).
- **Guitar chord-voicing constraints**: the fretting hand spans roughly four frets comfortably (the "one finger per fret" convention); wider stretches need a thumb-wrap or a **barre** (one finger covering several strings). A voicing is playable only if every sounded string's pitch is reachable within that span — fretted, open, or explicitly muted/skipped — a real constraint for a chord-voicing generator, not just a music-theory one (Fachords; Guitar Wiz).
- **Strum vs. fingerpick**: a strum excites all strings in one fast sweep, so onsets are deliberately staggered by tens of milliseconds string to string (an audible micro-arpeggiation, not a true chord attack); fingerpicking plucks each string individually, giving each note independent timing, dynamics, and pluck position (e.g., alternating-bass "Travis picking").
- **Harp/lute-family notes**: a pedal harp's strings are diatonically pre-tuned; each of 7 pedals shifts *every* string of one pitch class by a semitone across all octaves in a single mechanical action (a rod-and-disc linkage up the column), so harp chromaticism is planned in advance via pedal state, not improvised note-by-note (Harp Column). Lute-family instruments often double strings in unison or octave **courses**, adding a chorus-like shimmer beyond a single string's tone.
- **Sympathetic resonance**: a harp's full string set is acoustically coupled through the soundboard, so plucking one string excites others sharing its harmonics — a "halo of sound" beyond the struck note (*Sympathetic String Modes in the Concert Harp*). Sitar and tanpura carry this further with **jivari** [also romanized *jawari*]: a flattened, curved bridge lets the string touch down progressively along it, generating a bright, harmonically dense buzz a plain bridge does not produce — see [indian-classical-music.md](indian-classical-music.md) for the raga/drone context.

## Flutes and other air-jet instruments

An **edge tone** drives the flute family: the player's air jet strikes a sharp edge (the embouchure hole's far lip) and alternates between the pipe's inside and outside, shedding vortices that couple to and lock onto the air column's resonance (UNSW, *Flute acoustics*).

- The jet's own turbulence adds **broadband breath noise** riding under the periodic tone at all times — an embouchure-controlled tone-to-noise ratio, not an occasional effect, and part of why a flute's low register sounds airier than its brilliant high one ([timbre-and-orchestration.md](timbre-and-orchestration.md) covers the registral-strength side).
- **Overblowing/register**: raising jet velocity and adjusting the jet's offset against the edge — not a new fingering — selects a higher mode of the air column; many low- and middle-register notes share fingerings and differ mainly in air speed and embouchure, so register is substantially a *breath* control, not only a *finger* one.
- **Tonguing/attack types**: single tonguing (a "t"/"d"-consonant stop of the air stream) articulates one note; fast repeated notes use **double** ("t-k-t-k") or **triple** ("t-k-t / t-t-k") tonguing, alternating the tongue's contact point so the air stream is not fully interrupted each time — the same syllabic vocabulary carries over to reeds and, with more resistance, brass.
- **Breath-length phrase budget**: an open embouchure has little resistance, so air escapes relatively fast for the loudness produced, capping phrase length at total lung capacity and blowing effort; **circular breathing** (inhaling through the nose while cheek-stored air keeps the tone going through the mouth) is the trained workaround for phrases longer than one breath, and is easier on reed instruments — whose own resistance helps sustain cheek pressure — than on the open-embouchure flute.
- Shakuhachi carries pitch-bending and breathiness furthest as *idiom*: **meri/kari** (tilting the head to lower/raise pitch) deliberately trades a little pitch accuracy for a large, expressive shift in breathiness and color — see [east-asian-traditions.md](east-asian-traditions.md) for the honkyoku tradition this belongs to.

## Reed instruments

A **single reed** (clarinet, saxophone) is a cane flap beating against a fixed mouthpiece facing, acting as a pressure-controlled valve; a **double reed** (oboe, bassoon) is two cane blades beating against each other, narrower and more resistant on the oboe (~2 mm² rest opening) than the bassoon (~7 mm²). Either way the reed is itself a harmonically rich, semi-independent sound source that the bore then filters — a source-filter split worth treating like a voice's glottal pulse plus vocal tract.

- **Bore shape, not reed type, sets which harmonics survive**: a cylindrical bore closed at the reed end (clarinet) supports only **odd** harmonics and **overblows a twelfth** (jumps to the 3rd harmonic, not the 2nd, when the register key opens); a conical bore (oboe, saxophone, bassoon) supports the **full harmonic series** and overblows an **octave**, giving a fuller, less hollow fundamental-rich spectrum (NIU Clarinet Acoustics; HyperPhysics Oboe).
- **Register break**: the clarinet's chalumeau-to-clarion transition is a real fingering discontinuity, not just a spectral one — a melody generator that assumes smooth stepwise fingerings across that seam is writing something unplayable as notated.
- **Dynamics-brightness coupling** is strong and reed-driven here: higher blowing pressure pushes the reed further into nonlinear closure each cycle, generating more upper harmonics — the general cross-family mechanism below, concretely embodied in the reed itself rather than only in the bore.
- **Altissimo**: past the normal fingered range, bore resonances become weak and irregular, and control of pitch and color passes substantially to the player's own **vocal tract** resonances shaping the reed's behavior — a rare case where "the instrument" extends into the player's body (Bret Pimentel).
- **Breath budget**: the oboe's narrow reed opening restricts airflow so much it has the *longest* natural breath capacity of the winds — players sometimes must exhale unused air at a long phrase's end rather than running out (Orchestration Online).
- Idiomatic figures: legato stepwise lines within a register (leaps across the break are harder), reed-articulated staccato, flutter-tongue and growl as extended techniques, dynamic swells that are inherently also brightness swells.

## Brass

The player's **lips are the vibrating valve** — a fleshy double reed buzzing into a cup or funnel mouthpiece — tuned by lip tension and air flow to lock onto one resonance of the tubing; valves or a slide change the tubing's length and therefore its *entire* harmonic series at once (UNSW, *Brass instrument acoustics*).

- **Harmonic-series slotting**: low partials (2nd, 3rd, 4th) are widely spaced in pitch and easy to aim for; partials crowd closer together going up the series, so high notes are easy to miss onto the wrong partial from a small embouchure/airstream error. A fast, wide leap on one fixed valve combination means retuning the lips across that gap essentially blind — genuinely harder than an equivalent leap on a fingered instrument — which is why idiomatic brass writing favors stepwise or arpeggiated motion within a comfortable partial neighborhood, or a valve/slide change to reach nearby partials of a *different* fundamental instead of leaping far up one series.
- **Brassiness/cuivré at loud dynamics**: loud playing does not just raise level, it **nonlinearly enriches the spectrum** — the pressure wave steepens toward a shock front as it propagates down the bore, generating strong high harmonics beyond what a linear "louder is brighter" rule predicts. This bore-driven nonlinearity, not just the lips, gives brass its "brassy"/cuivré edge at forte, and is a distinct, high-value synthesis cue beyond a simple velocity-to-filter mapping (Norman et al. 2010; Myers et al. 2012).
- **Mutes** insert extra acoustic impedance into or around the bell, reshaping resonance peaks rather than just attenuating: a cup mute behaves roughly like a bandpass around 800–1200 Hz; a Harmon mute adds heavy resistance (audibly straining the player, especially up high) and enables a hand-controlled "wah." All mutes narrow the practical dynamic and pitch range and can shift intonation, especially at the extremes.
- **Endurance**: higher, louder playing demands tighter embouchure tension and faster lip vibration, which fatigues quickly; real brass parts include rests, and a generator that assigns brass nonstop high fortissimo is writing something no embouchure sustains — consistent with the register-strength doctrine in [timbre-and-orchestration.md](timbre-and-orchestration.md).
- Idiomatic figures: fanfare arpeggios (following the harmonic series, which is *why* they are idiomatic), stepwise lines in the upper register, glissando only within one slotted partial's lip-slur range (true continuous glissando needs a slide, i.e., trombone), stopped/muted color changes, hard accents (the attack itself brassens disproportionately).

## Piano and other struck keyboards

A hammer strikes and instantly leaves the string; there is no continued excitation, so everything expressive about a single note is set at the moment of the strike.

- **Hammer nonlinearity = velocity-to-brightness**: felt behaves as a nonlinear spring that stiffens as it compresses, so a harder hit shortens contact time and excites more, higher partials — one measured hammer shows the spectral cutoff rising by roughly 1800 Hz as hammer velocity rises from about 1 to 3.3 m/s (Askenfelt & Jansson; Russell). This single mechanism is *the* velocity-to-timbre law for any struck/mallet voice, not just piano.
- **Inharmonicity and stretched tuning**: real piano strings are stiff rods, not ideal strings, so partials run slightly sharp of exact integer multiples, more so for shorter/stiffer strings (an inharmonicity coefficient *B*, largest in the extreme treble). Tuners compensate with **stretched tuning** — octaves widened beyond a pure 2:1 ratio, most in the treble — so the piano's own sharpened partials line up with its own stretched fundamentals; a piano-like patch tuned to exact 12-TET forgoes this cue.
- **Damper/sustain pedal**: lifts all dampers at once, turning every undamped string into a **sympathetic resonator** for whatever is played — a diffuse, pitched harmonic wash distinct from added reverb, because it is selective (strings harmonically related to the struck note build up strongly, unrelated ones barely stir).
- **Una corda ("soft pedal")**: on a grand, shifts the whole action sideways so hammers strike fewer strings per note (two of three in the treble) *and* strike with a different, less-conditioned part of the felt — softer **and** differently colored, not simply quieter.
- **Repetition-rate limits**: a double-escapement grand action resets without the key fully rising, allowing fast repeated notes; a single-escapement (upright-style) action needs the key to rise almost all the way before it can re-fire — a real mechanical ceiling on how fast one repeated note can plausibly go, tighter for upright-voiced patches than grand-voiced ones (The Piano Deconstructed).
- Idiomatic figures: no sustain or crescendo on a held note (only pedal tricks fake it), so long notes decay from the instant they are struck; broken chords/arpeggiation substitute for a bowed instrument's sustain; wide, register-spanning voicings are easy (ten fingers, two hands) in a way no monophonic voice can match.

## Organs and other sustained keyboards

A pipe organ has **no natural decay envelope**: a flue or reed pipe speaks at a steady state as long as wind is supplied and stops abruptly (bar a short release transient) when the key is released — touch velocity does almost nothing, unlike piano.

- With per-note dynamics unavailable, **registration is the entire expressive vocabulary**: choosing which stops (ranks of pipes) sound together. Mutation stops sound a fixed non-unison harmonic of the played pitch (a Nazard at 2⅔′ sounds the 3rd harmonic, a Tierce at 1⅗′ roughly the 5th, a Larigot at 1⅓′ another upper partial); a mixture stop bundles several such ranks together. Combining unison, mutation, and mixture stops is literally **additive synthesis performed by rank selection** — a registration is a chosen set of harmonic-ratio partials at fixed relative gains (Wikipedia, *Mixture (organ stop)*; Encyclopedia of Organ Stops).
- **Chiff**: a short burst of turbulent noise at the front of a flue pipe's attack, before the wind sheet locks into the steady standing-wave regime; voicers control how much chiff versus smoother "chuff" appears by **nicking** (small notches cut into the languid edge) — the organ's own designed onset transient, and worth stealing directly as a synthesis recipe for any sustained voice that wants a breath-like attack (Bremen thesis; C. B. Fisk, *Pipe Flueways*).
- Idiomatic figures: block chords and full-value sustained notes (nothing decays, so there is little reason to re-strike), terraced dynamics (registration changes step, they do not crossfade smoothly without a swell box), pedal-point drones under manual work, and essentially no leap-difficulty or phrase-length constraint — the one family here that is fundamentally unconstrained beyond finger reach.

## Percussion

Percussion's acoustics — inharmonic membrane and bar/bell modes, the strike-driven attack-pitch glide, noise-plate cymbals and shakers, and the synthesis recipes for each — are covered in full in [percussion-sound-design.md](percussion-sound-design.md) and are not repeated here. The one point worth restating: percussion takes the cross-family attack-primacy principle below to its logical extreme, since for most struck/shaken instruments the identifying information is entirely in the first tens of milliseconds.

## Cross-family acoustic principles

### The attack transient is the physical fingerprint

[timbre-and-orchestration.md](timbre-and-orchestration.md) already establishes that the first tens of milliseconds carry most of an instrument's identity; what this page adds is *why*, mechanism by mechanism. Each family's attack noise is the audible signature of its excitation mechanism still settling into steady state: a bow's irregular multiple-slipping before Helmholtz motion locks in; a jet's turbulence before it locks onto an edge-tone mode; a reed's own beating transient before the bore's resonance takes over; a lip-buzz's onset chirp before a brass bore's standing wave establishes; a hammer or mallet's impact noise; an organ pipe's chiff before the wind sheet locks in. A convincing attack transient is not decoration — it is what a listener hears as *this specific physical process starting up*, and should be synthesized as that process's own noise rather than a generic click.

### Dynamics-brightness coupling is near-universal — and mechanism-specific

Across essentially every acoustic instrument, playing louder measurably brightens the tone (raises the spectral centroid), not just the level — a trumpet at forte has a markedly higher centroid than the same note at piano, and the relationship generalizes across families (Schubert & Wolfe 2006). [original-sound-design.md](original-sound-design.md) already names this as a key realism lever and the cure for a "synthetic" tell; what this page adds is that the underlying *mechanism* differs by family, which should inform how a voice's velocity mapping is shaped rather than treated as one universal curve: bow force/speed adds high-frequency stick-slip content; breath pressure raises jet velocity and turbulence noise; reed pressure drives harder nonlinear reed closure; a brass bore's shock-front steepening is the strongest and most nonlinear case of all (cuivré is closer to a threshold effect than a smooth curve); a piano hammer's stiffening spring shortens contact time. A single generic "cutoff ∝ velocity" curve captures the shape but not this family-specific character.

### Fixed body and room resonance

Every acoustic instrument radiates through a resonant structure whose peaks stay in the **same place regardless of the pitch played**. Violins show a broad "bridge hill" resonance around 2–2.5 kHz from the bridge's in-plane rocking mode plus the body's averaged response (Euphonics, *On the "Bridge Hill" of the Violin*); guitars have a low body/air (Helmholtz) resonance plus a comb of top- and back-plate modes; brass bells radiate more directionally at high frequencies than low; harp and piano soundboards must radiate a whole multi-octave string band through one fixed structure. This is the acoustic grounding for [original-sound-design.md](original-sound-design.md)'s "fixed body/formant resonance" technique (peaking filters at fixed, not pitch-tracking, frequencies): real instruments do exactly this by construction, not as a trick. The room itself adds a second, separate layer of fixed resonance and reverberant tail on top of the instrument's own body — [effects-and-mixing.md](effects-and-mixing.md)'s territory rather than this page's.

## Implications for generative engines

- **Give every synthesized voice an idiom card** — a small data record the composer layer can query before writing a note or chord, independent of how the voice is actually synthesized:
  ```
  range:                   [lowest, highest] playable pitch (scientific pitch notation)
  dynamic-brightness curve: how centroid/cutoff should move with velocity (shape, plus a threshold/"cuivré" knee if the family has one)
  attack family:            onset noise this voice's excitation mechanism would make (bow scratch / breath turbulence / reed beat / lip buzz / impact / chiff / none)
  sustain behavior:         decays-from-onset (piano/pluck/percussion) | player-sustained, needs continued energy (bowed/blown) | steady-state, no natural decay (organ)
  expression capabilities:  what can change *during* a held note (vibrato mechanism, dynamic swell, timbral bend) vs. only at onset
  phrase-length budget:     hard limit (breath, bow length) or none (organ, piano sustain)
  chord capability:         monophonic | double-stop-like | rolled/broken above N notes | full polyphony
  idiomatic figuration:     the family's natural fast-note shapes (arpeggios on brass, alternating bass on plucked strings, tremolo/trill families — see ornamentation-and-embellishment.md)
  forbidden gestures:       what this family's mechanism cannot do (a flute can't play a 4-note chord; a harp can't alter one pitch mid-run without a pedal change; a sustained organ tone can't crescendo on its own)
  ```
- **Enforce phrase-length budgets as hard constraints, not style flavor**: breath- and bow-limited voices need scheduled rests/bow-changes inserted into generated lines at plausible intervals (roughly every few measures at moderate tempo, sooner for loud/high/fast material), the same way [phrase-structure.md](phrase-structure.md) already paces breath in vocal-like lines. A part with no rests for a breath-limited voice reads as sequenced immediately, regardless of how good the synthesis is.
- **Route leap difficulty through the harmonic series for brass-like voices, not raw interval size**: penalize leaps that land on closely spaced high partials of a fixed "fundamental," and prefer stepwise/arpeggiated motion within a comfortable partial neighborhood or a "valve change" (a fundamental shift) to reach a distant target — a physically grounded, cheap alternative to a generic interval-size penalty.
- **Make chord capability a hard per-voice ceiling, not a harmony-engine afterthought**: a bowed-lead voice gets at most a rolled double-stop; a brass or reed voice gets none; a plucked or keyboard voice gets full chords within its own span/hand-count constraint (see [texture-and-density.md](texture-and-density.md) for how this interacts with overall polyphony budgets).
- **Let dynamics reshape timbre per the family's own mechanism, not just level**: a bowed voice's forte should add stick-slip edge, a reed voice's forte should sharpen via reed closure, a brass voice's fortissimo should have a spectral "knee" where cuivré-like enrichment kicks in disproportionately rather than a linear brighter-and-louder ramp. This extends [original-sound-design.md](original-sound-design.md)'s register/velocity-dependent-timbre rule with a specific curve shape per family.
- **Use fixed, non-pitch-tracking body resonance per voice**, tuned once at instrument creation (already prior doctrine in [original-sound-design.md](original-sound-design.md)); this page supplies plausible target frequencies to start from — a bridge-hill-like presence around 2–2.5 kHz for a bowed voice, a low body/air resonance plus a sparse mid comb for a plucked voice, and so on.
- **Sustain-family voices (organ-like) are the one case where none of the phrase/leap/breath constraints above apply** — use that deliberately as the "unconstrained" texture layer (drones, pads), precisely because the absence of constraint is itself the family's idiom.

## Open questions

- Does a listener need the *physically correct* constraint for a given synthesized timbre, or just *some* consistent, audible constraint? A voice that sounds nothing like a real brass instrument but obeys brass-style leap/rest logic might already read as "played" — untested.
- Which of these constraints survive background listening ([attention-and-background-listening.md](attention-and-background-listening.md))? Phrase-length/breath pacing plausibly matters even inattentively (it shapes large-scale rhythm); fine leap-difficulty penalties plausibly do not register consciously at all.
- A quantitative leap-difficulty cost function derived from harmonic-series spacing (brass-like voices) or fret/hand-span geometry (plucked voices) is sketched qualitatively here but not built or calibrated.
- Could idiom cards eventually be *learned* — fit from corpus data on real instrumental parts (interval distributions, rest placement, chord density by instrument) — rather than hand-derived from acoustics, the way [corpus-analysis.md](corpus-analysis.md) already does for melody/harmony statistics?
